## The Concept

Apply the portable data room model to a SINGLE DOCUMENT. A contract is not one blob — it's a collection of paragraphs, clauses, amendments, and signatures. Each unit can be individually signed, versioned, and verified. The document IS a data room where each paragraph is a file.

**Graphs of graphs of graphs.** The same fractal structure that works for files in data rooms works for paragraphs in documents.

---

## The Problem with Contracts Today

### Track Changes Are Not Fit for Purpose

Google Docs track changes and Word's revision history are convenient but unverifiable. Anyone with edit access can accept changes silently. There's no cryptographic proof of WHO changed WHAT and WHEN.

Git for contracts was tried and never took off — because Git doesn't connect changes to verified identities. A git commit says "someone with access to this SSH key made this change." It doesn't say "the CFO of Company X approved clause 7.3."

### The Dangerous Moment Is Not the First Read

The first time someone reads a contract, attention is high. Lawyers review every clause. Everyone is careful. This mirrors the TOFU (Trust On First Use) pattern: the most insecure moment is paradoxically the safest because people are paying attention.

**The danger is in the amendments.** The small changes. The "we updated our terms" email. The clause that shifted between v3 and v4. That's where problems hide — because attention has dropped.

### EULA Absurdity

Most EULAs link to a URL with a clause saying "terms may change at any time." The user signs a document that references a living page the company can modify after signing. This is fundamentally broken. Nobody reads EULAs because even if you did, they can change.

## The Model: Document as Data Room

A document is a ZIP-like structure where every unit is individually addressable and signable:

```
contract-nda-v3.sgdoc
├── manifest.json              ← Document metadata, version, parties
├── structure.json             ← Table of contents, paragraph ordering
├── sections/
│   ├── 1-definitions.md.sig   ← Section 1 + signature(s)
│   ├── 2-obligations.md.sig   ← Section 2 + signature(s)
│   ├── 3-confidentiality.md.sig
│   ├── 4-term.md.sig
│   ├── 5-remedies.md.sig
│   └── 6-general.md.sig
├── amendments/
│   ├── amend-001.json.sig     ← Amendment: who changed what, when, signed
│   └── amend-002.json.sig
├── signatures/
│   ├── party-a.sig            ← Party A's signature over the full document
│   └── party-b.sig            ← Party B's signature over the full document
├── reviews/
│   ├── legal-review-partyA.json.sig  ← Party A's lawyer reviewed sections 1-6
│   └── legal-review-partyB.json.sig  ← Party B's lawyer reviewed sections 1-4
└── history/
    ├── v1.json.sig            ← Original version, signed by author
    ├── v2.json.sig            ← First revision, diff from v1, signed
    └── v3.json.sig            ← Current version, diff from v2, signed
```

### Every Unit Is Signed

```
Section 3: Confidentiality

  Content: "The Receiving Party shall not disclose..."
  
  Signatures:
    - Drafted by: Party A's lawyer (key: sha256:abc...)
      Timestamp: 2026-01-15T10:00:00Z
    
    - Reviewed by: Party B's lawyer (key: sha256:def...)
      Timestamp: 2026-01-17T14:30:00Z
      Note: "Approved without changes"
    
    - Amended by: Party A (key: sha256:ghi...)
      Timestamp: 2026-02-01T09:00:00Z
      Change: "Added 'except as required by law' to disclosure exceptions"
      Signed diff: [cryptographic diff between v2 and v3 of this section]
    
    - Approved by: Party B (key: sha256:jkl...)
      Timestamp: 2026-02-03T16:00:00Z
      Note: "Amendment approved"
```

The full provenance of every clause is verifiable. Who drafted it, who reviewed it, who changed it, who approved the change — all PKI-signed.

## The EULA Revolution

### Third-Party EULA Verification

Privacy organisations, consumer advocacy groups, and legal review services can SIGN individual clauses they've reviewed:

```
EULA for Service X (v2.3, published 2026-02-01)

Clause 1: Data collection
  ✅ Reviewed by: Privacy Foundation (key: sha256:pf001...)
  Rating: "Standard data collection clause. No unusual provisions."

Clause 2: Data sharing with third parties
  ⚠️ Reviewed by: Privacy Foundation (key: sha256:pf001...)
  Rating: "Broader than typical. Allows sharing with 'business partners' 
           without naming them. We recommend caution."

Clause 3: Arbitration
  ✅ Reviewed by: Consumer Legal Aid (key: sha256:cla01...)
  Rating: "Standard binding arbitration. Waives class action rights."

Clause 17: Terms modification
  ❌ Reviewed by: Privacy Foundation (key: sha256:pf001...)
  Rating: "Company reserves the right to modify terms without notice. 
           This makes the entire agreement unreliable."

Summary: 478 of 500 clauses are standard (reviewed by 3 organisations).
         22 clauses are non-standard (flagged by reviewers).
         You need to read 22 clauses, not 500.
```

### Why This Changes Everything

| Today | With Fractal Signing |
|---|---|
| Nobody reads EULAs | Trusted organisations read and sign individual clauses |
| Company can change terms at any time | Changes invalidate signatures — reviewers are notified |
| "I agree to terms at [URL]" | "I agree to terms v2.3, hash sha256:abc..., reviewed by Privacy Foundation" |
| No way to know what changed | Every change is a signed diff, verifiable |
| Privacy orgs have no business model for EULA review | Micropayments per clause review. Funded by user subscriptions or company fees. |

### The Immutable Reference

When a user signs a EULA, they sign a SPECIFIC VERSION (by hash):

```
User signature:
  Document: "Service X EULA"
  Version: v2.3
  Hash: sha256:abc123...
  Signed: 2026-02-15T12:00:00Z
  
  The company CANNOT change the terms without:
  1. Publishing a new version (v2.4)
  2. Invalidating the old signatures
  3. Requiring users to re-sign
  4. Third-party reviewers being notified of changes
```

## Contract Negotiation Workflow

```
Step 1: Party A drafts contract
  Each section signed by Party A's drafter
  Document hash: v1

Step 2: Party B reviews
  Party B's lawyer signs each section they approve
  Party B's lawyer flags sections they want changed
  Flags are signed requests (PKI-signed questions/comments)

Step 3: Party A amends
  Changes are signed diffs (old text → new text, signed by Party A)
  Unchanged sections retain their original signatures
  Document hash: v2

Step 4: Party B reviews amendments
  Only the CHANGED sections need review
  Party B signs the diffs they approve
  Document hash: v3 (if further changes)

Step 5: Both parties sign the final document
  Each party signs the full document hash
  The full document includes all section signatures, all diffs, all reviews
  Complete audit trail is embedded in the document
```

### What Makes This Better Than Git

| Git | Fractal Document Signing |
|---|---|
| Commits linked to SSH keys | Signatures linked to verified PKI identities |
| Diff shows text changes | Diff is signed by the person who made the change |
| History is a linear chain | History includes reviews, approvals, questions — all signed |
| No concept of "approval" | Explicit approval signatures per section |
| Repository-level access control | Section-level access control (some parties see some clauses) |

## Quotation and Provenance

A researcher or journalist can provide PKI-signed paragraphs for citation:

```
Source: Dr. Jane Smith, University of Oxford
Signed content:
  "Our study found a 23% reduction in infection rates when 
   the intervention was applied in the first 48 hours."
  
  Key: sha256:drsmith01...
  Timestamp: 2026-02-10
  Context: Published in Lancet, DOI: 10.1016/...
  
  Licence: "You may quote this paragraph with attribution. 
            Modification is not permitted."
```

The journalist includes this signed block in their article. The reader can:
1. Verify the quote is authentic (signed by Dr. Smith's key)
2. Verify the quote hasn't been modified (hash match)
3. Trace back to the original publication
4. Check if the source has been retracted or updated

An LLM can verify: "Is this article consistent with the signed sources it cites?" — by checking every signed block against the article's claims.

## Economic Model: Micropayments for Verification

| Service | Who Pays | Who Gets Paid |
|---|---|---|
| EULA clause review | Users (subscription) or companies (compliance fee) | Privacy organisations, legal review services |
| Contract clause verification | Parties to the contract | Legal review services, industry standard bodies |
| Source verification for journalism | Publishers | Original researchers, data providers |
| Quote licensing | Publishers, content creators | Original authors |
| Amendment notification | Automatic (platform fee) | Reviewers who signed the original clause |

The economic model creates incentive for:
- Organisations to review and sign content (they get paid)
- Companies to use standard clauses (cheaper — already reviewed)
- Users to demand signed EULAs (they get protection)
- Researchers to sign their findings (they get attribution and micropayments)
