## The Insight

We have the technology to sign content and verify provenance. But technology without enforcement is optional. People ignore optional things.

**Licences are not optional.** Open source licences (GPL, MIT, Apache) and Creative Commons licences (CC BY, CC BY-SA, CC BY-NC) have decades of legal precedent. Companies that violate GPL get sued. Companies that violate Creative Commons get sued. Courts enforce these licences.

What if we add a SIGNED requirement to the licence? Not "include the licence text in a file nobody reads" — but "preserve the cryptographic signature so anyone can VERIFY the provenance chain." Break the signature chain → break the licence → legal liability.

**This is the legal stick that forces players to maintain provenance.**

## The GDPR Analogy: Forcing the Right Behaviour

GDPR didn't ask companies to invest in privacy. It REQUIRED them to, with fines up to 4% of global revenue. Companies that would never have invested in user rights were forced to do so. The result: a massive win for citizens worldwide, not just in the EU.

The same pattern applies here:

| Without Legal Stick | With Legal Stick |
|---|---|
| "Please keep the attribution when you quote me" → ignored | "The licence REQUIRES you to preserve the signature. Violation = licence breach." → enforced |
| "Please verify your sources" → ignored | "The licence REQUIRES verification links. Removing them = copyright violation." → enforced |
| "Please don't modify my words" → ignored | "The signed hash proves modification. Modified without permission = licence breach." → provable |
| Companies strip attribution because there's no consequence | Companies preserve signatures because the legal risk is real |

## CC-Signed: A New Licence Variant

### Concept: Creative Commons + Cryptographic Signature Preservation

Extend existing Creative Commons licences with a signed provenance requirement:

| Existing CC | CC-Signed Extension | What It Adds |
|---|---|---|
| **CC BY** (Attribution) | **CC BY-S** (Attribution + Signed) | You must preserve the cryptographic signature AND the verification link. Removing the signature = removing attribution = licence violation. |
| **CC BY-SA** (Attribution + ShareAlike) | **CC BY-SA-S** | Same as BY-S, plus derivatives must ALSO be signed. Your modifications must be signed with YOUR key, and the chain to the original must be preserved. |
| **CC BY-NC** (Attribution + NonCommercial) | **CC BY-NC-S** | Same as BY-S, and the signature chain makes commercial vs non-commercial use VERIFIABLE — not just claimed. |
| **CC BY-ND** (Attribution + NoDerivatives) | **CC BY-ND-S** | The signature hash proves the content is unmodified. ANY modification breaks the hash → provable licence violation. |

### The "-S" (Signed) Requirement

The signed element adds these licence conditions:

```
1. PRESERVE SIGNATURE: You must preserve the cryptographic signature 
   attached to this work. Removing, altering, or failing to transmit 
   the signature constitutes removal of attribution and is a violation 
   of this licence.

2. PRESERVE VERIFICATION: You must preserve the verification link or 
   mechanism that allows any party to verify the signature against the 
   original author's public key.

3. CHAIN OF PROVENANCE: If you create a derivative work (where permitted 
   by the licence), you must:
   a. Sign your derivative with your own key
   b. Preserve the original signature alongside yours
   c. Maintain the chain: original author → your modification
   
4. VERIFIABLE CLAIMS: If you quote or reference signed content, you must 
   include the signature of the specific section you are referencing, 
   so that any reader can verify the quote is authentic and unmodified.
```

## How It Works in Practice

### Scenario 1: A Researcher Publishes Findings

```
Dr. Smith publishes a research summary:

  Content: "Our study found a 23% reduction in infection rates..."
  Licence: CC BY-S 4.0
  Signed by: Dr. Smith (key: sha256:drsmith01...)
  Verification: verify.sgraph.ai/drsmith01/2026-02-24/study-summary

A journalist quotes the finding:

  Article: "Research from Oxford shows a 23% reduction..."
  Attribution: Dr. Smith, signed (sha256:drsmith01...) [verify]
  
  The journalist MUST include the signature reference.
  The journalist CANNOT remove the [verify] link.
  If they do → CC BY-S licence violation → same legal liability 
  as removing attribution under standard CC BY.

A content farm copies the article and strips the signature:

  Scraped text: "Research shows a 23% reduction..."
  No attribution. No signature. No verification link.
  
  This is PROVABLY a licence violation:
  → The original signed content exists with Dr. Smith's signature
  → The content farm's version has no signature
  → The content is identical (hash comparison)
  → Legal action is clear-cut: they violated CC BY-S
```

### Scenario 2: Code with Signed Provenance

```
Developer publishes a library:

  Code: useful-crypto-lib v2.3
  Licence: MIT-S (MIT + Signed)
  Signed by: Developer (key: sha256:dev01...)
  
  MIT-S adds: "This software includes a cryptographic signature 
  from the original author. You must preserve this signature in 
  all copies and substantial portions of the software."

A company uses the library:

  They MUST include the signature alongside the MIT licence text.
  The signature is machine-verifiable — not just a text file.
  Any audit can verify: this code came from this author, unmodified.
  
  If the code is modified (fork):
  → Fork must be signed by the modifier
  → Original signature preserved alongside
  → Chain: original author → modifier
  → Any user can verify the full provenance
```

### Scenario 3: EULA with Signed Clauses (from the Fractal Document Signing Brief)

```
Privacy Foundation reviews a EULA:

  Clause 7: "We will not sell your data to third parties."
  Reviewed by: Privacy Foundation (key: sha256:pf001...)
  Licence: CC BY-ND-S (the review is attributed, no derivatives, signed)
  
  The company CANNOT:
  → Modify the clause without breaking the Privacy Foundation's signature
  → Claim the Privacy Foundation approved a different version
  → Remove the signature and claim the clause is unreviewed
  
  If the company changes the clause:
  → The hash breaks → the signature no longer verifies
  → Privacy Foundation is automatically notified (signed content was modified)
  → The EULA now shows: "Clause 7: ⚠️ Modified since review. 
     Original reviewed version available at [verify link]"
  → Legal liability: the company modified signed content and 
     continued to imply it was reviewed
```

## The Legal Framework

### What Already Exists

| Framework | Legal Weight | Precedent |
|---|---|---|
| **Creative Commons** | Enforceable licence. Decades of case law. Recognised globally. | Multiple successful lawsuits for CC licence violations |
| **Open Source Licences** (GPL, MIT, Apache) | Enforceable licence. Extensive case law. | GPL Violations.org, Jacobsen v. Katzer (2008) — open source licence conditions are enforceable |
| **Copyright law** | Automatic protection. No registration needed in most jurisdictions. | The foundation on which all licences sit |
| **Digital signatures** | Legal recognition in EU (eIDAS), US (ESIGN Act, UETA), and most jurisdictions | Digital signatures have legal standing equivalent to handwritten signatures |
| **Moral rights** | In many jurisdictions, authors have inalienable rights to attribution and integrity | Removing attribution violates moral rights regardless of licence |

### What's New with CC-Signed

The signed element adds:

| New Element | Legal Basis |
|---|---|
| **Signature as attribution** | The cryptographic signature IS the attribution. Removing it = removing attribution = licence violation under existing CC law. |
| **Verification as a licence condition** | Preserving the verification mechanism is a licence condition. Same legal standing as "include the licence text" in MIT licence. |
| **Hash as proof of integrity** | The signed hash is PROOF that content was or wasn't modified. This makes "NoDerivatives" machine-verifiable, not just a human judgment. |
| **Chain of provenance as a licence condition** | Derivative works must maintain the signature chain. This is a licence condition with the same legal standing as "ShareAlike" in CC BY-SA. |

## Forcing Players to Play

The GDPR pattern: use legal requirements to force behaviour that wouldn't happen voluntarily.

### Who Gets Forced to Do the Right Thing

| Player | What They're Forced to Do | Why They Wouldn't Otherwise |
|---|---|---|
| **Content aggregators** | Preserve attribution signatures when republishing | They strip attribution to appear original |
| **News outlets** | Maintain source signature chains when citing research | They paraphrase sources without linking back |
| **SaaS companies** | Preserve signed EULAs when modifying terms | They modify terms and claim continuity |
| **AI training pipelines** | Preserve provenance when ingesting training data | They strip metadata to reduce storage |
| **Supply chain platforms** | Maintain signed provenance when routing data | They flatten data to simplify processing |
| **Code forkers** | Preserve author signatures when modifying code | They strip git history and claim authorship |
| **LLM applications** | Preserve source signatures when generating responses | They present sourced content as original generation |

### The Economic Incentive Chain

```
Legal requirement to preserve signatures
    → Signature infrastructure becomes necessary (not optional)
    → Demand for PKI tools, verification services, signing tools
    → Our platform and ecosystem grows (DRU/MRU/ARU increase)
    → More signed content in the ecosystem
    → More value in the trust web
    → More demand for content trust infrastructure
    → Virtuous cycle
```

**The licence creates the legal REQUIREMENT for the infrastructure we provide.** Just as GDPR created demand for privacy tools, CC-Signed creates demand for signing and verification tools.

## The Vision: A Signed Web

```
Today:
  Content is published → copied → modified → stripped → reposted
  Attribution is lost within 2-3 hops
  Original creators get no credit, no revenue, no verification
  
With CC-Signed:
  Content is published (signed) → copied (signature preserved — legal requirement)
  → quoted (signature reference preserved — legal requirement)
  → verified by any reader at any time
  → original creator is always reachable, always credited, always verifiable
  → breaking the chain = breaking the licence = legal liability
```

This isn't a technology change. It's a LEGAL change that makes the technology mandatory. The technology already exists (PKI signatures, verification, provenance chains). The licence creates the obligation to USE it.
