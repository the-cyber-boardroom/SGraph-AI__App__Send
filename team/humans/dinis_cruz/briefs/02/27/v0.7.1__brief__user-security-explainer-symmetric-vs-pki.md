## The Honest Answer

When you send a file via SG/Send, the answer to "who can read it?" depends on **which mode you used**. The difference is enormous. This document explains both, honestly, and tells you which to use when.

---

## Mode 1: Standard Send (Symmetric Key)

*This is the default mode. It's what happens when you send a link that looks like:*  
`https://send.sgraph.ai/send/...#abc123/xK9mP2qR...`

**The short version:** Your file is encrypted. The server cannot read it. But the decryption key travels with the link, so whoever handles the link — including your email provider — could in principle read your file.

### Who Can Read It

Here is the complete, honest list:

| Who | Can they read it? | When | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **You** (the sender) | Yes | While creating it | You created it |
| **The AI session** (if Claude created it) | Yes | During creation | The AI sees content it generates |
| **AI provider logs** (Anthropic) | Possibly | Depends on their retention policy | Standard API usage terms apply |
| **Your send workflow** (n8n, if used) | Yes (key in URL) | In transit | Protected by TLS; configure to not log full bodies |
| **Email in transit** | No | In transit | Encrypted with TLS — eavesdroppers on the network cannot read it |
| **Your email provider** (Gmail, Outlook, etc.) | Yes | After delivery | Email providers can read your email |
| **Anyone with access to the recipient's email** | Yes | After delivery | Shared accounts, compromised devices |
| **The recipient** | Yes | On clicking the link | This is the intended outcome |
| **SG/Send server** | **No** | Always | Zero-knowledge — server stores only encrypted bytes it cannot read |
| **Anyone who intercepts the link after first use** | No | After first download | Link is invalidated after first download |

### The Honest Comparison

This sounds like a long list. Let's compare it with the alternatives:

| How you could send the file | Who can read it |
|---|---|
| **Email attachment (no encryption)** | Every email provider in the chain, every mail server, anyone who intercepts it, NSA, your IT department |
| **WeTransfer, Dropbox link** | WeTransfer/Dropbox (they hold the keys), plus everyone in the email chain for the link |
| **SG/Send standard (this mode)** | AI session, AI provider logs, n8n (if used), your email provider, recipient's email access — **but not the server, not network interceptors, not anyone after first use** |
| **SG/Send PKI mode** | AI session (for content it generates), AI provider logs — **nobody else** |

Standard send is not perfect. It is substantially better than the alternatives most people use today. The server never sees your content — that part is unconditional.

### When Standard Send Is Fine

- Sending documents you'd be comfortable emailing, but want to add encryption-at-rest
- Sharing files where the main concern is the server/hosting provider (not the email chain)
- Quick shares where the recipient doesn't have a public key set up
- Content that isn't highly sensitive

### What Protects You in Standard Mode

Even though the link contains the key, you're protected by:

1. **One-time use** — the link stops working after the first download. Even if your email is later compromised, the link is dead.
2. **Short expiry** — links expire within 24 hours by default. Stale links in logs or archived emails don't work.
3. **TLS everywhere** — the key never travels unencrypted. Network interception is not a meaningful risk.
4. **Zero server knowledge** — SG/Send cannot be compelled to produce your file. They don't have it. They have bytes they cannot read.

## Mode 2: PKI Send (Public Key Encryption)

*Available when the recipient has shared their public key. The link looks like:*  
`https://send.sgraph.ai/d/b12/document-name`

**The short version:** Your file is encrypted with the recipient's public key. **Only the holder of their private key can decrypt it** — nobody else, no matter what.

### Who Can Read It

| Who | Can they read it? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **You** (the sender) | Yes | While creating/sending |
| **The AI session** (if Claude created it) | Yes | The AI sees content it generates |
| **AI provider logs** | Possibly (plaintext content, not the key) | Same caveat as symmetric mode |
| **Email provider** | **No** | The email contains only a reference URL, not a key |
| **SG/Send server** | **No** | Zero-knowledge preserved |
| **Anyone who intercepts the link** | **No** | The link is a pointer, not a key |
| **The recipient** | Yes | Using their private key, in their browser |
| **Anyone else** | **No** | Without the private key, the encrypted blob is computationally indecipherable |

### What This Adds Over Standard Mode

| Property | Standard | PKI |
|---|---|---|
| **Server can't read it** | ✅ | ✅ |
| **Email provider can't read it** | ❌ | ✅ |
| **Link interception is harmless** | ❌ (link = key) | ✅ (link ≠ key) |
| **Link can be permanent** | ❌ (must expire) | ✅ (link is just a reference) |
| **Recipient can verify sender** | ❌ | ✅ (with signing) |
| **Non-repudiation** | ❌ | ✅ (with signing) |

### The Private Key: Your Identity

In PKI mode, the private key is the thing that makes you, you. It is generated in your browser, stored in your browser, and never transmitted anywhere. SG/Send never sees it. Your email provider never sees it. Nobody sees it except you.

This means:
- **If you lose your private key, you cannot recover encrypted files** sent to that key. Back it up.
- **If someone gets your private key, they can decrypt everything** sent to it. Protect it.
- **Your private key is your identity.** With it, others can verify you sent something. Without it, impersonation is impossible.

### When PKI Mode Is Required

- Sending personal data (ID documents, health information, financial records)
- Sending legal or contractual documents where authorship must be verifiable
- Sending to someone where you cannot fully trust the email chain
- Sending anything that would be categorised HIGH or CRITICAL sensitivity
- Any time you want to prove you sent something (and not someone else)

## The Nudge: How to Move to PKI

PKI sounds technical. The actual steps are simple:

### For the Recipient (5 minutes, once)
1. Go to `send.sgraph.ai`
2. Click "Generate My Key Pair" (or use the SSH/PGP keygen tools)
3. Your key pair is generated in your browser — nothing sent to any server
4. Share your **public key** with people who might send you files

### For the Sender
1. Enter the recipient's public key (or their GitHub username — their keys are published there)
2. Send as normal — the file is automatically encrypted for their key
3. The recipient clicks the link and it decrypts automatically in their browser

No software to install. No account to create. The public key can be posted publicly — it's not secret. Only the private key (held by the recipient) is secret.

## The Comparison at a Glance

Think of it like physical mail:

| Analogy | SG/Send Mode | Who holds the key |
|---|---|---|
| A letter in a sealed envelope (standard post) | Standard send | Key is with the letter — postman could open it |
| A letter in a padlocked box, only recipient has the key | PKI send | Key never leaves recipient — postman cannot open it |
| A signed, padlocked letter — sender's signature verified | PKI + signing | Full: encrypted AND authenticated |

## Frequently Asked Questions

**"Is my data encrypted in both modes?"**  
Yes, always. AES-256-GCM. The server cannot read your files in either mode. The difference is who else can read it by having the key.

**"Is TLS encryption the same as end-to-end encryption?"**  
No. TLS protects data in transit — between your browser and the server, or between servers. It means network interceptors can't read it. But the parties at each end (Gmail, n8n, the server) can still read unencrypted content. End-to-end encryption means only the sender and recipient hold keys — which is what PKI mode provides.

**"If the email was encrypted in transit (TLS), doesn't that mean it's private?"**  
Transit encryption protects the data while it's moving. Once it arrives at Gmail, Gmail stores it in a form they can access. TLS prevents eavesdropping on the wire; it doesn't prevent the mail provider from reading your mail.

**"The standard mode entity list is long — should I be worried?"**  
For most use cases: no. Assess based on what you're sending. Sending a project proposal? Standard mode is fine — it's better than email. Sending a passport scan? Use PKI or out-of-band key delivery.

**"What does 'one-time use' actually protect me from?"**  
It means the decryption key (which is in the email) becomes worthless after the recipient downloads the file. Even if someone reads your email six months later, the link is dead and the file is gone. This converts a persistent risk into a narrow time window.
