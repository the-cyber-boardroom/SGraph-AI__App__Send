## The Problem

Most careers are opaque. The path to becoming a surgeon, a CISO, a successful entrepreneur, a barrister, a senior engineer — it looks clear from the outside and is almost entirely invisible from the inside.

This opacity is not evenly distributed. For people with access to the right networks, the right schools, the right family connections, informal guidance fills the gap. Someone tells them: "Do this internship. Get this qualification. Talk to this person." The path is still invisible, but they have a guide.

For everyone else — people from lower-income backgrounds, first-generation professionals, people whose communities have no history in a particular field — the path is not just opaque. It looks actively inaccessible. Not because it is, but because there is no map.

This is the social mobility problem. And it is solvable.

## The Forest Analogy

There is a phenomenon in ecology that explains part of the problem. When you stand in a mature forest, you cannot reverse-engineer how it got there. Every stage of the forest's development removed the conditions that made the previous stage possible. The pioneer species that allowed the first grasses to take hold were killed by the shade of the grasses. The grasses that allowed the first shrubs were killed by the shade of the shrubs. The shrubs that allowed the first trees were killed by the shade of the trees.

The forest destroyed its own history by growing.

Careers do the same thing. The senior executive who earned their position over 25 years has — usually without intending to — destroyed the evidence of how they got there. The uncertain period in their early 30s when they nearly quit. The mentor who believed in them when they didn't believe in themselves. The apparently irrelevant job that gave them exactly the skill they needed six years later. The mistake they made that taught them more than any success.

None of that appears on a LinkedIn profile. None of it is in the official biography. By the time they are visible and successful, the path that got them there has been overgrown.

This is not dishonesty. It is the natural compression of narrative. Success stories are told forward: "I always wanted to be X, I worked hard, I achieved X." The actual story — recursive, uncertain, full of apparent dead-ends that turned out to be essential — is almost never told.

## Trespasser Syndrome

Imposter syndrome is well-documented: the feeling, among high-achieving people, that they do not deserve their success and will eventually be found out.

But there is a related and perhaps more damaging phenomenon that affects people *before* they reach success: **trespasser syndrome**. The sense that a particular career, field, or professional community is not meant for people like them. That they would be trespassing by even trying.

The evidence consistently contradicts this feeling. Studies of career patterns repeatedly show that people from non-traditional backgrounds, people who entered a field through unconventional routes, people who have faced and overcome significant challenges — these people frequently outperform their more conventionally-pathed peers. The resilience, the perspective, the ability to see the system from outside because they were never assumed to be part of it — these are advantages, not deficits.

But you can only believe this if you have seen it. If you have never seen someone who looks like you, who started where you started, who had the challenges you had — succeed in a field — then the abstract argument that "you could do it" is not enough. You need the reference. You need the proof of possibility.

This is why representation matters beyond symbolism. It is not about making people feel included. It is about giving people the evidence they need to believe that trying is worth it. When one person breaks through a ceiling, they do not just create an opportunity — they create a generation of people who now believe the ceiling can be broken.

## The Last-Mile Credit Problem

There is a structural problem in how success is attributed and rewarded.

The people who receive the most visible credit, the most financial reward, and the most public recognition for an organisation's success are almost always the people who were last in the chain — the executives, the founders, the "faces." The people who actually did the core work — the teams, the individual contributors, the people who solved the hard problems — receive a fraction of the credit and a fraction of the reward.

This is not just unfair. It is also a distortion of the information available to people choosing careers. If the only visible success stories are executives and founders, then the map of "how to succeed" that young people build from observation is wrong. It says: get to the top. Be the face. The rest is invisible.

A true map of how value is created in organisations would look completely different. It would show the engineers whose architectural decisions determined everything that came after. The researchers whose findings became the foundation for ten years of product development. The teachers and managers who developed the people who did the visible work. The people whose expertise was essential but whose names appear in no press release.

## The Proposal: Wikipedia for Careers

The idea is to create a structured, open, community-built body of knowledge that answers a simple question for any career:

**What does it actually take to succeed in this field, and what paths have people actually taken to get there?**

Not the sanitised version. Not the LinkedIn version. The real version, with the uncertainty and the false starts and the pivots and the overlooked work and the moments of doubt and the things that actually mattered.

### What It Contains

For each career area, the Wikipedia for Careers would document:

**The Common Paths** — not just the one famous path, but the full distribution. What percentage of successful people in this field came through a traditional university route? What percentage came through vocational training? What percentage changed careers mid-life? What percentage came from unrelated fields and found that their "irrelevant" background was actually a superpower?

**The Hidden Accelerators** — what actually made the difference? Not the official credentials, but the experiences, relationships, challenges, and capabilities that people consistently identify as having been most important. Often these are unexpected: a specific type of work experience, a personal challenge overcome, a skill developed in a completely different context.

**The False Signals** — what do people think matters that actually doesn't? What credentials are chased because they look impressive but have little correlation with actual success? What advice is given repeatedly and follows that is consistently wrong?

**The Reference Stories** — interviews with people who have succeeded, deliberately including people from non-traditional backgrounds, documenting not the polished success story but the actual path. Including the uncertain periods. Including the pivots. Including the mistakes. Including the things they would do differently.

**The Cross-Career Connections** — which skills transfer? Which career backgrounds produce unusually strong performers in other fields? Where are the unexpected on-ramps and cross-over points?

### Why Open Source and Creative Commons

This project only works if the information is genuinely shared. It cannot be proprietary. It cannot be behind a paywall. It cannot be owned by a company that has financial interests in steering people toward particular paths.

The data, the interviews, the analysis — everything must be released under Creative Commons. Not because of ideology, but because:

1. **People will not share honestly with an organisation that might monetise their honesty.** The candid interview — where someone describes their early failures, their doubts, their unconventional path — requires trust that the information will be used for the public good, not for profit.

2. **The value compounds with openness.** A database of career paths that anyone can build on, analyse, cross-reference, and extend is worth infinitely more than the same database locked behind access controls.

3. **Social mobility is a collective problem that requires collective solutions.** Proprietary approaches to this problem have existed for decades and have not solved it. The only model that works at the scale needed is open.

## The Cybersecurity Example

Cybersecurity is a particularly compelling demonstration case for this project.

The field has a profound diversity problem — not just in gender and ethnicity, but in background, in educational path, in professional history. The dominant narrative of "how to get into cybersecurity" is narrow and often inaccurate. It focuses on certifications, on specific technical skills, on a particular educational pipeline.

The reality is different. Some of the most effective security professionals came from law, from psychology, from journalism, from teaching, from customer service. The skills that make a great security professional — systemic thinking, the ability to model adversarial behaviour, comfort with ambiguity, the ability to explain complex risk in plain language, attention to detail — are not exclusive to people who built their first firewall at age 14.

At the same time, cybersecurity professionals who go deep in their field develop capabilities that transfer unusually well to other domains: the habit of questioning assumptions, the ability to operate at multiple levels of abstraction simultaneously, comfort with uncertainty, the discipline of thinking about second and third-order consequences.

A Wikipedia for Careers applied to cybersecurity could do something specific: show that the people currently outside the field who think they are not qualified are often wrong. And show that the people currently in the field have capabilities they are not fully using because no one has mapped where those capabilities are valuable.

## The Invitation

This concept is looking for people who want to help build it. The technical infrastructure to scale this work exists or is being built. What it needs is the interviews, the research, the domain knowledge, and the willingness to do the patient, unfashionable work of building a body of knowledge that outlasts any individual contribution.

If you have a career you understand deeply — or a career you are navigating — and you are interested in contributing to a structured, open, Creative Commons project to map it honestly, this is the invitation.
