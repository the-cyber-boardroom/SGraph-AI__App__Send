# SG/Send Homepage UX Brief: The Challenge Campaign Flow

**Document:** `team/briefs/03/01/v0.1.0__sg-send__homepage-ux-brief__challenge-flow.md`
**Date:** 2026-03-01
**Status:** Draft
**Triggered by:** Pre-launch review of the SG/Send Challenge outreach campaign
**Scope:** The sgraph.ai homepage and the two primary conversion paths (Buy Credits → Stripe, Join Early Access → signup form)

---

## Context

We are about to launch the SG/Send Challenge — a personal outreach campaign where friends and contacts are asked to complete the full user journey: arrive at sgraph.ai, find the buy page, purchase £5 of credits, send an encrypted file, and get someone to open it. This campaign will be our first structured test of the end-to-end funnel with real users who have been primed with a specific intent ("go do this thing").

Before sending a single message, we reviewed the current homepage and conversion paths. What we found is a set of structural UX problems that will cause the Challenge to fail — not because the product is bad, but because the paths from intent to action are broken. Users who arrive wanting to buy credits or join Early Access will struggle to do so, not because the options don't exist, but because the site's information architecture buries them behind unnecessary intermediate pages, philosophy sections, and redundant scrolling.

This brief documents the problems from four role perspectives: the Advocate (who speaks for the user), the Sherpa (who walks the actual paths), the Ambassador (who owns the campaign's success), and the Designer (who evaluates how the site communicates through its structure). Each role sees the same evidence but through a different lens, and together they converge on a unified diagnosis and set of principles that should guide the fix.

## Guiding Principles

Before diving into role-specific analysis, we want to name the principles that the current design violates. These are not abstract design theory — they are directly observable in the paths above, and they should guide every homepage decision going forward.

### Principle 1: Every click must earn its existence

A click that does not bring the user closer to their goal, or that takes them to a page whose only purpose is to redirect them again, is a design failure. Intermediate pages that exist solely to provide context the user didn't ask for are obstacles, not waypoints.

**Violated by:** `/pricing/free/` (exists only to show philosophy before the actual signup form), `/pricing/cloud/` (exists only to explain "What's Included" before the actual buy button), the homepage "Get Started" section whose buttons go to these intermediate pages instead of directly to Stripe or the signup form.

### Principle 2: The primary action should be the most reachable thing on the page

If the business's most important conversion action requires more effort to reach than secondary information, the hierarchy is inverted. The thing the user most needs to do should be the thing that requires the least effort to find and execute.

**Violated by:** "Buy Credits" being visually secondary to "Get Started Free" on the homepage (outline button vs filled button, no "Recommended" label). The homepage actively steers users toward the longer, lower-priority path.

### Principle 3: Don't make me scroll past why to get to how

Users who arrive with intent — especially those sent by a friend with a specific ask — do not need to be convinced before they can act. Philosophy, architecture explanations, and deployment options are valuable content for browsers, but they must never stand between an intentful user and their goal.

**Violated by:** The pricing philosophy section ("Why is SG/Send free?", "Our Philosophy" cards) appearing above the actual pricing options on `/pricing/free/`. The "What's Included" and "How Credits Work" sections appearing above the buy button on `/pricing/cloud/`.

### Principle 4: Never break the trust chain

For a zero-knowledge encryption product, every pixel communicates trust. When a user completes a sensitive action (entering their email to sign up) and is suddenly ejected to a third-party domain with different branding, different colours, and their email visible in the URL — the trust chain snaps. The product spent every prior page building confidence in "your files, your keys, your privacy" and then handed the user to a bright green LaunchList page that felt like a completely different product.

**Violated by:** The Early Access signup redirecting to `getlaunchlist.com` with the user's email in the URL bar, zero SG/Send branding, and a referral mechanic they didn't ask for.

### Principle 5: Form follows function, not org chart

The site's current structure maps to the builder's mental model (free options, cloud options, philosophy, architecture, team), not to the user's mental model (send a file, buy credits, learn more). When the information architecture reflects internal categories rather than user tasks, users must translate between their intent and the site's structure at every step.

**Violated by:** The pricing page presenting 8 options across two sections (4 free tiers, 4 cloud tiers) when 90%+ of users need exactly one of two things: "join free" or "buy £5 of credits." The nav structure (Product / Team / Architecture / Pricing) reflecting internal roles rather than user tasks.

### Principle 6: The site should teach by doing, not by explaining

For a product whose core value is experiential (the encryption happens in your browser — you can see it), the most persuasive thing the site can do is get the user into the product as fast as possible. Every section of explanation before the user has tried the product is explanation they'll skim or skip. Every section after they've tried it is explanation they'll absorb.

**Violated by:** The homepage structure placing three explanation sections (Zero-Knowledge Encryption, feature cards, Built Entirely by AI Agents) between the hero and the "Get Started" cards — which themselves don't link directly to the actions anyway.

## Role Perspectives

### Advocate's Review: "Who is this homepage for?"

**Filing as:** Formal advocacy review
**Severity:** Objection — the current homepage actively harms users arriving with purchase intent

The homepage currently serves three personas simultaneously and serves none of them well:

**The Browser** — someone who found sgraph.ai through search or a link and wants to understand what it is. The homepage does a reasonable job for this persona in the hero section: clear headline, clear subtext, clear value proposition. But it then loses them in the mid-page by presenting too much undifferentiated information (encryption details, team stats, deployment options) without a clear reading path.

**The Intentful Buyer** — someone sent by the Challenge campaign with a specific instruction: "go to sgraph.ai, find the buy page, buy £5 of credits." This persona arrives with money ready and is actively prevented from spending it. The "Buy Credits" action is visually subordinate to "Get Started Free" and requires 3+ clicks to reach Stripe. This is the persona the Challenge campaign creates, and the homepage is hostile to them.

**The Technical Evaluator** — someone who wants to inspect the architecture, check the repo, verify the encryption claims. The homepage gives them partial information (feature cards, GitHub link) but buries the deep content behind nav items they'll find eventually.

The fundamental advocacy objection is this: **the homepage's visual hierarchy and information architecture prioritise the least commercially important action (free signup) over the most commercially important action (paid credits), and then make both actions harder to complete than they need to be.**

For the Challenge campaign specifically, every Challenge participant is an Intentful Buyer persona. They will arrive at sgraph.ai having been told "find the buy page and buy £5 of credits." The current site makes this a puzzle. That is not a fun challenge — it is friction that will cause abandonment and generate support messages to you (Dinis) instead of completions.

**Recommendation:** The homepage must have a direct path to Stripe and a direct path to the signup form, both reachable in one click from above the fold. The "Buy Credits" path should be the primary visual action for the Challenge campaign period.

### Sherpa's Review: "I walked both paths. Here is the friction map."

**Filing as:** Friction log and trail observation
**Severity:** Critical — multiple friction points will cause abandonment in the Challenge flow

I walked both paths end to end, the way a Challenge participant would. Here is every friction point, structured as data:

**Friction Point 1: Hero CTA hierarchy is inverted**
- **Stage:** Homepage, first interaction
- **Expected:** "Buy Credits" is the obvious primary action (the Challenge message says "buy £5 of credits")
- **Actual:** "Get Started Free" is visually primary (filled green button, left position). "See Pricing" is secondary (outline button, right position). There is no "Buy Credits" button visible.
- **Impact:** User who was told to "buy credits" must make a judgement call between two CTAs, neither of which says "Buy Credits." Most will click "See Pricing" and enter the long funnel. Some will click "Get Started Free" and enter the wrong funnel entirely.
- **Predicted trail:** Split traffic, with the "Get Started Free" cohort hitting a dead end (they wanted to buy, not sign up for free) and bouncing back.

**Friction Point 2: Pricing page opens with philosophy, not prices**
- **Stage:** `/pricing/` or `/pricing/free/`, first scroll
- **Expected:** A pricing page shows prices.
- **Actual:** The page opens with "Why is SG/Send free?" and four philosophy cards (Open Source, More Deployments = More Security, We Monetise the Service Not the Software, Transparency Builds Trust). The actual pricing options require scrolling past this content.
- **Impact:** User who arrived wanting to see a price and a buy button is presented with a manifesto first. They must assess whether this content is relevant to their goal, decide to scroll past it, and then find the actual pricing.
- **Predicted trail:** Time-on-page increases but not in a good way. Users scanning for a price will scroll quickly, potentially overshooting the pricing cards.

**Friction Point 3: Eight pricing cards create decision paralysis**
- **Stage:** `/pricing/`, pricing grid
- **Expected:** One or two clear options matching the user's intent.
- **Actual:** Eight cards across two sections — four free tiers (Early Access, Open Source Self-Hosted, Own Cloud Account, White Label) and four paid tiers (Multi-Tenant, Single-Tenant, AWS Marketplace, Managed). The Challenge user needs exactly one: Multi-Tenant at £5. But it is one of eight visually similar cards, and only one is tagged "Available Now."
- **Impact:** Decision paralysis. The user must read and dismiss seven irrelevant options to find the one they need.
- **Predicted trail:** Increased scroll depth, card-hover patterns suggesting scanning behaviour, and potential abandonment from users who feel the product is "too enterprise" or "not for me."

**Friction Point 4: `/pricing/cloud/` is a detour page**
- **Stage:** After clicking "Buy Credits" on the pricing grid
- **Expected:** Stripe checkout.
- **Actual:** A detail page with "What's Included" cards and "How Credits Work" cards. The actual buy button is below the fold.
- **Impact:** User has already committed to buying. This page re-explains what they've already decided. It introduces doubt ("wait, should I read all this first?") and costs another scroll + click.
- **Predicted trail:** Fast scroll to bottom, indicating the content was not needed. High exit rate from this page would confirm it's a barrier, not an aid.

**Friction Point 5: Early Access confirmation destroys trust context**
- **Stage:** After submitting the Early Access form
- **Expected:** A confirmation within the SG/Send experience — same dark theme, same branding, same feeling of security.
- **Actual:** Redirect to `getlaunchlist.com/s/zSa8gl/[email]` — bright green background, LaunchList logo, referral mechanics, user's email in the URL. Visually and contextually, a completely different product.
- **Impact:** For a zero-knowledge encryption product that just spent every page building trust with "we can't read your files," being ejected to a third-party domain with your email visible in the URL is a jarring trust violation. The user just demonstrated trust by entering their details. The product responded by handing them to a stranger.
- **Predicted trail:** This is a terminal page — users won't come back from here to continue exploring. The "Back to SGraph Send" button exists but leads to send.sgraph.ai, not sgraph.ai.

**Friction Point 6: Homepage "Get Started" section is a false summit**
- **Stage:** Homepage, scrolled to mid-page
- **Expected:** The "Get Started" section with "Join Early Access" and "Buy Credits" buttons would go directly to the signup form and Stripe respectively.
- **Actual:** The buttons go to the intermediate pages (`/pricing/free/` and `/pricing/cloud/`), adding another layer of indirection.
- **Impact:** Users who scrolled past the hero and found this section — which looks like exactly what they need — are sent back into the same broken funnels. The section promises resolution but delivers more navigation.
- **Predicted trail:** Return visits to the homepage after hitting the intermediate pages, indicating the user expected a shorter path.

### Ambassador's Review: "This funnel will kill the Challenge campaign's conversion rate."

**Filing as:** Campaign risk assessment
**Severity:** Campaign-blocking — the SG/Send Challenge should not launch until the primary conversion path is fixed

The Challenge campaign is designed to test the full user journey: discovery → payment → usage → sharing. The campaign's value comes from observing where real people get stuck. But there is a difference between "getting stuck on the product" (valuable friction — tells us about the encryption UX) and "getting stuck on the website" (wasted friction — tells us nothing we don't already know about bad funnels).

Right now, the sgraph.ai homepage will generate the second kind of friction almost exclusively. Challenge participants will spend their effort navigating the site's information architecture, not evaluating the product. The feedback we'll receive will be "I couldn't find where to buy credits" and "I gave up on the pricing page" — not "the encryption flow was confusing" or "I didn't understand how to share the key."

**Specific campaign risks:**

**Risk 1: The Challenge message says "go to sgraph.ai and find the buy page" — but the buy page doesn't exist.** There is no single page a user can land on that has a buy button above the fold. The closest thing is `/pricing/cloud/`, but even that requires scrolling to the bottom. The Challenge's own framing ("find the buy page") implies a destination that the site doesn't provide.

**Risk 2: The viral loop breaks at the first link.** The Ambassador's primary growth lever is the viral loop: recipient receives encrypted file → opens it → understands the product → sends their own file. For the Challenge, the loop is: participant receives WhatsApp message → goes to sgraph.ai → buys credits → sends file → recipient opens it → participant confirms via SG/Send. Every unnecessary click between "goes to sgraph.ai" and "buys credits" reduces completion rate. With 3 clicks and 3 scrolls, we should expect significant drop-off — industry data suggests ~20% abandonment per additional step in a purchase flow.

**Risk 3: The £5 Amazon voucher ROI depends on completions, not clicks.** We are spending £5 per Challenge participant who completes the full flow. If participants abandon at the pricing page because they can't find the buy button, we get zero product feedback and the campaign cost was wasted on the outreach effort itself.

**Risk 4: Feedback will be contaminated.** When we ask "where did you get stuck?", we want answers about the encryption product. If the website navigation is the primary friction source, all our feedback will be about the website instead of the product. We will have spent the social capital of asking friends for a favour and learned nothing about the actual product experience.

**Recommendation:** Fix the homepage conversion paths before launching the Challenge. The minimum viable fix is: one click from the homepage hero to Stripe, one click to the signup form. Everything else can wait — but these two paths cannot be broken when we start sending WhatsApp messages.

### Designer's Review: "The site's form contradicts its function."

**Filing as:** Design review
**Severity:** Structural — the information architecture communicates the wrong hierarchy

Evaluating sgraph.ai through the design tradition's core principles, the homepage has strong elements and fundamental structural problems. I'll address both.

**What works well:**

The visual design language is strong. The dark theme with teal accents communicates security and technical sophistication. The typography hierarchy is clear within individual sections. The hero headline ("Send files. Nobody can read them. Not even us.") is excellent — it communicates the core value proposition in natural language without jargon. The Stripe checkout page is well-branded and trustworthy. The feature cards are cleanly laid out. The "Built Entirely by AI Agents" section with the stat counters is distinctive and memorable.

**What is structurally broken:**

**Hierarchy violation — the site teaches before it enables.** The homepage follows a classic "explain then convert" pattern: hero → explanation → explanation → explanation → conversion. This pattern assumes the user arrived without intent and needs to be educated before acting. For users arriving from the Challenge campaign (or any referral with context), this structure forces them to scroll through content they don't need to reach actions they're ready to take. The information architecture should be: hero → action → explanation (for those who want it).

**Affordance failure — the CTAs don't communicate their destinations.** "Get Started Free →" affords "I will begin using the product for free." It actually means "I will be taken to a philosophy page, then to a signup form, then to a third-party waitlist." "See Pricing →" affords "I will see prices." It delivers philosophy first, prices second. When a button's affordance doesn't match its destination, the user's trust in the interface degrades. They learn that buttons don't do what they say — and for a trust-first product, this is corrosive.

**Consistency failure — the same action has different paths.** "Buy Credits" appears in three places (homepage "Get Started" section, `/pricing/` grid, `/pricing/cloud/` bottom) and each instance takes the user to a different intermediate page instead of to the same destination (Stripe). A consistent design would have every "Buy Credits" button go to Stripe. The user should be able to predict what a button does based on its label.

**Proportion failure — 8 pricing options for a product with 2 user actions.** The pricing page presents eight cards because the product has eight deployment models. This is the organisation's internal model leaked into the user interface. From the user's perspective, there are two actions: "try it free" or "buy credits." The six other deployment options (self-hosted, own cloud, white label, single-tenant, AWS Marketplace, managed) are relevant to a tiny fraction of visitors and should not compete for attention with the two primary options.

**Honesty failure — the LaunchList redirect.** The SG/Send brand promises "your files, your keys, your privacy." The signup flow promises "we'll send you an access token within 48 hours." The LaunchList page delivers a referral programme, a waitlist position, social sharing buttons, and the user's email in the URL. This is not what the interface promised. Rams's principle of honest design means the thing should not pretend to be something it is not — and the post-signup experience pretends to be part of SG/Send when it is part of LaunchList.

**The core design failure is this: the site's structure reflects the builder's model of the product (deployment options, philosophy, architecture, team) rather than the user's model of their task (send an encrypted file, buy credits to do so, or try it free first).** When form follows the org chart instead of following function, the user must do translation work at every step. That translation work is the friction the Sherpa documented above.

**Recommendation:** Restructure the homepage so that its form follows the user's function:

1. The hero section should contain the two primary CTAs pointing directly to their destinations (Stripe and the signup form — or an inline form). No intermediate pages.
2. "Buy Credits" should be the visually primary CTA during the Challenge campaign. It should be the filled button, left position.
3. The explanation sections (Zero-Knowledge Encryption, feature cards) should appear below the action section — available for those who scroll, but never blocking those who don't need to.
4. The pricing page should lead with the two primary options (Free / £5) and collapse the remaining six options behind an "Other deployment options" expander or a separate page.
5. The Early Access signup should either be an inline form on the homepage or a modal — never a redirect to a third-party domain. If LaunchList must be used for the backend, the confirmation should be hosted within the SG/Send domain with SG/Send branding.

## Summary: Converged Diagnosis

All four roles arrive at the same conclusion through different lenses:

| Role | Lens | Diagnosis |
|------|------|-----------|
| **Advocate** | "Who is this for?" | The homepage doesn't serve the Intentful Buyer persona. The Challenge creates exactly this persona. |
| **Sherpa** | "Where do people get stuck?" | Six documented friction points across both paths. Every one is site navigation friction, not product friction. |
| **Ambassador** | "Will the campaign work?" | The campaign will generate website feedback instead of product feedback. Conversion rate will be unacceptably low. |
| **Designer** | "Does the form serve the function?" | The site's structure reflects the builder's mental model, not the user's task model. Hierarchy, affordance, consistency, proportion, and honesty are all violated. |

**The unified recommendation:** Before launching the SG/Send Challenge, restructure the homepage so that:

1. **"Buy Credits"** goes to Stripe in one click from the hero. No intermediate pages.
2. **"Join Early Access"** goes to a signup form (inline or one click away) with no third-party redirect. The confirmation stays within the SG/Send brand.
3. **The explanation content** (encryption details, philosophy, team stats, deployment options) moves below the action section, not above it.
4. **The visual hierarchy** makes "Buy Credits" primary and "Join Early Access" secondary during the Challenge period.
5. **Every instance of "Buy Credits"** anywhere on the site goes directly to the same Stripe URL. One label, one destination.

The product is good. The encryption works. The Stripe checkout is clean. The value proposition is clear. The problem is that the site stands between the user and the product — and for the Challenge campaign, that is the difference between useful feedback and wasted social capital.
