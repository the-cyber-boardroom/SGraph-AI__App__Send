# The Organic Food Model for Open Source

We have a tragedy of the commons in open source software. It is well documented. Companies make millions and billions on the back of open source and do not give back. And before we reach for the pitchforks, I want to be clear: this is not malice. It is economics. The system we have does not reward giving back. It makes giving back a political decision inside a company, easy to cut because there are no repercussions, and hard to justify because there is no measurable return.

Charity will not fix this. "It is the right thing to do" will not fix this. The only thing that will fix this is market forces. And I think we already have two models that show us how.

## The Organic Food Model

Organic food is a triumph of market economics applied to ethical behaviour. It works because it makes doing the right thing visible, measurable, and commercially advantageous.

The consumer can see the label. The consumer can choose. The company that sources organic ingredients can market that fact. The supply chain is auditable. Nobody is forced to buy organic. But the market rewards those who do, and over time, organic has gone from a niche premium to a mainstream expectation.

B Corp takes this further: not just the product, but the governance, the employee treatment, the environmental impact. All measurable. All visible. All part of the purchasing decision.

## The Pollution Model

Environmental regulation started with a simple insight: pollution was a side effect of bad engineering and bad practices that never got fixed. When companies were forced (by regulation or by market pressure) to reduce pollution, many of them discovered that cleaner processes were also cheaper processes. Better engineering. Less waste. Lower costs.

The environmental movement created measurement (carbon footprint, emissions tracking), transparency (public reporting), and market mechanisms (carbon credits, green certifications). Companies that pollute more are visible. Consumers and business customers can choose to buy from companies that pollute less. The market rewards cleaner operations.

## Apply This to Open Source

Now imagine the same model for software supply chains.

**Step 1: Declare your supply chain.** Companies already need to do this for security (SBOMs, software bills of materials). Extend it: for every piece of open source software in your stack, declare it. This is not optional. It is already becoming a regulatory requirement for security. Make it a commercial requirement too.

**Step 2: Calculate the value contribution.** For each dependency, estimate how much value it contributes to your product. This is debatable. The formula can be argued. But it should exist. If your entire backend runs on an open source framework, that framework is contributing significant value. If you use a small utility library for date formatting, the contribution is small.

This is useful beyond open source. Investors should want to know this. Where is the value in your company? How much of what you sell is your own innovation versus standing on the shoulders of open source? A company that claims to be an AI company but is essentially a thin wrapper around an LLM should be transparent about that. Not because it is wrong, but because the market should know.

**Step 3: Pay proportionally.** Based on the value contribution, pay the open source projects you depend on. Not as charity. As a cost of doing business. The same way you pay for cloud infrastructure, for office space, for salaries. The open source software that powers your product is part of your supply chain. Pay for it.

**Step 4: Make it visible.** This is where the organic food model comes in. Create a rating, a certification, a label. Companies that maintain a healthy payment scheme for their open source dependencies get a good rating. Companies that exploit their dependencies without paying get a bad rating. Publish both.

Just like a restaurant health rating on the door: you can still choose to eat at the restaurant with a bad rating. But you know. And most people, given the choice, will choose the restaurant with the good rating.

## Why Companies Should Want This

Here is the counterintuitive part: paying for open source is cheaper than not paying.

When companies do not pay, open source projects are maintained by two or three people in their spare time. The software is fragile. Security patches are slow. Features that the company needs do not get built. So the company hires internal engineers to maintain their fork, to patch vulnerabilities, to build the features they need. They spend more money internally maintaining open source software than they would have spent supporting the project directly.

This is exactly what happened with pollution. Companies thought reducing emissions would be expensive. Many discovered that the polluting processes were also the inefficient ones. Fixing the pollution fixed the engineering. The same is true for open source: supporting the projects you depend on means they are better maintained, more secure, faster to evolve, and cheaper for you in the long run.

## The Cambrian Explosion

We are about to see an explosion of open source companies. LLMs are enabling small teams to build sophisticated software. The barriers to creating and maintaining open source projects are dropping. But the financial models have not caught up.

If we want this Cambrian explosion to produce sustainable companies (not just side projects that burn out their maintainers), we need the financial infrastructure. We need the measurement, the transparency, the market mechanisms that reward paying for open source and penalise exploiting it.

## What This Is Not

This is not about changing open source licences. This is not about policy or regulation (though regulation may follow). This is not about forcing anyone to do anything.

This is about building a commercial model, a market economics model, that makes markets more efficient, not less. The information asymmetry today (companies do not know or disclose what open source they depend on, how much value it provides, or whether the projects are sustainably funded) makes the market inefficient. Fix the information asymmetry, and the market will do the rest.

Companies that pay their supply chain will be more resilient, more secure, and more attractive to customers who care about sustainability. Companies that exploit their supply chain will be visible, and the market will adjust.

Just like organic food. Just like pollution. The model exists. We just need to apply it to software.
