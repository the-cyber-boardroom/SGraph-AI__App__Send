# The Two Things Missing from Every Student's Education

I had two conversations this week that left me genuinely worried. One was with a 17-year-old heading into computer science. The other was with a university biology student. Both are smart, motivated, and being failed by an education system that has not caught up with the world they are entering.

## They Do Not Know Git

Neither of them knew what version control was. The computer science student. The biology student. Neither.

When I asked how they manage files, the answer was what it always is: "final_v2_APPROVED_v3_really_final.docx." Folders full of copies. No history. No ability to see what changed between versions. No ability to go back. No concept that this problem was solved decades ago.

This is not a niche developer tool anymore. Version control is how the modern world manages knowledge. It is how code is written, how documents are collaborated on, how research is tracked, how decisions are recorded. A student graduating in 2026 without understanding version control is like a student graduating in 1996 without understanding email.

And it is not just that they do not use Git. They do not know the concept exists. They have never been asked the question: "how do you track changes to your work over time?" Because nobody taught them that the question matters.

## They Do Not Know Open Source

Neither student could explain what open source was. Not the philosophy. Not the licences. Not the practical reality that most of the software they use every day is built on open source foundations.

They did not know that there are copyright licences that allow you to use, modify, and share software freely. They did not know that the biggest companies in the world contribute to open source projects. They did not know that they, as students, could contribute to real-world projects that real people use.

More importantly, they did not know that open source applies to them. Their essays, their research, their projects: all of these could benefit from the same principles of sharing, collaboration, and building on each other's work. But nobody told them. And so they work in isolation, reinventing wheels, unable to learn from or contribute to a broader community.

## The R and SPSS Disaster

The biology student had failed a statistics module. They had to retake it. They hated it. And I do not blame them.

The module was taught in R and SPSS. These are tools that, while powerful, are relics of an era when the interface was an afterthought. The student was fighting the software instead of learning the concepts. Three-hour lab blocks spent debugging syntax errors in a language designed for statisticians, not for biology students trying to understand whether their experiment produced meaningful results.

I watched my own daughter go through the same thing recently at university. The R exercises were more complex than they needed to be. The SPSS interface was hostile. The students were learning to hate computing, not to use it.

This is the opposite of what should be happening.

## Learning by Doing, Not by Suffering

Here is what should happen. A biology student should be given a biochemistry problem. A real one. "Here is a dataset from an experiment. Does this drug affect cell growth? Find out." And then they should have tools that help them explore the data visually, interactively, in the context of the biology they are studying.

They should not start with "a variable is a named container for a value." They should start with a number on the screen that changes when they drag a slider, and the chart updates, and they see the relationship. The concept of a variable emerges naturally. The concept of a function emerges when they want to repeat the analysis on different data. The concept of a loop emerges when they have 50 datasets.

We have the technology to create these learning experiences right now. Visual, dynamic, interactive, in context. LLMs can explain what the code does in plain English. Notebooks can mix text, code, and visualisations. AI can generate the boilerplate and let the student focus on the thinking.

Instead, we are teaching them to type `install.packages("ggplot2")` and wonder why they hate computers.

## The Brett Victor Problem

Brett Victor once said that the problem with dogma is not that it exists, but that you stop asking the question. The dogma in education is: "students must learn programming fundamentals before they can do anything useful." The question nobody asks is: "useful to whom, and for what?"

A biology student does not need to learn programming fundamentals. They need to learn how to use computation to answer biological questions. The fundamentals will follow, organically, from the doing. But only if we design the learning experience around the doing, not around the fundamentals.

## Why This Matters for Technology

Every student who graduates hating computers is a future passive consumer of technology instead of an active participant. Every student who does not know Git will struggle to collaborate effectively in any modern workplace. Every student who does not understand open source will miss opportunities to learn from, contribute to, and benefit from the largest body of shared knowledge ever created.

The tools exist. The AI exists. The interactive environments exist. What is missing is the will to redesign education around learning by doing, supported by technology that meets students where they are, not where the curriculum committee thinks they should be.

These are not edge cases. These are representative of an entire generation being taught 20th-century skills with 20th-century tools for a 21st-century world. And the gap is getting wider every semester.
