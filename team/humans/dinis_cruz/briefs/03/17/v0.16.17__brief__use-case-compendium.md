## How to Read This Document

The use cases are organized into five tiers. Each tier represents a step up in complexity, PKI requirements, collaboration depth, and data sensitivity. The three primitives (encrypted file transfer, folder/zip, vault) apply across all tiers, but different tiers lean on different primitives.

| Tier | Collaboration | Vault Role | PKI Level | Key Industries |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | One-to-one | Ephemeral transfer | Shared keys, light PKI | Accountancy, mortgages, consulting |
| 2 | Multi-party, sequential | Working source of truth | Light to medium PKI | Supply chain, board packs, multi-party negotiations |
| 3 | Agent-driven workflows | Data plane (ephemeral or persistent) | Medium PKI, per-agent keys | Agentic coordination, data rooms, legal document workflows |
| 4 | High-integrity, signed | Authoritative source of truth | Strong PKI, mandatory signing | Healthcare, high-stakes contracts, regulatory compliance |
| 5 | Network isolation | Secure data bridge | Strong PKI, typed data | Cross-domain networks, air-gapped systems, infrastructure isolation |

## Tier 1: Point-to-Point Secure Transfer

**Industries:** Accountancy, mortgage brokers, consulting firms, financial advisors.

**The pattern:** Two parties need to exchange documents securely. The workflow is temporal: someone sends a bundle of documents, the other party reviews them, maybe sends back an updated version. There is no deep collaboration. Nobody is editing the same file at the same time. It is sequential, one-directional, and often one-off.

**What they need:**
- Send a single document securely (a tax return, a mortgage application, a consulting report)
- Send a bundle of documents that represents the current state (all receipts for Q4, all supporting documents for a mortgage)
- Occasionally, a vault where documents accumulate over time (an ongoing engagement where the accountant keeps adding materials throughout the year)

**The vault here is not the source of truth.** The accountant has their own systems. The mortgage broker has their own CRM. SG/Send is the secure transport layer. A convenient way to get documents between two parties without email attachments, WeTransfer, or shared drives.

**PKI level:** Light. Shared vault keys or simple access tokens. No multi-party signing. No chain of custody. The security need is confidentiality in transit, not provenance over time.

**Data sensitivity:** Moderate. Financial documents, personal information, tax records. Sensitive enough that unencrypted email is inappropriate. Not so sensitive that you need per-document signing and multi-level access control.

**Agentic angle:** An accountant's AI assistant receives a vault key from a client, processes the documents, generates a summary, and sends back a report via SG/Send. The CLI makes this scriptable. "Hey Claude, here is my accountant's public key, send this tax information." That workflow is already possible today.

**Business model for a startup in this space:** Serverless, low cost to run. Recurring revenue from professionals who need to send and receive documents regularly. White-label the SG/Send transfer with their own branding. Add domain-specific features (receipt categorization for accountants, document checklists for mortgage applications). The encrypted infrastructure is handled. They focus on the workflow.

## Tier 2: Multi-Party Sequential Workflows

**Industries:** Supply chain management, board governance, multi-party negotiations, procurement, trade finance.

**The pattern:** Multiple parties are involved, but the workflow is largely sequential or has defined handoff points. Party A does their part, hands off to Party B, who hands off to Party C. Not everyone needs to see everything. Different parties need different slices of the data.

**What they need:**
- Per-party encryption (each party only sees their relevant data)
- Sequential enforcement (Party B can only start after Party A has signed off)
- A vault that acts as the working source of truth during the process (not permanent, but authoritative while the transaction is active)
- Zip-based distribution for parties that prefer email workflows, with an upgrade path to server-based real-time access

**The vault here is a working source of truth.** Not the final archive (the company has its own systems for that), but the single authoritative state of this transaction while it is in flight. When the transaction completes, the vault can be archived or deleted.

**PKI level:** Medium. Per-party encryption. Signed handoffs to enforce sequencing. Enough PKI to ensure Party B knows Party A actually completed their step. Not yet at the level of legally binding signatures, but cryptographic proof of who did what and when.

**Data sensitivity:** Moderate to high. Order details, pricing, supplier information, board materials, negotiation positions. The per-party encryption ensures that a compromise at one point in the chain only exposes that party's data. This is data minimization enforced by architecture.

**GDPR alignment:** Strong. Per-party encryption means each entity only holds data relevant to their role. Storage location is irrelevant (the server holds ciphertext). Provenance is cryptographic. Right to erasure is achievable by deleting the vault key.

**The supply chain brief (see companion document) is the detailed example for this tier.** Board packs and multi-party negotiations follow the same pattern with different content.

**Business model:** More substantial than Tier 1. The company built here needs to understand the domain (supply chain logistics, board governance, trade finance) and build the specific workflow UI. SG/Send provides the encrypted infrastructure. The startup provides the domain expertise and the customer relationship. This company needs a Town Planner architecture: backups, redundancy, reliability guarantees. That is not something SG/Send should run. That is what the startup should build on top.

## Tier 3: Agent-Driven Workflows

**Industries:** Legal document processing, data rooms, AI-assisted research, automated compliance, LLM-based analysis pipelines.

**The pattern:** Agents (AI or automated systems) are coordinating work. They need a data plane to communicate, share intermediate results, and hand off tasks. The workflow might be ephemeral (a single research task that completes and is discarded) or persistent (an ongoing data room that agents continuously update).

**What they need:**
- Per-agent encryption (each agent only reads messages intended for it)
- Signed messages (verify that a message came from a specific agent, not hallucinated or injected)
- A vault as the communication plane and shared state
- Start/stop/replay capabilities for debugging and auditing
- Branch-per-agent for parallel work

**The vault here is a data plane.** It might be ephemeral (per-transaction) or long-lived (a data room that accumulates analysis over months). The key property is that it provides authenticated, encrypted, sequenced communication between automated entities.

**PKI level:** Medium to strong. Per-agent key pairs. Signed messages are important for prompt injection defense: an agent physically cannot read instructions meant for another agent because it does not have the private key. This is a security property that policy-based systems cannot provide.

**This is the space where people have tried to use blockchain.** The vault model provides similar properties (immutable history, signed transactions, cryptographic verification) without the consensus overhead, the gas costs, or the public ledger. It is a signed, encrypted, append-only log. That is what most blockchain use cases actually needed.

**The agent-to-agent communication brief (see companion document) is the detailed architecture for this tier.**

**Business model:** These are likely the most technically sophisticated startups. They need strong engineering teams and deep understanding of both the domain and the agent orchestration patterns. The dev pack (agentic team templates, role definitions, CI/CD setup) is particularly valuable here. SG/Send could charge for customized dev packs as a revenue stream.

## Tier 4: High-Integrity, Signed Environments

**Industries:** Healthcare (patient records, clinical trials), legal (contracts, evidence, chain of custody), financial services (regulatory filings, audit trails), government (classified document handling).

**The pattern:** Every action must be signed. Every document must have provenance. Every access must be logged. The vault is not just a working file. It is the authoritative, legally meaningful record. Signatures are not just for authentication. They assign responsibility.

**What they need:**
- Mandatory signing on every commit and every document
- Multi-level access control via PKI (some users see some data, others see different data, enforced cryptographically)
- Full audit trail with cryptographic proof of who did what and when
- Vault as the authoritative source of truth (not a copy, not a transport layer, the record)
- Backup of backups. Redundancy. Disaster recovery. This is Town Planner territory.

**PKI level:** Strong. Every participant has a key pair. Every action is signed. The signature is not just damage control (proving you did not tamper with something). It is assignment of responsibility (this doctor approved this treatment, this lawyer signed this contract, this auditor certified this filing).

**Data sensitivity:** Very high. Patient health information, legal evidence, financial audit records, classified documents. The encryption is not optional. The signing is not optional. The provenance is not optional. These are often regulatory requirements, not just good practice.

**Business model:** These are serious companies with serious compliance requirements. They need infrastructure that meets regulatory standards. They need SLAs, uptime guarantees, audit certifications. This is not a weekend project. This is a funded startup or an existing company that integrates SG/Send primitives into their compliance stack. The value proposition: "your data is encrypted end-to-end, every action is signed, and we can prove cryptographically who accessed what and when. Try doing that with SharePoint."

## Tier 5: Network Isolation and Cross-Domain

**Industries:** Critical infrastructure, manufacturing, industrial control systems, defence, government networks, any environment with air-gapped or segmented networks.

**The pattern:** Networks are isolated for security. The isolation is the protection. But data still needs to move in and out. The traditional solution is to open a port, which defeats the isolation. A vault (or encrypted zip) provides a structured, typed, protected way to move data across the boundary without opening network ports.

**What they need:**
- Vault or zip as a data bridge between isolated network segments
- Strongly typed data (the vault contains specific, expected file types, not arbitrary payloads)
- PKI signing to verify the source of the data entering the isolated network
- Logging and monitoring of what crosses the boundary

**The real-world problem:** Servers with unpatched vulnerabilities that cannot be taken offline for patching (production systems, medical devices, industrial control systems). The solution is network segmentation: isolate the vulnerable system. But the isolated system still needs data (configuration updates, log extraction, monitoring data). Every port opened to provide that data is a highway through the isolation.

A vault solves this: package the data, encrypt it, sign it, transfer it as a file (USB, one-way diode, scheduled batch). The receiving system decrypts and validates the signature. No open ports. No persistent network connections. The data crosses the air gap as a self-contained, encrypted, signed package.

**PKI level:** Strong. The signing is critical here. You need to verify that the data entering your isolated network actually came from the expected source and has not been tampered with in transit.

**Business model:** This intersects with defense and critical infrastructure, which means longer sales cycles, higher contract values, and compliance requirements (Common Criteria, government certifications). The company built here needs security clearances, certification expertise, and domain knowledge of industrial control systems or government networks. SG/Send provides the encryption and signing primitives. The company provides the domain expertise and the compliance wrapper.

## Summary Matrix

| Tier | Vault Role | PKI | Collaboration | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ephemeral transport | Light (shared keys) | One-to-one | Accountant sends tax documents |
| 2 | Working source of truth | Medium (per-party) | Multi-party sequential | Supply chain order fulfilment |
| 3 | Data plane | Medium-strong (per-agent) | Agent-driven | AI research pipeline with signed handoffs |
| 4 | Authoritative record | Strong (mandatory signing) | Multi-party, high-stakes | Clinical trial documentation |
| 5 | Secure data bridge | Strong (source verification) | Cross-boundary | Data transfer to air-gapped network |

Each tier builds on the previous one. The primitives are the same. The complexity, the PKI depth, and the business requirements increase. A Tier 1 company can start with shared keys and zip files. A Tier 5 company needs the full PKI stack with signed commits and per-entity encryption. Both are built on the same three primitives.
