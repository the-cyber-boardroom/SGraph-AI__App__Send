## The Problem with URIs

In the Semantic Web and RDF world, URIs are the anchor for identity. Every concept, every relationship, every entity gets a URI. The theory is elegant. The practice is not.

URIs as identity anchors:

- Don't scale. Every new concept requires a new resolvable URI.
- Are verbose. Payloads bloat with repeated namespace prefixes.
- Are semi-persistent in the wrong way. They can break (404), they can change, they can be hijacked.
- Require infrastructure. Someone has to host the resolution endpoint.
- Don't provide authenticity. A URI tells you where something is defined, not who defined it or whether it has been tampered with.
- Complicate everything. Tooling has to handle URI resolution, namespace management, content negotiation.

Their main function is anchoring: providing a way to know what is what. A shared reference point so two systems can agree they are talking about the same concept.

## Digital Signatures as Better Anchors

Certificates and digital signatures solve the same anchoring problem but with stronger guarantees:

- **Authenticity:** a signature tells you WHO made this assertion, not just where it is hosted.
- **Integrity:** a hash tells you the content has not been tampered with.
- **Chain of trust:** certificate chains provide hierarchical authority (who signed this, who signed them, who is the root authority).
- **No infrastructure dependency:** verification does not require a live endpoint. You need access to the certificate chain, but that chain can be cached, distributed, embedded.

The URI says "go to this location to resolve this concept." The certificate says "this entity asserts this, and here is the cryptographic proof."

## From URIs to Clues

The replacement for URIs is not a single alternative but a concept of "clues." A clue is: "this is where you should go to resolve this reference." It is a resolution hint, not a canonical location.

Just like certificate chains: you don't need the entire chain on your machine. You need enough information to follow the chain. "Who signed this?" leads to "who signed them?" leads to the root. Each step is a clue pointing to the next resolution.

This maps directly to how SG/Send vaults work. A vault tree is a set of references (file IDs) that resolve to encrypted blobs. The tree is the set of clues. The blobs are the content. The decryption key is the authority. You don't need to host a URI endpoint. You need the tree (structure), the blobs (content), and the key (authority).

## Document Identity

This leads to a deeper question: should documents have identities?

Not just "who wrote this document" (the author has an identity), but the document itself as an entity with its own cryptographic identity. A signed document is not just a file with a signature attached. It is an entity that can:

- Be verified independently of where it is stored
- Have its provenance traced (who created it, when, from what inputs)
- Be referenced by its identity (public key or hash) rather than its location (URL)

Take this further: paragraphs could have identities. Sections could have identities. Individual assertions could be signed independently within a document. This enables granular provenance: "this paragraph was written by entity X at time T, based on inputs Y and Z."

## Connection to SG/Send

The vault system already implements document-level identity implicitly: every blob has a content-addressed ID (hash), every vault has a tree that maps human-readable paths to blob IDs, and the encryption key is the authority boundary.

The next step: explicit document signing. The CLI expansion (PKI features: generate, sign, verify, encrypt) is already on the roadmap. Combining vault storage with document signing creates a system where documents are:

- Content-addressed (hash-based identity)
- Encrypted for specific readers (authority via public keys)
- Signed by their creators (provenance via digital signatures)
- Resolvable via clues (vault tree structure), not URIs

This is the semantic web done right: identity through cryptography, not through URLs.
