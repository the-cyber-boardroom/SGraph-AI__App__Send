## Context

The average EULA for a cloud service is 4,000 to 8,000 words of dense legal language. Nobody reads them. Studies consistently show that less than 1% of users read the terms before clicking "I agree." The services know this. That is the point.

Buried in those thousands of words are clauses that grant the company broad rights over user content: the right to reproduce, modify, distribute, sublicense, create derivative works, and train machine learning models. These clauses are written in language designed to be technically accurate and practically incomprehensible. When users eventually notice (and they always do), the company issues a "clarification" and narrows the language, but the trust damage is done. This pattern has repeated across multiple major file transfer and cloud storage platforms in recent years.

The fundamental problem is not any one company behaving badly. It is the model itself. When a service can read your files, the only thing standing between your data and any use of it is a policy document. Policy documents can change. They change quietly, with 30 days' notice buried in an email, and a clause buried in a paragraph, in a section, in a document that nobody reads.

SG/Send has a structural advantage that makes this entire category of problem impossible.

## The Architectural Difference

Every conventional file transfer and cloud storage service can read your files. They choose not to. That is a promise. Promises can change. A new CEO, a new investor, a new business model, a new clause in the terms of service.

SG/Send cannot read your files. Encryption happens in the browser. The key lives in the URL fragment (never sent to the server). The server stores AES-256-GCM ciphertext. Without the key, the data is noise. This is not a policy decision. It is a mathematical fact.

This means:

- We cannot train AI on your files (we never see plaintext)
- We cannot sell your files (we cannot read them)
- We cannot hand your files to law enforcement (we cannot decrypt them)
- We cannot quietly update a clause to grant ourselves rights over content we cannot access

The architecture makes the policy enforceable by physics, not by trust.

## The Policy

This is the complete privacy policy for send.sgraph.ai:

---

**How we handle your data**

1. **We cannot read your files.** Encryption happens in your browser before anything is uploaded. The decryption key never reaches our servers. We store encrypted data that is meaningless without your key.

2. **We cannot train AI on your files.** We never have access to the unencrypted content. Not now. Not if we change our minds. Not if we update our terms. The encryption makes it impossible, not a policy.

3. **We cannot hand your files to anyone.** Not law enforcement, not governments, not advertisers, not AI companies. We do not have the key. We cannot comply with a request to produce your data in readable form even if we wanted to.

4. **We track what we need to run the service.** Upload count, download count, storage used, vault operations. Enough to bill you and keep the service running. Nothing about what is inside your files, because we cannot see inside your files.

5. **We do not track you.** Zero cookies. Zero tracking pixels. Zero analytics partners. Zero advertising networks. No Google Analytics, no Facebook Pixel, no third-party scripts watching what you do. We do not build profiles. We do not sell attention. We do not even know who you are unless you create an account.

6. **Your files are deleted when they expire or when you delete them.** Encrypted data is removed from our servers. We do not keep copies. There is nothing useful to keep.

---

Six statements. Each one says "cannot" or "do not," and each one is verifiable. Open your browser's developer tools. Check the network tab. Check the cookies. Check the third-party requests. You will find nothing, because there is nothing. The architecture and the implementation match the policy. No trust required.

## Site Placement

This policy should be one of the most visible elements on the site. Not buried in a footer link. Not hidden behind a "Legal" page. Visible.

### Placement Options

**Homepage:** The policy (or a condensed version) appears on the main landing page. Not as a link to a separate page. As actual text on the page, visible without clicking anything.

**Every transfer page:** When a user uploads or downloads a file, a single line is visible: "We cannot read your files. Here's why." with a link to the full six sentences.

**Vault page:** Same pattern. "Your vault is encrypted. We cannot see what is inside."

**Footer (every page):** The full six sentences. Not a link to them. The actual text. It is short enough to fit.

**Dedicated page:** `/privacy` or `/policy` contains the six sentences, followed by a technical explainer: "How does this work?" with a diagram of the encryption pipeline (browser encrypts with AES-256-GCM, key stays in URL fragment, server stores ciphertext, receiver decrypts in browser).

### QR Code / Shareable

Make the policy page shareable. A QR code on the site that links to `/privacy`. Short enough to screenshot. Short enough to paste into a group chat. The policy itself becomes the marketing asset.

The entire policy fits in a single social media post. "Our privacy policy is 6 sentences. Here it is:" followed by the six points. That is a post that gets shared.

## Legal Considerations

The six-sentence version is the user-facing policy. It may need a companion legal document for regulatory compliance (GDPR, ePrivacy, etc.) that covers the standard requirements: data controller identity, contact details, lawful basis for processing, data retention periods, rights of data subjects. Note: because we have zero cookies, we do not need a cookie banner or a cookie policy. This is itself a differentiator. No consent popups. No "accept all cookies" dark patterns. The site loads clean.

The approach: the six sentences are the policy. The legal document is the appendix. The user sees the six sentences. The lawyer sees the appendix. Both exist. The six sentences are what the brand stands behind.

The legal appendix should be written in the same direct style: short sentences, plain language, no legalese. If a clause cannot be explained in one sentence, rewrite it until it can.

## Why This Matters Now

Trust in cloud services is eroding. Users have watched multiple platforms quietly expand their terms to cover AI training, content licensing, and data commercialisation, then issue "clarifications" only after public backlash. The pattern is so common that younger users now assume any cloud service will eventually claim rights over their data. "Read the terms of service" has become a punchline because the terms are unreadable by design.

Users are actively looking for something different. They value:

- Transparency (short, readable policy that does not require a lawyer to interpret)
- Verifiability (show me how the encryption works, don't just promise privacy)
- Shareability (a policy simple enough to screenshot and send in a group chat)
- Architectural guarantees (the service cannot access my data, not just a promise that it will not)

The six-sentence policy addresses all four. The "cannot" framing is the differentiator. Every competitor says "we will not." We say "we cannot." That one word is the entire marketing message.
