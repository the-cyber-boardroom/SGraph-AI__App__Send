# Working at Two Altitudes: Why the Future Is Not "Everyone Becomes a Manager"

There is a narrative forming around AI agents that goes something like this: knowledge workers will stop being practitioners. They will become managers of agents. They will delegate everything, review the outputs, and never touch the actual work again.

I think this is partially right and mostly dangerous.

## The Management Promotion Trap

We have seen this pattern before. For the last 30-50 years, the standard career path for a technical person has been: get really good at something, then get promoted into a role that does not do that thing.

Amazing developers become engineering managers. Brilliant security engineers become CISOs. Outstanding architects become VPs. And the moment they step into that management role, the skills that made them exceptional start to atrophy. Their job becomes managing others, delegating, fostering teams, running meetings, creating roadmaps. All valuable work. But a different skill set entirely.

The agentic AI narrative is repeating this pattern at a new scale. "You do not need to code anymore. You just need to manage agents." "You do not need to write anymore. You just need to review agent outputs." "You do not need to do the work. You just need to direct the work."

This is the management promotion trap, accelerated.

## The Self-Driving Car Problem

Consider self-driving cars. The pitch is: the car drives itself until it really needs you, and then you take over. This sounds reasonable until you think about it for thirty seconds.

By definition, if the car has been driving itself for two hours, the human has not been paying attention. By definition, the less you drive, the less sharp your driving skills become. And now you are asking someone who has not touched the wheel for two hours to suddenly take over at the moment of maximum difficulty, the moment the autonomous system could not handle. This is not a safety model. It is an accountability transfer.

The same logic applies to managing agents. If you have not read code in months because agents write it all, you cannot meaningfully review what they produce. If you have not done hands-on security work because agents handle it, you cannot verify their findings. The less you practice, the less qualified you are to oversee.

## What I Actually Do

When I became a CISO, my power was that I was still technical. I could operate at board level, exec level, peer level, discussing strategy, risk appetite, and programme maturity. But I could also go hands-on: read code, review architectures, debug issues, write tooling.

This was not just about keeping my skills sharp (though it did that). It was about adding genuine value. I had 40 years of development experience, 25 years of security, 15 years of cloud development. Of course I could add value at the hands-on level. The experience I brought to a code review or an architecture discussion was qualitatively different from what a junior engineer could provide.

Today I work the same way with AI agents. I operate at two altitudes simultaneously. At the strategic level, I manage 20+ AI agents across multiple teams. I define briefs, set direction, review outputs, coordinate across projects. At the hands-on level, I write code, debug vaults, build components, iterate on implementations. I go deep into the domain expertise areas where I have genuine skill.

Both altitudes matter. The strategic work without the hands-on work produces confident-sounding plans disconnected from reality. The hands-on work without the strategic work produces technically excellent solutions to the wrong problems.

## The Junior Problem

People are starting to ask: if AI agents do all the junior-level work, how does anyone become senior? If there are no entry-level coding tasks because agents handle them, where do new developers learn?

This is a real question, but I think the answer is not "protect junior roles from automation." The answer is that LLMs actually make the learning faster and more multi-dimensional.

You only learn by doing. That has not changed. But now "doing" is richer. A junior developer working alongside AI agents is not just writing code. They are reading agent-generated code, understanding why decisions were made, verifying correctness, learning patterns at scale. They have a tireless pair programmer that explains every line, suggests alternatives, and never gets impatient.

The path from junior to senior can go faster, not because the junior skips the learning, but because the learning density per hour increases. The AI does not replace the doing. It amplifies it.

## The Librarian Example

One of my most powerful agent roles is the Librarian. The Librarian catalogues everything, connects documents, tracks what has been decided, maintains context across projects. Combined with the Historian and the Journalist, these roles are transformative for project communication and knowledge management.

In the past, no company could afford to have a librarian on every project. The overhead was unjustifiable. And so every project suffered from the same problems: lost context, duplicated work, tribal knowledge, communication overhead that scales quadratically with team size.

But what if someone whose core skill is knowledge management could now manage 50 or 100 Librarian agents spread across 50 or 100 projects in their organisation? Or what if a single Librarian serves five or ten startups? Suddenly a role that never scaled, that was never well-served by tooling, that companies could never afford, becomes viable.

This is not "the Librarian becomes a manager." This is "the Librarian operates at two altitudes." At the strategic level, they design the cataloguing methodology, the cross-project linking, the knowledge architecture. At the hands-on level, they do the actual cataloguing, the actual connecting, the actual sense-making, amplified by agents that handle the volume.

## The Abstraction Trap

There is a counterargument that says: "Programming has always evolved toward higher abstractions. Nobody writes assembly anymore. Nobody worries about memory allocation in most languages. We always move up the stack."

This is true, and it has also caused enormous damage.

Look at the state of modern software development. A trivial application has 500 to 2,000 dependencies. A CI pipeline downloads half the internet just to build something. The spaghetti behind the scenes is extraordinary. Technical debt is measured in years, not sprints. And the root cause is exactly the pattern being proposed: abstract the complexity into higher-order components and then have nobody on the team who understands those components.

We have been "vibe coding" at the architectural level for 15-20 years. Pulling in dependencies nobody reads. Building on abstractions nobody understands. And it has made software engineering fragile, bloated, and expensive to maintain.

Even in places where abstraction has worked well, someone still reads the low-level code. Linus Torvalds reads C code. The Linux kernel maintainers care deeply about the technical details at every level. The reason Linux is reliable is not that everyone works at a high level of abstraction. It is that some people work at every level simultaneously.

In the past, having people who could work at both the architectural level and the implementation level was expensive and rare. Now, with AI agents handling the volume, it is becoming economically viable. The expert who used to review 50 lines of code per hour can now review 5,000, with agents handling the initial analysis and flagging the parts that need human attention.

## Two Altitudes, Not One

My argument is simple. The future of knowledge work is not "everyone becomes a manager of agents." It is people operating at two altitudes:

**Strategic altitude:** Managing agents, delegating, setting direction, reviewing at scale, working across projects and teams. This is the management layer, and AI makes it more powerful than ever.

**Hands-on altitude:** Going deep into domain expertise, reading the actual work, verifying quality, building things directly, keeping skills sharp. This is the practitioner layer, and AI makes it more scalable than ever.

Both altitudes, in the same person, at different times during the same day. Not one or the other. Both.

The organisations that figure this out will have people who are both strategically effective and technically sharp. The organisations that push everyone into management will have confident managers who cannot verify what their agents produce, building on abstractions nobody understands, repeating the same mistakes we have made for decades but faster.

The future is not about choosing between doing and managing. It is about doing both, at different altitudes, with AI making both more effective than either could be alone.
