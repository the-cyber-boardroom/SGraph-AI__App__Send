## Part 1: Outreach Message Variants

The human described two distinct messaging approaches. Both should be available as templates.

### Variant A: The Feedback Ask

The core ask: "try it, tell me what you think, here's a voucher."

> Hi {name}, as you've probably noticed, I've been working on a number of startups, and my latest one — SG/Send — is really starting to come together.
>
> I'd love to get your views and feedback on it. Would you do me a favour? In exchange for a £5 Amazon gift voucher, could you go to the website, buy £5 of credits, try the product out, and let me know what you think?
>
> I'm really curious about your experience — what you found easy, what you liked, what you didn't, and how smooth the process was.
>
> Bear in mind we're still early — there might be rough edges, and that's exactly the kind of feedback I need.
>
> sgraph.ai

**When to use:** First outreach to anyone. The standard template.

### Variant B: The "I Just Wired Something Up" Series

A repeatable pattern: each time a new feature ships, send a follow-up asking people to test that specific thing.

> Hey {name}, I just wired up {new feature} on SG/Send. Would you mind trying it out and telling me what you think?

**The power of this pattern:** It works multiple times. Each message is a new touchpoint with a genuine reason to reach out:

| Message # | Trigger | Ask |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "I just launched the site" | "Can you try the full flow?" + voucher |
| 2 | "I just wired up Stripe" | "Can you try buying credits?" |
| 3 | "I just wired up auto-provisioning" | "Can you test if the token arrives automatically?" |
| 4 | "I just added your language" | "Can you check if the translation makes sense?" |
| 5 | "I just shipped a performance update" | "Can you try it — is it faster for you?" |

**Each outreach has a genuine reason** — you're not pestering people, you're iterating in public and asking for help. People respect that.

### Variant C: Post-Voucher Follow-Up

For people who already did the challenge and received the voucher:

> Hey {name}, thanks again for trying SG/Send last week! Quick question — have you had a chance to use it since? I'm curious if it's something you'd use again, or if there's something missing that would make it more useful for you.

This is the critical "will they buy more?" question, delivered conversationally.

## Part 2: EAP User Follow-Up — Immediate Action

### The Problem

The first batch of EAP users received access tokens but usage appears to be low. They need a nudge.

### The Message

Two things to communicate:
1. We just pushed a massive update (v2.0, multilingual, performance improvements) — give them a reason to come back
2. Here's a new access code (EAP-March-Week-1) — give them fresh credits and a reason to try again

**Draft email from Sherpa:**

> Subject: SG/Send just got a big update — plus a fresh access code for you 🔐
>
> Hi {name},
>
> Just a quick update — we've been busy since you joined the Early Access Programme.
>
> What's new:
> • The website is now available in 12 languages and 17 countries
> • Significant performance improvements across the board
> • New branded checkout for purchasing credits
> • Improved file transfer experience
>
> We'd love for you to try the updated version. Here's a fresh access code:
>
> **{EAP-March-Week-1}**
>
> Just go to send.sgraph.ai, enter your code, and send an encrypted file.
>
> If you have any questions or feedback, just reply to this email — I'm here to help.
>
> —  
> Sherpa  
> SG/Send | Your files, your keys, your privacy
>
> P.S. If you've been using the service and have feedback, we'd love to hear it — good or bad. Every bit helps us improve.

### Lifecycle Communication Cadence

Beyond this one-off nudge, plan a regular touch-point cadence:

| Trigger | Message | From |
|---|---|---|
| **Token created** | Welcome email with token + getting started guide | Sherpa |
| **First file sent** | "Great, your first encrypted transfer! Here's what happened under the hood..." | Sherpa |
| **50% credits used** | "You've used 250 of your 500 credits — here's a quick summary" | Sherpa |
| **90% credits used** | "You're running low — top up to keep sending" | Sherpa |
| **Credits expired** | "Your credits have expired — here's how to get more" | Sherpa |
| **7 days inactive** | "Haven't sent anything recently — need help getting started?" | Sherpa |
| **30 days inactive** | "We've shipped some updates since you last visited..." | Journalist + Sherpa |
| **New feature shipped** | "We just added {feature} — come try it" | Journalist |

These are the beginning of a proper user lifecycle communication pipeline. For now, they're manual emails from Sherpa. Eventually, they're automated triggers from the billing/usage tracking system.

## Part 3: Building a Messaging Service

The human noted: "this is the kind of thing we need to build a service around — so it's easy to create."

### The Pattern

Every outreach and lifecycle message shares a common structure:

```
{recipient}     — who gets it
{channel}       — WhatsApp, email, LinkedIn, SG/Send
{template}      — which message variant
{variables}     — name, token, feature, usage stats
{language}      — which locale
{trigger}       — what event caused this message
{sent_at}       — when it was sent
{response}      — did they respond? what did they say?
```

### Future: Messaging as a Service

Eventually this becomes a service:

```python
messaging.send(
    recipient="user@example.com",
    channel="email",
    template="welcome_with_token",
    variables={"name": "João", "token": "EAP-March-Week-1"},
    language="pt-PT"
)
```

The service handles: template rendering, language selection, channel delivery (email via WorkMail, SMS via SNS, WhatsApp via API), tracking (sent/opened/clicked), and scheduling.

This is not for today — but the message templates being created now should be structured in a way that a service can use them later.
