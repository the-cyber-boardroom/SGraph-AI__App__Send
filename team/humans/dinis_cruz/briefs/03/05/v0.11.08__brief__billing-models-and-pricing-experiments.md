# Brief: Billing Models and Pricing Experiments — From "Sender Pays" to "Collect Call" and Beyond

**version** v0.11.08  
**date** 5 Mar 2026  
**from** Human (project lead) + technical analysis  
**to** Ambassador (lead), Sherpa, Developer, Architect  
**type** Strategy brief — pricing, billing architecture, competitive positioning  

---

## Context

User feedback confirmed: pricing in gigabytes feels more intuitive than pricing in file credits. The user's instinct of "a gigabyte a pound" feels right. But the conversation surfaced a deeper question: who pays?

Currently, the sender always pays. But in many real-world scenarios — companies receiving files from clients, law firms collecting evidence, healthcare providers receiving patient data — the recipient has more incentive to pay than the sender. This is the "collect call" or "reverse charge" model, and it opens up entirely new markets.

Now is the perfect time to go wide and experiment with early users. This brief maps every billing model we could offer, the use cases each serves, and the technical requirements.

## The Current State of the Market

### WeTransfer (Post-Bending Spoons Acquisition, 2025-2026)

| Tier | Price | Limits | Retention |
|---|---|---|---|
| Free | £0 | 3GB per transfer, 10 transfers/month | 3 days |
| Starter | ~£7/month | Password protection, 300GB/month | 3 days |
| Ultimate | ~£19-25/month | Unlimited transfers, all features | Indefinite |

Users are increasingly frustrated: the free tier is being squeezed (was 2GB, uploads interrupted, ads everywhere), and the only upgrade path is a monthly subscription. User reviews mention "greedy changes," "forced to pay," and "upload stopped at 98%."

### MASV (The Cautionary Tale)

MASV charges $0.25/GB on egress (download). This means a 100GB file downloaded by 3 recipients costs $75, not $25. Users discovered this the hard way — one user reported a $275 charge for what they thought was a $25 upload because the recipient clicked download multiple times. The user felt "ripped off" and switched to a competitor.

**Lesson for us:** Charging per download GB is defensible technically (egress costs money) but feels deceptive if the sender doesn't understand it upfront. Whatever we do, the pricing must be transparent and predictable before the user commits.

### GoodSend (Pay-Per-Send)

$2.50 per send, credits in bundles of 10 ($25 minimum). No subscription. Simple, but at $2.50 per send regardless of file size, it's expensive for small files and cheap for large ones. No GB-based pricing.

## The Billing Models

### Model 1: Sender Pays (Current — File Credits)

**How it works:** Sender buys credits (£5 = 500 files). Each file upload consumes 1 credit regardless of size.

**Pros:** Simple. Already working. Easy to understand.

**Cons:** A 1KB text file costs the same as a 500MB video. Users with large files feel they're overpaying for the per-file model. Users with many small files get great value. Misaligned incentives for bandwidth-heavy use cases.

**Keep for:** Power users who send many small files. API/automation users who send high volumes of small documents.

### Model 2: Sender Pays (GB Credits)

**How it works:** Sender buys storage/transfer in gigabytes. £1 per GB (the user's suggested price point). £5 = 5GB of upload capacity.

**What counts:** Upload size. The sender knows exactly what they'll pay before uploading. Downloads are free up to a limit (see "Download Allowance" below).

**Pros:** Intuitive — maps directly to what the user cares about (how much data they're sending). No surprises. Competitive with MASV ($0.25/GB = ~£0.20/GB) at a higher price point but with encryption and better UX included.

**Cons:** Large files consume credits fast (a 3.5GB video = £3.50). Need to handle the download/bandwidth cost somehow.

**Price testing:**

| Price Point | Feels Like | Margin |
|---|---|---|
| £1/GB | User's instinct. Simple. Easy to remember. | Good — S3 storage + CloudFront egress well under £0.10/GB |
| £0.50/GB | Aggressive. Undercuts MASV. | Thinner but still profitable |
| £2/GB | Premium. Justifiable with encryption + viewers. | High margin |

**Recommended starting point:** £1/GB. Simple, memorable, validated by user feedback.

### Model 3: Download Allowance (Free Downloads with Limits)

**How it works:** Every upload comes with X free downloads. After the allowance is consumed, additional downloads are charged to the sender (or the file becomes unavailable until more credits are purchased).

**Options:**

| Allowance | Good For | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **5 downloads free** | Person-to-person sharing (send to a few friends) | Generous enough for most use cases |
| **5x the upload size free** | "£1/GB gets you 1GB upload + 5GB of downloads" | Maps bandwidth cost directly |
| **Unlimited downloads for 7 days** | Time-limited virality (share widely, but it expires) | Could be abused as a CDN |
| **Pay-per-download after allowance** | Enterprise/heavy use | Surprise charges (the MASV problem) |

**Recommended:** 5 downloads free per upload. After that, additional downloads consume the sender's GB credits (or the sender tops up). This is transparent: "£1/GB includes 5 downloads. Need more? Top up."

For the 3.5GB video shared with 100 people scenario: 3.5GB upload + (100 x 3.5GB downloads) = 353.5GB of bandwidth. At 5 free downloads, the first 17.5GB is included. The remaining 336GB needs to be covered — either by the sender topping up, or by switching to the "collect call" model.

### Model 4: Recipient Pays ("Collect Call" / "Reverse Charge")

**How it works:** The recipient has an SG/Send account with credits. When they download a file, the download is charged to THEIR account, not the sender's.

**The analogy:** Exactly like a collect call / reverse charge call in telephony. The caller (sender) initiates the connection but doesn't pay for it. The person receiving (downloading) accepts the charge.

**Use cases where this is natural:**

| Scenario | Why the Recipient Should Pay |
|---|---|
| **Company receiving files from clients** | A law firm asks clients to upload evidence. The firm should pay for receiving it, not the client. |
| **Job applications** | A company asks candidates to submit portfolios. The company pays for storage/bandwidth, not the applicant. |
| **Healthcare: patient uploads to clinic** | The clinic asks patients to upload medical images. The clinic pays — it's a business cost. |
| **Freelancer delivering to client** | The photographer delivers 50GB of wedding photos. The client paying for the download is natural — they commissioned the work. |
| **Government/regulatory submission** | A company submits compliance documents to a regulator. The regulator pays for receiving. |
| **Media submission portals** | A broadcaster asks contributors to submit footage. The broadcaster pays. (MASV Portals already does this.) |

**How it works technically:**

1. Recipient creates an "upload portal" or "receive link" — a URL where anyone can upload files to the recipient's vault
2. The portal is branded with the recipient's identity
3. Sender uploads files via the portal — no account needed, no credits needed
4. The upload and storage are charged to the recipient's account
5. Downloads by the recipient are also charged to their account

**The portal pattern:** MASV already validates this with "MASV Portals" — free to create, branded upload windows where anyone can send files to a recipient. The recipient pays for all transfers. MASV users love this feature. We should copy the pattern and add encryption.

### Model 5: Split Billing

**How it works:** The cost is split between sender and recipient. The sender pays for the upload and initial storage. The recipient pays for downloads.

**Example:** Sender uploads 5GB (charges: £5 to sender). Recipient downloads 5GB (charges: £5 to recipient, or free if within allowance).

**Use cases:** Collaborative scenarios where both parties benefit — legal discovery (both sides pay), business partnerships, bidirectional data sharing.

**Complexity:** Higher than other models. Both parties need accounts. Not recommended for MVP but worth having in the architecture.

### Model 6: Third-Party Pays (Sponsor / Enterprise Account)

**How it works:** An organisation has a master account that covers all costs for its users. Individual senders and recipients don't see pricing — the organisation's account absorbs everything.

**Use cases:**

| Scenario | Who Pays |
|---|---|
| **Enterprise deployment** | The company's SG/Send account covers all employee usage |
| **Event organiser** | The conference pays for all speaker file submissions |
| **Agency/client** | The agency account covers all client file transfers |
| **University** | The institution covers all student/faculty transfers |

**How it works technically:** The master account generates sub-accounts or access tokens that are linked back to the master billing. Usage by any linked account rolls up to the master.

**This is the Chrome extension corporate branded model:** The enterprise deploys the branded extension, all employees use it, all billing rolls up to the enterprise account.

### Model 7: Freemium with Metered Upgrade

**How it works:** Free tier with meaningful limits. Paid only when limits are exceeded. No subscription — just top up when needed.

**Proposed free tier:**

| Resource | Free Allowance | After Allowance |
|---|---|---|
| Upload | 1GB per month | £1/GB |
| Downloads per file | 5 | £0.20/GB or top up |
| Retention | 7 days | £0.50/week or permanent for £2 |
| Files per month | 50 | £1/GB |
| Vault storage | None (paid feature) | Vault pricing TBD |

**Why 1GB free:** It's enough to try the product meaningfully — send a few PDFs, share a short video, create a small data room. It's more than WeTransfer free (which now limits to 10 transfers/month). It lets the user experience the quality before committing money.

**Why not unlimited free:** SG/Send doesn't have ad revenue. Every transfer costs real money (S3, CloudFront, Lambda). A generous free tier that gets abused will bankrupt the business.

### Model 8: Time-Based Pricing (Retention Credits)

**How it works:** Base upload is cheap or free. The user pays for how long they want the file to remain available.

| Retention | Price |
|---|---|
| 24 hours | Free (with any upload) |
| 7 days | Included with paid uploads |
| 30 days | +£0.50 per GB |
| 90 days | +£1 per GB |
| Permanent | +£2 per GB (moves to vault storage pricing) |

**Use case:** WeTransfer's 3-day limit was explicitly cited as annoying by the user. Offering 7 days as default (vs WeTransfer's 3) is already a differentiator. Paid extensions for 30/90/permanent create a revenue stream from users who need files to stick around.

### Model 9: Subscription (Predictable Monthly)

**How it works:** Monthly fee for a set amount of capacity.

| Tier | Price | Includes |
|---|---|---|
| Personal | £5/month | 10GB upload, 50GB download, 30-day retention |
| Professional | £15/month | 50GB upload, 250GB download, 90-day retention, vault |
| Business | £50/month | 200GB, unlimited downloads, permanent retention, vault, branded portal |

**When to introduce:** Not now. Subscriptions make sense when usage patterns are predictable. Early users need pay-as-you-go to experiment without commitment. Subscription is a Phase 2 offering once we understand usage patterns.

## The "Upload Portal" — Enabling Reverse Charge

The collect call model requires a new primitive: the **upload portal**. This is a URL where anyone can send files TO a recipient, with the recipient paying.

### How It Works

1. **Recipient creates a portal:** "I want to receive files." Gets a branded URL: `send.sgraph.ai/portal/acme-legal`
2. **Portal configuration:** The recipient sets limits (max file size, accepted file types, expiry, encryption requirements)
3. **Sender uses the portal:** Navigates to the URL, drops files, optionally adds a message. No account needed. No payment needed.
4. **Files are encrypted and stored:** Charged to the recipient's account
5. **Recipient is notified:** "New files received from [sender name/email]"
6. **Recipient accesses files:** Via their vault or via the portal dashboard

### Why This Is Powerful

The portal inverts the entire acquisition model. Currently: the sender discovers SG/Send, signs up, buys credits, uploads. With portals: the RECIPIENT is the customer. They create portals and distribute them. Every person who uploads via the portal experiences SG/Send without paying — and many of them will think "I want this for my own use."

**DRU impact:** Every portal is a user acquisition channel. The recipient pays; the senders are free users who experience the product. Some fraction become paying customers themselves.

**Competitive advantage:** MASV charges $0.25/GB for portal uploads (billed to the portal owner). We can offer better value at £1/GB with end-to-end encryption that MASV doesn't have.

## Billing Architecture Requirements

### What the Billing System Must Track

| Event | Data |
|---|---|
| `file_uploaded` | File ID, size (bytes), uploader account, timestamp |
| `file_downloaded` | File ID, size (bytes), downloader (if known), timestamp |
| `file_stored_day` | File ID, size, days since upload |
| `portal_upload` | Portal ID, file ID, size, portal owner account |
| `vault_operation` | Vault ID, operation type, size, timestamp |
| `credit_purchased` | Account, amount, payment method, timestamp |
| `credit_consumed` | Account, amount, reason (upload/download/storage/portal), timestamp |

### What the Billing System Must Support

| Capability | Required For |
|---|---|
| **Per-account credit balance** | All models |
| **Per-file download counter** | Download allowance, analytics |
| **Per-file retention timer** | Time-based pricing |
| **Per-portal billing rollup** | Collect call / reverse charge |
| **Account linking** (sub-accounts to master) | Enterprise / third-party pays |
| **Usage alerts** | "You've used 80% of your credits" |
| **Billing API** | Programmatic credit purchase and usage tracking |

### Stripe Integration

All payment flows through Stripe. The billing system calculates what's owed; Stripe handles payment. For the portal/collect-call model, the portal owner's Stripe payment method is charged for all portal-received uploads.

## The "No Surprises" Principle

### Why MASV's Problem Is a Comms Failure, Not a Pricing Failure

MASV charging per-download-GB is technically defensible — egress costs money. The failure was not the pricing model. It was the complete absence of visibility, monitoring, and proactive communication. The user uploaded 100GB expecting a $25 charge and received a $275 bill because nobody told them what was happening as it happened. That is a failure in:

- **Comms:** The pricing page didn't make the download multiplier obvious
- **Observability:** The user had no dashboard showing real-time cost accumulation
- **Monitoring:** No alert fired when costs exceeded the expected amount
- **Visualisation:** No chart, no progress bar, no "you've used X of your budget" indicator

If MASV had sent a notification at $50 saying "your transfer has been downloaded 3 times, current cost: $75, projected cost at current rate: $150 — want to set a download limit?" the user would have felt informed and in control, not ripped off.

**We must never do this to our customers.** Billing surprises are a trust-destroying event. In encrypted file transfer — where trust is the entire value proposition — a billing surprise is a brand-fatal error.

### Buy Credits: No Surprises by Design

For the foreseeable future, SG/Send uses a **prepaid credit model.** The user buys credits before using them. Credits have a cap — you cannot spend more than you purchased. This is structurally incapable of producing surprise charges.

| Property | How Credits Enforce It |
|---|---|
| **No overspend** | You can't use credits you haven't bought. Balance hits zero, service pauses gracefully. |
| **Predictable cost** | The user knows exactly what they spent: £5. Not £5 plus surprise overages. |
| **No recurring charge** | No subscription that auto-renews and surprises the credit card statement. |
| **User controls timing** | Buy when you need, use at your pace, top up when ready. No monthly pressure. |

The credit model applies to both pricing dimensions: file-based credits (500 files for £5) and GB-based credits (5GB for £5). Both are prepaid. Both have a hard cap. Neither can produce a surprise.

### What the User Must See — Always

Even with prepaid credits, users need full visibility into what's happening with their balance. The goal: the user should never have to wonder "how many credits do I have left?" or "what happened to my credits?"

**Real-time balance display:**
- Credit balance visible at all times in the UI (top bar, extension popup, vault dashboard)
- Balance updates immediately after every operation (upload, download consumed, storage day charged)

**Usage breakdown:**
- Per-file cost shown before upload ("This 500MB file will use 0.5 GB credits")
- Per-operation cost shown before action ("Saving this to vault will use 1 credit")
- Running total for the current session ("This session: 3 files uploaded, 1.2GB, 1.2 credits used")

**Proactive alerts:**
- "You have 20% of your credits remaining"
- "At your current usage rate, credits will last approximately 3 more days"
- "This upload will use your last 0.8 GB of credits — top up?"
- Alert BEFORE an action that would exhaust credits, not after

**Transaction history:**
- Every credit purchase and every credit consumption logged with timestamp, operation type, file name, and amount
- Exportable as CSV for users who expense it
- Accessible in the extension management UI (extension.sgraph.ai) and in the main dashboard

**Download tracking (for senders):**
- "Your file has been downloaded 3 of 5 free times"
- "2 free downloads remaining before additional credits are consumed"
- Notification when the free download allowance is reached
- Option to top up or disable further downloads

### What This Means for Architecture

The billing system is not a background service — it is a **user-facing, real-time product feature.** It needs:

- Sub-second balance updates (not batch processing overnight)
- WebSocket or SSE push for live balance changes (if two browser sessions are open, both see the balance update)
- Pre-flight cost calculation for every operation ("this will cost X — proceed?")
- Alert engine that fires proactively, not reactively
- Full audit trail accessible to the user, not just to admins

This is the same philosophy as the security posture traffic light in the Chrome extension: give the user a clear, always-visible signal about the state of something they care about. For security, it's green/amber/red. For billing, it's the credit balance and usage rate.
