## Framing Principle

The pricing page is not only a commercial surface. For the AWS Conference audience it is a proof point: "This team understands commercialisation, has structured their offers correctly, and is not vaporware." Every element — what is shown, what is hidden, what is labelled "coming soon" — is a signal to a sophisticated evaluator.

The brief states: "the pricing page is also important to provide guidance on what exists today and what we will be doing next." That dual purpose shapes every recommendation here.

## 3. Pricing Page Structure Proposal

### 3.1 Page Headline

> **The price of encrypted infrastructure. On any cloud, at any scale.**

**Rationale:** Positions the pricing page as infrastructure pricing, not consumer SaaS pricing. Signals commercial maturity to the AWS Conference audience. Anchors to "any cloud" — reflecting the 7-deployment-target reality.

### 3.2 Page Sub-headline

> From free and open source to one-click AWS Marketplace deployment. Every mode uses client-side encryption — the server cannot read your files regardless of where it runs.

**Rationale:** Surfaces the encryption guarantee immediately (not just at the bottom). The "client-side" framing echoes the hero line positioning.

### 3.3 Narrative Structure (Top to Bottom)

The page should tell a progression story, not just show a table. Proposed sections:

---

**Section A: Start Free**
*"Try the product before you commit to anything."*

Two cards:
- **Try It Free** (Early Access Programme — no payment, evaluation credits)
- **Open Source** (GitHub, self-host, free forever)

No pricing objection to overcome. This section removes all friction.

---

**Section B: Pay As You Use**
*"When you're ready to send at scale."*

One featured card:
- **£5 Starter Pack** — 500 transfers at £0.01/file, 3-month validity, Stripe checkout

This is the only paid tier that exists today. Feature it cleanly. Do not bury it behind complexity.

---

**Section C: Deploy in Your Environment**
*"Run SG/Send in your own infrastructure — your cloud, your rules."*

Three cards:
- **Docker Container** — deploy on ECS, Fargate, GCP, or EC2. Free (you pay compute). EXISTS.
- **AWS Marketplace — Playwright Service** — COMING SOON. Register Interest.
- **AWS Marketplace — SG/Send Server** — COMING SOON. Register Interest.

**Design note:** The "coming soon" cards should look confident, not apologetic. AWS conference attendees expect to see a roadmap. A grey "coming soon" badge is fine; a greyed-out, half-visible card signals embarrassment.

---

**Section D: Enterprise**
*"Dedicated infrastructure, custom SLA, compliance-ready."*

One card:
- **Dedicated / Single-Tenant** — "Contact us." No price shown. Brief feature list.

This is the enterprise conversation-starter. The AWS Conference audience will recognise this pattern.

---

**Section E: Architecture Guarantee (Full-Width Callout)**
*"Every mode. Same encryption."*

> Whether you use our hosted service or deploy in your own AWS account, client-side AES-256-GCM encryption is identical. The server cannot read your files — regardless of where it runs.

This section should appear between Section C and Section D. It reassures technical buyers that choosing the managed option does not weaken the security model.

---

### 3.4 On Showing vs. Hiding Prices for "Coming Soon" Items

**Recommendation: Show the pricing model category, not the number.**

For the AWS Marketplace AMIs, the brief identifies four potential models: free tier, BYOL, usage-based, and annual subscription. We do not yet know which model AWS will accept or which will perform best.

The correct approach for the pricing page:
- Show the model type: "Free tier (you pay EC2 compute) + optional paid add-ons"
- Do NOT show a specific dollar/pound number for the AMI listings
- Use "Pricing to be confirmed on AWS Marketplace" as the price line
- Use "Register Interest" as the CTA

This is commercially honest, protects against being locked into wrong numbers, and signals to evaluators that pricing is being structured thoughtfully — not plucked from thin air.

## 4. AWS Marketplace Strategy — Pricing Model Recommendation

### 4.1 Starting Point: Free Tier First

The brief recommends starting with a free tier to get traction and usage data. This is correct for the following reasons:

1. **AWS Marketplace discoverability** — free listings appear in more searches. Paid listings require more procurement steps.
2. **Feedback loop** — free tier users generate usage data that informs the paid tier structure.
3. **Conference optics** — "Free on AWS Marketplace" is a simpler message for conference conversations than "£0.02 per API call after 1,000 calls."
4. **Risk reduction** — if the AMI listing has any issues (packaging, documentation, support expectations), a free tier has lower consequences.

### 4.2 The Recommended Starting Model per AMI

| AMI | Model | Rationale |
|-----|-------|-----------|
| **Playwright Service** | Free tier (EC2 compute only) | Browser automation is developer tooling. Developers will not pay upfront for something unproven. Free = adoption. |
| **SG/Send Server** | Free tier with optional BYOL upgrade | The server code is the same as the hosted product. Free community tier, paid tier for SLA/support/compliance docs. |
| **Tools Suite** | Free tier | Tools run client-side. Value accrues from the vault integration (SG/Send Server), not the tools AMI itself. |

### 4.3 The Path to Paid

Free tier starts the clock. Paid tiers get added once:
1. Usage data shows the free tier is being used
2. Enterprise buyers start asking for support SLAs and compliance docs
3. AWS metering is tested and understood

The brief is explicit: "Start with free tier to get traction and usage data. Add paid tiers once we understand the demand." The pricing page should reflect this sequencing honestly — it demonstrates commercial discipline, not commercial immaturity.

## 5. Investor / Partner Messaging on the Pricing Page

### 5.1 What Signals the Page Should Send

The AWS Conference audience will review this page. They are evaluating commercial maturity. The signals that build confidence:

| Signal | How to Achieve It |
|--------|------------------|
| **Working payment infrastructure** | The Stripe checkout must be prominent and functional. A live £5 button is more credible than a polished "enterprise pricing" page with no actual purchase path. |
| **Honest roadmap** | "Coming soon" labels on the AMI cards are not a weakness — they are a signal that the team knows the difference between what exists and what is planned. |
| **Deployment breadth** | The "7 deployment targets, one codebase" statement on the pricing page signals that the product is not tied to one cloud vendor. This matters to AWS partners, who want portable ecosystem tools. |
| **Client-side encryption in all tiers** | The Architecture Guarantee callout should be prominent. Every pricing tier carries the same security model. This eliminates the "cheaper plan = weaker security" concern. |
| **Open source foundation** | "Clone the source, audit it, deploy it free" is a trust signal that enterprise buyers recognise as a sign of architectural confidence. Closed-source-only products are a procurement risk for security tools. |

### 5.2 What Must NOT Be on the Pricing Page

**Items to exclude — they create FUD or signal embarrassment:**

| Item | Why to Exclude |
|------|---------------|
| Agent/team stats bar ("18 agents, 73 endpoints") | Signals "startup in progress", not "trusted infrastructure tool" |
| Vague "contact sales" without any price anchor | Signals the product is not commercially structured |
| Greyed-out or visually disabled "coming soon" cards | Signals the roadmap is speculative, not planned |
| "Zero-knowledge" language (use "client-encrypted" instead) | Jargon that creates anxiety in non-technical buyers |
| "Will not" where "cannot" is accurate | Weakens the primary differentiator to a policy promise |
| Pricing numbers for tiers that haven't been modelled | Locks in wrong numbers before data exists |
| Expiring free tier messaging ("free for the first 30 days") | SG/Send's free tier is open source and genuinely free — do not create false urgency |

### 5.3 The Partner / AWS-Specific Conversation

For an AWS partner or AWS Marketplace evaluator, the key messages are:

1. **"We already have CI/CD baking AMIs"** — the pipeline is real. This is not a first-time marketplace submission from a team that has never done this.
2. **"Seven deployment targets means the customer chooses their risk posture"** — this is an AWS-friendly message. It supports customer choice rather than vendor lock-in.
3. **"The Docker container exists today"** — enterprise buyers can evaluate the technology without waiting for the AMI listing. The container is the same codebase.
4. **"Open source under permissive licence"** — no procurement blocker. Enterprise can audit, fork, or extend.

## 7. Summary: Three Key Pricing Decisions

1. **Lead with the £5 Starter Pack as the only current paid tier.** It is live, it is Stripe-connected, it is £0.01/file. Feature it prominently. Do not dilute it with speculative paid tiers shown at the same visual weight.

2. **Show the AWS Marketplace AMIs as "coming soon" — confidently, not apologetically.** Use "Register Interest" CTAs, show the model type but not specific numbers, and describe what each AMI delivers once available. This is an honest roadmap, not a placeholder.

3. **Use the pricing page to carry the deployment breadth story.** "Seven deployment targets, one codebase, same encryption everywhere" is a competitive moat claim that belongs on the pricing page — not just in investor materials. Technical buyers at the AWS Conference will respond to this claim.
