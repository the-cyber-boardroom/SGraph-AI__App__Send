## Executive Summary

These three briefs form a connected pipeline: the website improvements brief gets users to the payment page, the payment notification brief handles what happens when they pay, and the Challenge brief orchestrates the entire journey as a structured outreach campaign. The Sherpa's job is to walk every path a real person will take and identify where they will get stuck, confused, or give up.

**The critical finding across all three briefs is the same gap identified in the page redesign review: the post-payment dead zone.** A user pays GBP 5 and then enters a period of unknown duration where they have spent money but cannot use the product. The payment notification pipeline brief is the mechanism for closing this gap -- but it proposes a manual, multi-channel alert to the human, who then manually creates a token and manually emails it. For the Challenge campaign's target of 50-100 participants, this manual pipeline will break.

The second critical finding is that the Challenge brief assumes several capabilities that do not exist yet: the "Buy Credits" button on the homepage is not yet linked to Stripe, the post-payment success page sends users to send.sgraph.ai where they cannot do anything without a token, and the "send confirmation via SG/Send" step in the Challenge requires the participant to independently figure out how to send a message through a product they are using for the first time.

**PROPOSED features referenced in these briefs that DO NOT EXIST (per reality document v0.6.36):**
- Stripe webhook endpoint on FastAPI admin API -- does not exist
- Email notification service (WorkMail or n8n integration for payment alerts) -- does not exist
- SMS notification service (AWS SNS or Twilio) -- does not exist
- WhatsApp notification service (any provider) -- does not exist
- Two-way WhatsApp agent communication -- does not exist
- Automated token provisioning on payment -- does not exist
- News/blog section on sgraph.ai -- does not exist
- Docs site (docs.sgraph.ai) -- does not exist
- "Buy Credits" button on homepage linked to Stripe -- does not exist (Stripe link exists only on `/pricing/cloud/`)

**What DOES exist:**
- Early Access signup endpoint (`POST /api/early-access/signup`) -- exists and tested
- Payment success page (`/en-gb/payment/success/`) -- exists with content reviewed below
- Payment cancel page (`/en-gb/payment/cancel/`) -- exists with content reviewed below

- Token management API (create, revoke, reactivate) -- exists on Admin Lambda

## Brief 1: Payment Notification Pipeline

### User Journey: "I just paid GBP 5. Now what?"

Walking this from the moment a user completes Stripe checkout:

| Step | Time | What happens (proposed) | What the user experiences | Friction |
|------|------|------------------------|--------------------------|----------|
| 1 | T+0s | Stripe processes payment | User sees Stripe "Payment successful" confirmation | None -- Stripe handles this well |
| 2 | T+2s | Stripe redirects to `/payment/success/` | User sees "Payment successful" with next steps | Low -- page exists, content is clear |
| 3 | T+2s | Stripe fires webhook to FastAPI | PROPOSED -- endpoint does not exist yet | N/A (backend) |
| 4 | T+3s | Email + SMS + WhatsApp sent to human | PROPOSED -- none of these channels exist yet | N/A (backend) |
| 5 | T+?? | Human sees notification, opens admin panel | Depends on which channel the human checks first | **Variable -- could be minutes or hours** |
| 6 | T+?? | Human navigates to admin panel, creates token | Manual process: `https://admin.send.sgraph.ai/tokens/create` | **Depends on human availability** |
| 7 | T+?? | Human emails token to customer | Manual process: compose email, copy token, send | **Depends on human availability** |
| 8 | T+?? | Customer receives email, reads instructions | Email must be clear about what to do with the token | **Moderate -- new concept for most users** |
| 9 | T+?? | Customer goes to send.sgraph.ai, enters token | Access gate prompts for token | **Low if instructions are clear** |
| 10 | T+?? | Customer can now use the product | First file upload | **The journey finally begins** |

### Friction Points Identified

**F1: The entire pipeline is manual.** Even with four notification channels, every step after the webhook (which itself does not exist yet) requires human action. The brief explicitly acknowledges this: "The human needs to see this immediately so they can manually create the access token and email it to the customer." The four notification channels are redundancy for a single point of failure: the human.

**F2: No SLA for token delivery.** The payment success page says "typically within a few hours." The brief does not define what "a few hours" means. For the Challenge campaign, where participants are trying to complete a flow in a single session, even 30 minutes is too long. The user has paid, their browser is open, and they are waiting. Every minute of delay increases the probability they close the tab and never return.

**F3: The "Go to SG/Send" button on the payment success page is actively harmful.** I verified this button exists in the current `/en-gb/payment/success/index.html` -- it links to `https://send.sgraph.ai` with `class="btn btn-primary btn--lg"`. It is the primary CTA on the page. When the user clicks it, they will arrive at send.sgraph.ai without a token and be unable to do anything. This button creates the false expectation that they can use the product immediately after payment. This was also flagged in the page redesign review (observation 11), but it bears repeating because the payment notification brief does not address it.

**F4: No confirmation email from SG/Send.** Stripe sends a receipt, but the user receives nothing from SG/Send until the human manually emails the token. There is no "thank you for purchasing, your token is being prepared" email. The gap between Stripe's receipt and the token delivery email is silent. The user does not know if their purchase was registered.

**F5: The notification email template does not include customer-ready content.** The brief's email template is designed for the internal team: "New payment received: GBP 5 from {email}. Create access token." There is no template for the email that goes TO the customer with their token. The brief focuses entirely on alerting the human and says nothing about what the customer receives.

**F6: No fallback if all four notification channels fail.** The brief proposes email, SMS, WhatsApp, and a second email. If all four are delayed (weekend, phone off, notifications silenced), the customer waits indefinitely. There is no monitoring, no escalation, and no automated fallback.

### Support Burden Prediction

| Question | Likelihood | When | Severity |
|----------|-----------|------|----------|
| "I paid but haven't received my token" | **Very high** -- nearly every customer | 30 min to 24h after payment | High -- paying customer blocked from using product |
| "How long does the token take?" | **High** | Immediately after seeing success page | Medium -- expectation management |
| "I clicked Go to SG/Send but it's asking for a token I don't have" | **High** | Immediately after payment | High -- user feels misled |
| "Where is my receipt?" | **Low** -- Stripe handles this | After payment | Low -- Stripe sends receipt automatically |
| "I want a refund" | **Medium** -- if token delivery takes >24h | 24-48h after payment | High -- reputation risk |

**Predicted support volume for Challenge campaign (50-100 participants):** 30-60 "where is my token" emails to sherpa@sgraph.ai within the first week. If token delivery averages 4 hours, most participants will email before receiving their token.

### Recommendations

**R1 (P0): Automate token creation on payment.** The webhook should trigger automatic token creation via the Admin Lambda's `POST /tokens/create` endpoint. The token should be emailed to the customer within minutes of payment. This eliminates the manual bottleneck entirely. The brief already describes the webhook infrastructure -- extending it to call the token creation API and send a customer email is the minimal viable pipeline.

**R2 (P0): Fix the payment success page immediately.** Three changes before any outreach:
1. Remove or relabel the "Go to SG/Send" primary CTA -- replace with "Check Your Email" or demote to secondary.
2. Change "typically within a few hours" to a specific commitment: "within 2 hours" (if manual) or "within 5 minutes" (if automated).
3. Add "Haven't received your token after [X]? Email sherpa@sgraph.ai" as a fallback.

**R3 (P1): Send an immediate confirmation email from SG/Send.** When the webhook fires, before the token is even created, send an email: "Thank you for purchasing SG/Send credits. Your access token is being prepared and will arrive at this email address within [X]. Here's what you'll be able to do: [brief product description]." This closes the silent gap.

**R4 (P1): Add a token status page.** A simple page at `sgraph.ai/token-status/` or `send.sgraph.ai/status` where the user can enter their email and see whether their token has been created. This converts "email the Sherpa" support requests into self-service checks.

**R5 (P2): Build the notification pipeline incrementally.** Start with email only (one channel, to the human). Add SMS and WhatsApp later. The brief proposes all four channels simultaneously, which is ambitious for a first sprint. Email to the human plus automated token creation is the minimum viable pipeline.

## Brief 2: The SG/Send Challenge -- Outreach Campaign

### User Journey: Walking the Challenge Path as a Participant

I will walk every step of the Challenge from the participant's perspective, noting where they will get stuck.

#### Step 1: Receive the challenge message

| Channel | User experience | Friction |
|---------|----------------|----------|
| WhatsApp | Personal message from a friend. Sees the challenge description and sgraph.ai link. | **Low** -- WhatsApp messages have high open/read rates |
| LinkedIn | Public post or DM. Sees the challenge with a screenshot of the site. | **Medium** -- LinkedIn DMs from non-connections may be filtered |
| Email | Longer-form description. May be caught by spam filters. | **Medium** -- promotional-sounding email to known contacts |

**Friction point C1: The message asks the user to spend GBP 5.** Even with the Amazon voucher promise, asking someone to spend money immediately creates a trust barrier. The message says "I'll send you a GBP 5 Amazon voucher as a thank you" -- but the user has to trust that the voucher will actually arrive. For close friends, this is not an issue. For LinkedIn contacts and acquaintances, this is a real objection. The voucher promise is unverifiable at the point of the ask.

**Friction point C2: The user must decide whether to engage before knowing what they are signing up for.** The WhatsApp message wisely says "Wait for their 'yes' before sending the detailed steps." This is good practice -- it creates a micro-commitment. But it also means the user says "yes" based on a brief description. When they then receive the detailed steps and discover they need to create an account, enter payment details, learn a new product, and send a file to someone, the actual commitment may feel larger than expected.

#### Step 2: Go to sgraph.ai, find pricing, buy credits

Walking this with the CURRENT website (as it exists today, not the proposed redesign):

| Sub-step | User action | What happens | Friction |
|----------|------------|-------------|----------|
| 2a | Types sgraph.ai in browser | Homepage loads (locale auto-detected or defaults to en-gb) | None |
| 2b | Looks for "Buy Credits" or "Pricing" | Must find nav link "Pricing" (currently position 4 of 4) | **Medium** -- not immediately obvious |
| 2c | Clicks Pricing | Lands on `/pricing/` with philosophy text and 8 cards | **Medium** -- lots of content to scan |
| 2d | Scrolls past philosophy section | Finds "SG/Send Cloud" section with 4 cards | **Medium** -- scroll distance varies by device |
| 2e | Finds "Multi-Tenant" card with "Buy Credits" | Clicks "Buy Credits" | **Low** -- if they found the card |
| 2f | Lands on `/pricing/cloud/` | Sees "What's Included" section | **Medium** -- another page to navigate |
| 2g | Scrolls to find "Buy Credits -- GBP 5" button | Clicks the Stripe button | **Medium** -- below the fold |
| 2h | Stripe checkout opens | Enters payment details | **Low** -- Stripe is familiar and trusted |
| 2i | Completes payment | Redirected to `/payment/success/` | None |

**Total friction (current site): 4 clicks, 3-4 scrolls, 4 page loads.** This matches the analysis in the page redesign review. For a Challenge participant who was told to "find the buy button," steps 2b through 2g are where they will get lost. The brief itself acknowledges this: "If They Fail: CTA is buried or confusing."

**With the proposed redesign** (from the page redesign proposal -- PROPOSED, not yet implemented): The path reduces to 1 click, 0 scrolls, 1 page load. This is critical for Challenge success.

**Friction point C3: The Challenge CANNOT launch until the homepage redesign ships.** The brief says "Find the buy button" as step 2. On the current site, finding the buy button requires navigating through 4 pages. The Challenge's usability-test framing ("can you find the buy button?") will produce data, but most of that data will tell us what we already know: the current buy path has too many steps. The Challenge should launch after the redesign, not before.

#### Step 3: Use credits to send an encrypted file

This is where the journey crosses from sgraph.ai (marketing site) to send.sgraph.ai (the product). The user must:

| Sub-step | User action | What happens | Friction |
|----------|------------|-------------|----------|
| 3a | Wait for token to arrive (email) | Dead period -- minutes to hours | **Critical -- highest friction in the entire Challenge** |
| 3b | Receive email with token | Must understand what a "token" is and what to do with it | **Medium** -- new concept |
| 3c | Go to send.sgraph.ai | Token gate appears | **Low** -- if instructions are clear |
| 3d | Enter token | Access granted | **Low** -- single input field |
| 3e | Choose a file to upload | Upload page with drag-and-drop | **Low** -- standard UX |
| 3f | Click "Encrypt & Send" | File encrypted in browser, uploaded, link generated | **Low** -- but user may not understand what happened |
| 3g | Copy the share link | Link includes decryption key in URL hash fragment | **Low** -- copy button |
| 3h | Send the link to a recipient (email, WhatsApp, etc.) | Manual step -- user must choose a channel and a recipient | **Medium** -- "send it to someone" is vague |

**Friction point C4: Step 3a is the Challenge-killer.** The brief's flow assumes continuity: buy credits -> send a file. But there is a gap between payment and receiving the token. If the user completes Stripe checkout at 2:00 PM and receives their token at 6:00 PM, the Challenge's momentum is gone. They have moved on to other tasks and may never return.

**Friction point C5: "Send an encrypted file to someone (or back to themselves)."** This is the first time the participant must make a decision that is not guided by the Challenge instructions. Who do they send it to? What file do they send? The brief suggests "or back to themselves" as an escape hatch, but sending a file to yourself requires understanding the download flow (open the link, enter the key, decrypt, download). A participant who sends a test file to themselves and then cannot figure out how to open it will conclude the Challenge is broken.

**Friction point C6: The participant needs a recipient who will cooperate.** "Get the file viewed/downloaded by the recipient" requires the recipient to click a link from an unknown product, trust it, and complete the decryption process. If the participant sends the link to a friend who ignores it, the Challenge stalls. The "send to yourself" option avoids this, but it is a less convincing test of the product.

#### Step 4: Send confirmation via SG/Send

| Sub-step | User action | What happens | Friction |
|----------|------------|-------------|----------|
| 4a | Understand they need to send a message through SG/Send | Must read the Challenge instructions carefully | **Medium** -- may have forgotten this step |
| 4b | Create a new transfer containing a confirmation message | Use the text upload feature (not file upload) | **High** -- the user must discover text mode exists |
| 4c | Address the transfer to the Challenge organiser | How? SG/Send creates links, it does not "address" transfers to people | **High** -- the Challenge assumes SG/Send has addressable messaging, which it does not |
| 4d | Send the link to the Challenge organiser | Via WhatsApp/email, not via SG/Send itself | **Medium** -- extra step outside the product |

**Friction point C7: SG/Send does not have a messaging or inbox feature.** The Challenge says "Send a message THROUGH SG/Send confirming you completed it." SG/Send creates encrypted file transfers with shareable links. There is no inbox, no recipient addressing, and no messaging. The participant would need to: (a) create a transfer with a text message, (b) get the share link, (c) send that link to the organiser via WhatsApp or email. This is not "sending through SG/Send" -- it is creating a transfer and then sharing the link through another channel. The brief's framing implies a capability that does not exist.

**Friction point C8: The participant has already completed 9-10 actions by this point.** By the time they reach step 4, they have: received the WhatsApp message, navigated to sgraph.ai, found pricing, completed Stripe checkout, waited for a token, entered the token, uploaded a file, shared the link, had the recipient open it, and now need to send a confirmation. The "10 minutes" estimate in the brief is unrealistic. Conservatively: 2-3 minutes for navigation and payment, 30 minutes to several hours waiting for the token, 5-10 minutes for the first file transfer, 5-30 minutes waiting for the recipient to open it, and 5 minutes for the confirmation. The total elapsed time is measured in hours, not minutes.

#### Step 5: Receive the Amazon voucher

| Sub-step | User action | What happens | Friction |
|----------|------------|-------------|----------|
| 5a | Organiser receives confirmation | Manual process -- organiser checks SG/Send link | **Depends on organiser availability** |
| 5b | Organiser creates SG/Send transfer with voucher | Manual process | **Depends on organiser availability** |
| 5c | Organiser sends the SG/Send link to participant | Via WhatsApp/email | **Depends on organiser availability** |
| 5d | Participant opens the link, decrypts, activates voucher | Participant must use the download flow | **Low** -- they have done this before (step 3) |

**Friction point C9: Triple manual dependency.** The voucher delivery depends on: (a) the participant completing all steps, (b) the organiser noticing and verifying, and (c) the organiser creating and sending the voucher. At 50-100 participants, managing this manually with a spreadsheet is time-consuming. If 30 participants complete the Challenge on the same day, that is 30 manual voucher deliveries.

### Support Burden Prediction

| Question | Likelihood | When in the flow | Predicted volume (per 50 participants) |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|---------------------------------------|
| "I can't find the buy button" | **High** (before redesign) / **Low** (after redesign) | Step 2 | 15-25 (before) / 2-5 (after) |
| "I paid but don't have a token yet" | **Very high** | Step 3a | 25-40 |
| "What is a token? How do I use it?" | **High** | Step 3b-3d | 15-25 |
| "I sent a file -- how does the other person open it?" | **Medium** | Step 3h | 10-15 |
| "How do I send the confirmation back through SG/Send?" | **High** | Step 4 | 20-30 |

**Total predicted support volume: 100-170 emails/messages for 50 participants.** That is 2-3 support interactions per participant. At 100 participants, this doubles. Sherpa cannot handle this volume manually without dedicated response time.

### Recommendations

**R6 (P0): Do not launch the Challenge until the homepage redesign ships.** The brief's value proposition is "can you find the buy button?" On the current site, this is a frustrating scavenger hunt, not an engaging challenge. After the redesign, it becomes a single-click action, and the Challenge can focus on the actual product experience.

**R7 (P0): Solve the token delivery delay before launching.** The Challenge's flow breaks at the token delivery gap. Either automate token creation (see R1) or provide a mechanism for instant token delivery (token displayed on the payment success page, or emailed within 5 minutes).

**R8 (P1): Rewrite Challenge step 4 to match what the product actually does.** Instead of "Send a message THROUGH SG/Send confirming you completed it," say: "Create a new encrypted transfer on send.sgraph.ai with a text message saying 'Challenge complete!' and send me the link via WhatsApp." This is honest about how the product works and does not imply inbox/messaging features that do not exist.

**R9 (P1): Create a Challenge-specific getting-started guide.** A dedicated page at `sgraph.ai/challenge/` or `send.sgraph.ai/challenge/` with step-by-step instructions, screenshots, and a FAQ for common Challenge questions. This reduces support burden by providing self-service answers.

**R10 (P1): Revise the "10 minutes" time estimate.** The honest time commitment is: 5 minutes for payment (immediate), then a waiting period for token delivery, then 10-15 minutes for the first send-and-receive cycle. Tell participants: "The payment takes 5 minutes. You'll receive your token by email (usually within [X]). Once you have it, sending your first encrypted file takes about 10 minutes." Set expectations honestly.

**R11 (P2): Consider a "sandbox" mode for the Challenge.** A pre-provisioned demo token that Challenge participants can use immediately after payment, valid for 5 transfers. This eliminates the token wait entirely and lets the user start immediately. The real token (500 files) arrives later by email. This is more infrastructure, but it solves the single biggest friction point.

Neither provides immediate access. For a user choosing between free and paid, the time difference is: 48 hours vs. "a few hours." The paid path is faster but still not instant. This comparison does not make a compelling case for paying -- the user saves maybe 44 hours for GBP 5. The compelling case would be: free = 48 hours, paid = instant. That requires automated token provisioning on payment.

#### Journey C: "I already have a token"

| Step | User action | What they see | Friction |

**Friction point W4: The "Already have a token?" link opens in the same tab.** This was flagged in the page redesign review (observation 9). If the user realises they do not have a token after clicking, they must navigate back. The link should use `target="_blank"`.

### Content Strategy: User Impact

The brief proposes a news section, docs site, and tiered translation strategy. From the Sherpa's perspective:

**The news section serves trust.** A user evaluating SG/Send for the first time wants to know this is an active project, not abandonware. A single launch announcement ("v0.10.1: sgraph.ai goes multilingual") gives the site a pulse. Without it, the site feels static.

**The docs site is needed but not urgent for the Challenge.** Challenge participants will have step-by-step instructions in the WhatsApp message. A docs site would help, but its absence will not block the Challenge. It will, however, generate support emails from users who want to understand how encryption works before trusting the product.

**The tiered translation strategy is pragmatic.** Translating only core pages (home, pricing, product, about) into all 17 variants, with news and docs in English only, is the right trade-off. The Sherpa's concern is: when a user clicks from a Portuguese pricing page to an English-only blog post, the language switch needs to be signposted. A sudden language change without explanation feels like a bug.

### Stripe Integration: Last-Mile Issues

The brief identifies the critical remaining blocker: "Link this URL from the sgraph.ai pricing page 'Buy Credits' button." This is the single line of HTML that connects the website to revenue. The Sherpa confirms this is accurate -- the Stripe link currently exists only on `/pricing/cloud/index.html`. The homepage and main pricing page do not link to Stripe.

**Friction point W5: After-payment redirect configuration.** The brief says to set up `sgraph.ai/payment/success/`. This page exists (verified at `/en-gb/payment/success/index.html`), but the Stripe Payment Link must be configured to redirect to it. If this redirect is not set, the user will see Stripe's generic confirmation page and may not know what to do next. This is a Stripe dashboard configuration, not a code change, but it is a blocker.

**Friction point W6: No privacy policy or terms page exists.** The brief says to "add sgraph.ai privacy policy and terms links in Stripe checkout settings." The Sherpa notes that Stripe requires valid URLs. If `/privacy/` and `/terms/` pages do not exist on sgraph.ai, these links will 404. The pages need to exist before they can be linked from Stripe.

### Recommendations

**R13 (P0): Fix pricing table CSS for non-English languages before multilingual outreach.** If the Challenge targets Spanish, Portuguese, and German contacts, those pricing pages must render correctly. Broken tables in the target language invalidate the multilingual value proposition.

**R14 (P0): Remove unsourced competitor pricing claims immediately.** Every content inaccuracy undermines trust. A zero-knowledge encryption product that makes unverifiable claims about competitors is contradicting its own transparency values.

**R15 (P1): Create privacy policy and terms pages.** These are needed for Stripe checkout compliance and for user trust. Even minimal versions are better than 404s.

**R16 (P1): Configure Stripe Payment Link to redirect to `/en-gb/payment/success/`.** Verify this works across all 17 locale variants -- the redirect URL should match the user's locale. If Stripe only supports a single redirect URL, use `/en-gb/payment/success/` as the default and add locale detection on the success page.

**R17 (P2): Add language transition indicators.** When a user navigates from a translated page to an English-only page (docs, blog), show a clear indicator: "This page is available in English only." This prevents confusion and manages expectations.

## Summary

The three briefs describe a coherent pipeline from website to payment to outreach. The ambition is correct: drive real users through the product, learn from their friction, and build a repeatable acquisition flow. The multilingual advantage is real and differentiating.

The primary risk is launching before the infrastructure is ready. The Challenge brief describes a fluid journey that assumes capabilities (instant token delivery, messaging via SG/Send, "10 minutes" end-to-end) that do not match the current product reality. Launching with these gaps will produce frustrated participants, high support burden, and data that reflects infrastructure problems rather than product-market fit.

The single highest-impact intervention is automated token provisioning. If a user pays GBP 5 and receives their token within 5 minutes, the Challenge becomes viable. If they pay and wait hours, the Challenge fails regardless of how good the website is.

The Sherpa stands ready to handle incoming questions from Challenge participants. Every question logged is a free usability insight. But the Sherpa's strong recommendation is: close the known gaps first, then launch. The friction points documented here are predictable and preventable. Let the Challenge reveal the friction we cannot predict.
