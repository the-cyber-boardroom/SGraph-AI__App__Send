## 1. User Journey Audit

### Journey 1 — Recipient (Priority #1)

**Context:** Someone received a share link. They land on sgraph.ai — likely for the first time. Their primary question is: "Is this safe to open?"

**What works today:**
- Friendly token format (`oral-equal-1234`) reduces perceived phishing risk vs. a random UUID
- "We cannot decrypt your files" is architecturally accurate and differentiating
- Zero-cookie stance removes a trust barrier immediately

**What fails today:**
- No prominent "what is this?" explanation above the fold for first-time recipients
- Trust must be established before the user will click anything — but the current site buries the security explanation
- No social proof for recipients ("10,000 files shared this week" or similar ambient trust signal)
- The path from "received file" to "I now want to send files myself" is not designed — it's accidental if it happens at all

**What to fix:**
- Add a lightweight "receiving a file?" explainer on the receipt page that explains the encryption model in one sentence before the file is decrypted
- After successful file receipt, show: "You just received a zero-knowledge encrypted file. Want to send one?" with a one-click CTA

---

### Journey 2 — Evaluator (Org Research)

**Context:** A security-conscious buyer, IT manager, or procurement lead is evaluating SGraph Send for team use. They will read every word on /security/ and look for red flags.

**What works today:**
- AES-256-GCM, Web Crypto API, no server-side key — these are credible technical claims
- "Cannot" framing is precise and legally meaningful
- 17 locales signals serious intent

**What fails today:**
- /architecture/ page mixed with marketing is unconvincing — evaluators want raw documentation, not a sales pitch about architecture
- No audit trail mention visible on the public site (even though audit trails exist in v0.3.0)
- No FAQ section addressing "what happens if your servers are subpoenaed?" or "can you be compelled to hand over files?"
- No SOC2 / compliance status (even if the answer is "planned")

**What to fix:**
- Move deep architecture docs to qa.send.sgraph.ai (as planned)
- On /security/, answer the three evaluator questions directly: subpoena resistance, breach exposure, retention controls
- Add a "For Security Teams" section with a downloadable one-pager (or link to /security/ PDF export via SgPrint)

---

### Journey 3 — Investor (30-Second Pitch)

**Context:** Investor lands on send.sgraph.ai. They have 30 seconds. They want: problem, solution, moat, traction.

**What works today:**
- The privacy policy contrast ("6 sentences vs 4,000 words") is memorable and press-ready
- "Cannot not will not" is a genuine moat signal — it implies the architecture is the differentiator, not policy

**What fails today:**
- Homepage does not lead with the problem statement — investors need "X billion files are shared insecurely every day" before they care about the solution
- No traction numbers visible (even directional: "X files shared", "X countries")
- The investor pitch is buried inside product UX rather than surfaced on a dedicated path

**What to fix:**
- Add a brief problem framing sentence above the hero tagline
- Surface one traction metric (even modest) in the hero section — this grounds the pitch
- Create a clear path: hero → /why/ → /pricing/ → contact, that works as an investor tour without requiring them to use the product

## 2. Voice and Tone

### Principle 1 — Precise over Vague

The product makes architectural guarantees. The copy must match.

| Current/Generic | Proposed |
|---|---|
| "Your files are protected" | "Your files are encrypted before they leave your browser" |
| "We take privacy seriously" | "We cannot read your files. This is not a policy — it is an architecture." |
| "Secure file sharing" | "Zero-knowledge file sharing" |

### Principle 2 — "Cannot" not "Will Not"

Every privacy promise on the site must use the architectural framing. "Will not" implies choice. "Cannot" implies design.

| Do not write | Write instead |
|---|---|
| "We will not share your data" | "We cannot share your data — we do not have it in a readable form" |
| "We won't read your files" | "We cannot read your files — the key never reaches our server" |

### Principle 3 — Respect for Reader Intelligence

Evaluators and power users will reject oversimplification. Recipients need clarity but not condescension.

- Write for an intelligent non-expert: no jargon without a one-clause definition
- Never write "military-grade encryption" — write "AES-256-GCM, the same standard used by Signal and iMessage"
- Avoid exclamation marks in security copy — they undermine credibility

### Principle 4 — Short is a Feature, Not a Constraint

The six-sentence privacy policy is not a limitation — it is a product differentiator. Apply the same principle to all copy.

- Homepage hero: headline + one sub-sentence + CTA (no paragraph)
- Feature descriptions: one sentence each, with optional expand
- /security/ page: every claim in one sentence, then a "learn more" link to technical docs

## 3. Trust Signals

### What Exists Today (v0.3.0)

- AES-256-GCM encryption (client-side, Web Crypto API)
- Zero cookies, no tracking pixels, no analytics on product
- Hashed IP only (SHA-256 with daily salt)
- Friendly tokens (reduces phishing perception)
- Retention controls (sender-chosen deletion)
- Transparency panel (shows what data is stored)
- Receipt confirmation

### What Is Missing (and Needed)

| Signal | Priority | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Subpoena resistance statement | High | "Even under legal compulsion, we cannot produce plaintext" |
| Open-source or auditable code | High | Even a link to the public repo builds trust |
| Breach exposure statement | High | "In a breach, attackers get encrypted ciphertext — unreadable without the key" |
| Traction signal | Medium | File count, country count, or user count (directional is fine) |
| Security FAQ | Medium | 5 questions evaluators always ask |
| Last audit date | Medium | Even "last reviewed March 2026" signals maintenance |
| SOC2 status | Low (now) | "Planned for v1.0" is acceptable |

### What to Add to Homepage

1. The six-sentence privacy policy — visible on the page, not linked
2. A one-line breach statement: "If our servers were breached tomorrow, attackers would find encrypted ciphertext. Useless without your key."
3. A traction counter (even if small) — signals real usage

## 4. Content to Remove

### /agents/ Page

**Recommendation: Remove entirely.**

The agents concept is either a proposed feature or an internal architecture concern. It does not map to any of the four audiences. Evaluators reading this page will expect agent-based integrations that may not exist. Investors will misread it as a product commitment. Remove it and redirect to the homepage.

### /architecture/ Page (Public Site)

**Recommendation: Move to qa.send.sgraph.ai**

Architecture documentation belongs in the technical evaluation environment, not on the marketing site. On the public site, replace /architecture/ with a two-paragraph summary on /security/ that links to the technical docs.

Exception: Keep a high-level "how it works" diagram (three steps: encrypt in browser → upload ciphertext → decrypt in browser) on /security/ for evaluators who want a visual anchor.

### Other Content to Review

| Content | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Any copy using "will not" for privacy promises | Replace with "cannot" framing |
| Any paragraph longer than 4 sentences on the homepage | Cut or move to a secondary page |
| Any use of "military-grade" | Replace with specific cipher name |
| Cookie/consent banners (if present) | Remove — zero cookies means no banner needed |
| Generic "contact us" forms | Replace with a specific prompt: "Evaluating for your organisation? Tell us your use case." |

## 5. New User Journeys (Redesign)

### Journey A — Recipient to Sender

**Trigger:** User successfully decrypts a received file.

**Current state:** Dead end. User decrypts, downloads, leaves.

**Redesigned flow:**
1. File decrypted successfully → show confirmation screen
2. Confirmation screen includes: "You just received a zero-knowledge encrypted file. Nothing was stored on our server in a readable form."
3. Below the confirmation: "Want to send something back securely?" → "Try it — no signup required" → lands on upload wizard
4. After their first send: "Share this link with your recipient" — closes the loop

**Goal metric:** Percentage of recipients who become senders within the same session

---

### Journey B — Evaluator to Trial

**Trigger:** Evaluator arrives via search ("zero knowledge file sharing" or "secure file transfer for enterprise").

**Redesigned flow:**
1. Land on /security/ (optimised for this query)
2. /security/ answers: what is stored, what we cannot see, what happens under legal compulsion, what happens in a breach
3. Prominent CTA: "See it working — send a test file in 60 seconds, no account needed"
4. After trial: "Want to evaluate for your organisation?" → contact form with specific fields (org size, use case, compliance requirements)
5. Optional: download a security one-pager (generated via SgPrint from /security/ content)

---

### Journey C — Power User to Workflow Integration

**Trigger:** Power user arrives from a developer forum, Hacker News, or referral.

**Redesigned flow:**
1. Land on homepage → immediately see "No signup. No tracking. Open in a browser tab."
2. Click through to /features/ — sees gallery view, folder browser, SgPrint, data rooms, vaults
3. CTA shifts to: "Set up a personal vault" or "Share a folder" (workflow-specific)
4. After first complex use: "Bookmark send.sgraph.ai — your vault is always here"

**Solution:** Upload a folder — it becomes a browsable gallery at a single link. Client opens it in their browser, no account, no app.

**Pain:** "I need to send a contract, medical record, or financial document. Email is not encrypted end-to-end. I don't know where it ends up."

**Solution:** File is encrypted in your browser before upload. We cannot read it. The recipient decrypts it with a key you share separately.

**Demo CTA:** "Send a document — see what the server actually receives (encrypted bytes, nothing more)"

---

### Page 3 — /why/replace-wetransfer/

**Pain:** "WeTransfer scans files and serves ads. I just want the file to arrive."

**Solution:** We do not scan files because we cannot — they arrive as encrypted ciphertext. No ads. No account. Larger file sizes.

**Demo CTA:** "Send a file — compare the experience"

**Trust callout:** "Privacy policy: 6 sentences. WeTransfer's: [word count]."

---

### Page 4 — /why/client-deliverables/

**Pain:** "I deliver work to clients — design files, reports, code. I want them to receive it professionally, not as a sketchy download link."

**Solution:** Gallery view presents files as a curated portfolio. Friendly token (`oral-equal-1234` style) replaces random strings. Optional: SgPrint cover page as a PDF brief.

**Demo CTA:** "Create a client delivery in 2 minutes"

---

### Page 5 — /why/data-room/

## 8. CTA Strategy

### Primary CTA (single, consistent across all pages)

**"Send a file — no signup required"**

- Used in: hero, top of /features/, top of /security/, bottom of every /why/ page
- Visual treatment: teal button, lightning bolt icon, white text
- Destination: upload wizard (first step of 6-step flow)

### Secondary CTA (context-dependent)

| Page | Secondary CTA |
|---|---|
| /security/ | "Download security one-pager" (SgPrint PDF) |
| /features/ | "See the gallery view demo" |
| /pricing/ | "Talk to us about team pricing" |
| /why/data-room/ | "Set up a data room in 5 minutes" |
| Post-receipt confirmation | "Send something back" |

### Tertiary CTA (low-friction, ambient)

- "Share this page" — on-brand: share the privacy-first sharing tool using the privacy-first sharing tool
- "Read our 6-sentence privacy policy" — inline on homepage, not linked
- "View on GitHub" — for evaluators and power users, signals openness

### Anti-patterns to Avoid

- Do not use "Get started for free" — it implies a paid barrier, which undercuts the "no signup" message
- Do not use "Learn more" as a standalone CTA — too vague; replace with the specific action ("See how encryption works")
- Do not gate any demo behind an email form — the first experience must be zero-friction

## 10. i18n Considerations

### Content Structure Rules for 17-Locale Support

**1. Avoid idioms in primary copy.**
"Your keys, your privacy" translates cleanly. "No funny business" does not. Every headline and CTA should be reviewed for idiom density before translation.

**2. The six-sentence privacy policy is a translation unit.**
Each sentence should be translated as a complete unit, not word-for-word. Translators must preserve the "cannot" framing — in some languages, the distinction between "cannot" and "will not" requires a specific verb form. Flag this explicitly in the translation brief.

**3. Friendly tokens are language-neutral by default.**
`oral-equal-1234` uses English adjectives and nouns — these will be opaque to non-English recipients. Options:
- Option A: Keep English tokens globally (they function as opaque identifiers, not meaningful phrases)
- Option B: Generate locale-specific tokens for the sender's locale (adds complexity)
- Recommendation: Keep English tokens for v0.17.x; document as a known limitation to revisit at v1.0

**4. RTL layout is a layout concern, not a content concern.**
Arabic and Hebrew require RTL layout. The three-step "how it works" section must flow right-to-left in RTL locales. Flag for the IFD designer — this is a CSS and Web Component concern, not a copy concern.

**5. Legal copy ("cannot", "never") must be reviewed by locale.**
In some jurisdictions, absolute claims ("we can never...") carry legal weight. The six-sentence privacy policy should be reviewed by a local legal contact before going live in each new locale. For v0.17.x, ship with a caveat in the translation notes.

**6. CTA button text length varies by language.**
"Send a file — no signup required" is 34 characters in English. German equivalents can run 60+. Design the CTA button with a max-width constraint and allow text to wrap on one additional line in verbose locales.

**7. The "6 sentences vs 4,000 words" contrast needs locale-checking.**
Some privacy policies in EU locales are legally required to be verbose. The contrast may be less striking — or legally questionable — in those markets. Adjust the contrast copy per locale or use a neutral framing: "Six sentences. Complete."
