## 1. LinkedIn Post Analysis

### The Post

> "Anybody here speaks Klingon? The latest MVP of the SGraph Send service is now available in: English, Portugues (Brasil), Portugues (Portugal), tlhIngan Hol"

**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:ugcPost:7428147910451638272/

The post includes screenshots of the product UI in all four languages, with the flag-based language selector visible.

### What Is Working

**The Klingon hook is excellent.** It does three things at once:

1. **Stops the scroll.** LinkedIn feeds are a wall of corporate announcements and self-congratulatory updates. A post about Klingon translation breaks the pattern. People who would never stop for "we launched multilingual support" will stop for "we translated our product into Klingon."

2. **Demonstrates technical capability without explaining it.** The implicit message is: our i18n architecture is robust enough to handle a constructed language with a non-Latin script designation (`tlh`). If it can handle Klingon, it can handle any language. This communicates architectural maturity to technical audiences without a single line of technical explanation.

3. **Creates a sharing trigger.** People share content that makes them look interesting to their network. Sharing a post about Klingon makes the sharer look culturally aware and technically curious. This is the kind of content that crosses network boundaries -- it reaches people outside the security/privacy bubble.

**The visual proof matters.** Screenshots of the actual product in four languages are stronger than any claim. They show a real, working product -- not a mockup, not a roadmap slide, not a "coming soon." The language selector with flags is immediately legible. Anyone scrolling past can see that the product works in multiple languages without reading the caption.

**The multilingual angle signals international ambition.** Two variants of Portuguese (Brasil and Portugal) is a subtle but important detail. It tells Portuguese-speaking audiences that SGraph Send respects dialectal differences, not just language boundaries. For communities in Brazil and Portugal, this is a signal of care that most products never send.

**The tone is right.** Conversational, playful, and confident. Not a press release. Not marketing speak. It reads like someone who built something interesting and wants to show it off. That authenticity is what performs on LinkedIn.

### What Could Be Stronger

**No link to the product.** The post does not include a URL to `send.sgraph.ai`. Anyone intrigued has no way to try the product without searching for it. Every LinkedIn post about the product should include the URL, even if it is repeated in the comments.

**No call to action.** The post ends with the language list. It could end with an invitation: "Try it yourself" or "Which language should we add next?" A question generates comments. Comments boost visibility. Visibility drives awareness.

**No mention of what the product does.** A viewer who has never heard of SGraph Send sees a multilingual product but does not know what it does. One sentence -- "SGraph Send is zero-knowledge encrypted file sharing" -- would anchor the post for new audiences.

**The screenshots are static.** A 15-second screen recording showing the language switch in real time would be more engaging than four screenshots. Video content receives significantly higher engagement on LinkedIn than image posts.

### Recommendations for Future Posts

1. Always include the product URL: `send.sgraph.ai`
2. Always include one sentence explaining what the product is
3. End with a question or call to action to generate comments
4. Consider video (screen recordings) for UI-focused posts
5. Tag relevant communities or individuals when appropriate (Klingon language communities, i18n practitioners, etc.)

This is the strongest narrative we have right now. The decision to remove Google Analytics (D024) and adopt a provable no-cookie policy (D025) is not just a technical choice -- it is a philosophical position that resonates with every person who has ever been annoyed by a cookie consent banner. That is, effectively, everyone on the internet.

**LinkedIn Post Variation A -- The Absence as Feature:**

> We removed Google Analytics from SGraph Send.
>
> Not because we found something better. Because we found something we did not need.
>
> Our site now uses zero cookies. No tracking scripts. No consent banner. No "we value your privacy" popup.
>
> Open your browser's developer tools on send.sgraph.ai. Check the cookies tab. It is empty.
>
> That is not a limitation. It is a design choice.
>
> #privacy #infosec #webdev #zerocookies

**LinkedIn Post Variation B -- The Counter-Intuitive Story:**

**LinkedIn Post Draft:**

> 1. Never override existing HTML/UI
> 2. Minor versions extend, not rebuild

### 2.6 Building on Engagement

The Klingon post creates an opening for several follow-up strategies:

1. **Reply to every comment.** Each reply boosts the post's visibility and signals that the poster is engaged. If someone asks about Klingon, share a detail about the translation process. If someone asks about the product, explain zero-knowledge encryption in one sentence.

2. **Ask "which language next?"** in a follow-up comment on the original post. This generates additional engagement and provides genuine market intelligence about which communities to prioritise.

3. **Cross-post the language story to relevant communities.** The Klingon angle works in developer communities (Hacker News, Reddit r/programming), Star Trek communities, and i18n/l10n professional groups. Each community receives a version of the post tailored to their interests.

4. **Track engagement metrics.** Even without Google Analytics on the product site, LinkedIn's native analytics shows impressions, clicks, and engagement. Record these as a baseline for measuring future post performance.

5. **Convert attention to trial.** Anyone who engages with the post is a candidate for a direct message: "Thanks for the comment! Would you like to try SGraph Send? Here's a link: send.sgraph.ai". This is the Ambassador's domain, but the Journalist should provide the messaging.

### Copy Guidance for Translators

When translating these keys into Portuguese (both variants) and Klingon:

1. **Keep sentences short.** The English versions are deliberately terse. Translations should match this brevity rather than expanding for grammatical completeness.

2. **"Zero-knowledge" should not be translated literally** in Portuguese. Use "criptografia de conhecimento zero" (pt-BR) or "cifra de conhecimento zero" (pt-PT) -- the established terms in the Portuguese-language security community.

3. **For Klingon**, the translation should follow the same approach as the existing `tlh.json` -- functional translations that capture meaning rather than word-for-word transliteration. The Klingon translations are primarily a demonstration of the i18n architecture's flexibility, but they should still make sense to a Klingon speaker.

4. **The "no cookies" badge** is a factual claim. It should be translated as a statement of fact, not as marketing. In Portuguese: "Este site nao usa cookies." (pt-BR) / "Este site nao utiliza cookies." (pt-PT).

## 4. Key Messages for External Communications

The following five messages should be the foundation of all external communications for the next phase. Each message is factual, verifiable, and differentiated.

### Message 1: "Your files are encrypted before they leave your browser."

**Why this message:** It is the core value proposition stated as a verifiable fact. It answers "why should I use this?" in one sentence. It differentiates SGraph Send from every file-sharing service where the server can read the uploaded content.

**Where to use it:** Landing page hero, LinkedIn bio, email signatures, press materials, any first-contact communication.

**Proof point:** The encryption happens via Web Crypto API (AES-256-GCM) in the browser. The decryption key is generated client-side and never sent to the server. This is auditable in the browser's developer tools.

### Message 2: "This site uses no cookies."

**Why this message:** It is the most surprising privacy claim a website can make. It creates cognitive dissonance -- every other website uses cookies, so the absence is notable. It is immediately verifiable by anyone with a browser.

**Where to use it:** Footer badge, privacy page, LinkedIn posts, any privacy-focused communication.

**Proof point:** No first-party cookies, no third-party cookies, no session cookies, no persistent cookies. Google Analytics has been removed. Server-side analytics only. Verifiable via browser developer tools.

### Message 3: "We replaced Google Analytics with server-side analytics. We do not track individual users."

**Why this message:** It tells the story of a deliberate choice. Most companies add Google Analytics without thinking. SGraph Send added it, realised it contradicted the product's values, and removed it. The replacement (server-side request counting with hashed IPs) is technically superior for a privacy product.

**Where to use it:** Blog posts, technical audiences, developer communities, privacy-focused press.

**Proof point:** No client-side analytics scripts. IP addresses hashed with daily rotating salt (irreversible). No third-party data sharing.

### Message 4: "No account needed. No app to install. Just a link."

**Why this message:** It communicates zero friction. The recipient does not need to create an account, download software, or learn a new tool. They click a link and the file appears. This is the "send a file" utility message from the original brief -- lead with utility, support with security.

**Where to use it:** Email sharing templates, download page, onboarding flow, any communication to recipients (not senders).

**Proof point:** The hash-fragment URL (v0.1.1) contains the transfer ID and decryption key. One click to access. Auto-decrypt is implemented.

### Message 5: "Available in 4 languages -- including Klingon."

**Why this message:** It is memorable, shareable, and demonstrates capability through humour. The Klingon angle gets attention. The two Portuguese variants (Brasil + Portugal) demonstrate real international readiness. Together they signal that SGraph Send is built for a global audience from day one.

**Where to use it:** Social media, community outreach, conference talks, any situation where memorability matters.

**Proof point:** Four locale files live in production. Language selector in the UI. 80+ strings translated per language. The i18n architecture supports adding any language without touching UI code.

### Message Priority Matrix

| Priority | Message | Primary Audience | Primary Channel |
|----------|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1 | Files encrypted before leaving browser | All audiences | Landing page, LinkedIn |
| 2 | Zero cookies | Privacy-conscious users, developers | Footer badge, blog, LinkedIn |
| 3 | Replaced GA with server-side analytics | Developers, technical evaluators | Blog, dev communities |
| 4 | No account, no app, just a link | Recipients, casual senders | Email templates, download page |
