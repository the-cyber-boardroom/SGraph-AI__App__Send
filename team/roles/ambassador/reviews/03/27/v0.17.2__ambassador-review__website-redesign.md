# Ambassador Review — Website Redesign
**v0.17.2 | 27 March 2026**

---

## 1. Current Positioning Audit

**Implied promise:** "Send files securely. Simple."

**Implied customer:** Tech-adjacent professional who already knows what end-to-end encryption means and is vaguely worried about privacy.

**What's off:**

The current hero — *"Send files. Nobody can read them. Not even us."* — is excellent. Punchy, credible, clear. But it stops there. It does not answer the second question in every recipient's mind: *"Okay, so I received something encrypted — now what? How do I actually use this?"*

The site treats security as the only message. It isn't. SG/Send has a second extraordinary claim that competitors cannot make: **recipients can browse, view, and interact with content without downloading it.** That claim is invisible on the current site. The viewer/gallery/folder experience is buried, treating SG/Send like a Dropbox alternative when it's actually a different product category.

Three other problems:

1. **No emotional landing.** "Nobody can read them" is a negative frame — what you prevent, not what you give. The emotional payload for recipients is *confidence*, for senders it is *control*. Neither is named.
2. **"AI-Built" is prominent but unresolved.** Is that a proof point or a warning? Right now it reads as neither — it's just information. The site needs a clear stance (see Section 4).
3. **The pay-per-use model is undersold.** For a privacy-first product, "no account, no subscription, £0.01 per file" is a trust signal as much as a pricing model. It tells recipients: *this company has no incentive to harvest your data because it doesn't run on your data.*

## 2. Competitive Gap Analysis

**Genuine advantages SG/Send holds:**

| Advantage | Why it matters | Can WeTransfer say it? | Can Proton say it? |
|---|---|---|---|
| True client-side ZK encryption | Key never leaves sender's device — architectural, not policy | No | No (server-side ZK is an oxymoron) |
| In-browser content viewing | Recipients browse galleries, folders, PDFs without downloading | No | No |
| Friendly token URLs | Human-readable, shareable, memorable | No | No |
| Zero cookies, verifiable | Users can inspect — no dark-pattern consent required | No | No |
| No account ever | Frictionless for both sender and recipient | No (requires account) | No (requires account) |
| Pay-per-use | No data-harvesting business model incentive | No (subscription) | No (subscription) |
| "Cannot" not "will not" | Architectural impossibility — not a policy promise | No | No |

**Weaknesses to acknowledge honestly:**

- No mobile app yet (download fallback on mobile)
- No persistent storage / file history for senders (by design, but users may not expect it)
- Unknown brand — trust starts at zero with every new visitor

**The #1 message no competitor can say:**

> *"We cannot read your files — and you never have to download them to view them."*

WeTransfer cannot say the first clause. Proton cannot say the second. Nobody else can say both. This is the north-star claim.

**The WeTransfer AI training controversy** is an active positioning window. WeTransfer's terms allowed using uploaded content to train AI models. That controversy is live. SG/Send's architectural impossibility makes it the only credible counter-narrative: not a policy change, not a pledge — an impossibility. Press and privacy advocates will pick this up if it's framed correctly.

## 3. Primary Audience Ranking

**Rank 1: The Recipient (70% of homepage real estate)**

Every recipient arrives with one question: *"Can I trust this link someone just sent me?"* If SG/Send fails to answer that in 5 seconds, they close the tab. The recipient is not just a user — they are the next sender. Converting a recipient who trusted the experience is the single highest-ROI outcome on the homepage.

What recipients need to see immediately:
- Evidence the file is safe to open
- Explanation of what will happen (no download required)
- Social/institutional signals that this is legitimate

**Rank 2: The Evaluator (20% of homepage real estate)**

Organisation-level research buyers — legal teams, creative agencies, healthcare ops — who have been sent a link and are now researching whether to adopt SG/Send for their whole team. They need the competitive table, the technical credibility signals, and the use-case pages.

**Rank 3: Investor (5%)**

The investor is not a homepage audience. They need a dedicated `/investors` or `/why` page, or a one-page PDF. Investor language on a consumer homepage creates distrust.

**Rank 4: Power User (5%)**

Power users self-identify and find the tools. They do not need prominent real estate. Link to `/tools` from the footer.

## 4. The "AI-Built" Story — Clear Position

**Recommendation: Reframe as process transparency, not product identity.**

The current framing — *"Built Entirely by AI Agents"* — makes AI the subject of the sentence. For a security product, that is a liability. Security buyers ask: *"Who is responsible when something goes wrong?"* If the answer is "18 AI agents," trust evaporates.

The reframe is straightforward: **the "AI-built" story is a transparency story, not a tech story.**

SG/Send's ability to publish its full agent audit trail — every decision, every test, every endpoint documented — is a security *advantage*, not a curiosity. No human team documents at this granularity. No competitor can show you the full provenance of every line of their security code.

**Copy direction:**

> *"Every line of SG/Send has been written, reviewed, and documented by AI agents operating under a single human architect. That means the full audit trail is public. Read it."*

This reframes the story from "AI wrote it" (liability) to "you can inspect everything" (trust). It also weaponises the open-source + full-documentation angle.

**What to remove:** The raw stat-bar style "18 agents, 73 endpoints" on the homepage. It reads like a startup brag, not a trust signal.

**What to keep:** A dedicated `/how-it-works` or `/transparency` page with the full technical audit. Link it from the footer. Let the press and technical evaluators find it.

>

> Emotion: Control, dignity

**Option F — The Six-Sentence Guarantee**
> *"Our entire privacy policy is six sentences. Read it now."*
> *(with the six sentences displayed inline on the homepage)*
>
> Who it's for: Evaluators doing due diligence, legal/compliance buyers
> Emotion: Surprise, credibility

## 6. The One Sentence

**North-star sentence for the redesign:**

> *"The only encrypted file sharing tool where recipients can browse, view, and interact with your content — without downloading it, without an account, and without us ever being able to read it."*

This sentence is long for a hero — it belongs in the subheading, press kit, and sales one-pager. The hero headline should be shorter (see Section 5). But this sentence should govern every copy decision: if a page element does not support or lead toward this claim, it should be cut or moved.

## 7. New Use Cases — Positioning

**Portfolio views for creative professionals — give this its own page now.**

The use case is specific, emotionally resonant, and has a natural word-of-mouth loop: an artist sends their portfolio to an agent or producer. The agent sees a beautiful gallery view. The agent sends SG/Send links to their other clients. Each creative professional recommends it to their network.

The read-receipt / view-tracking angle ("has my agent watched my showreel?") is a killer feature for this audience. It deserves its own landing page at `/portfolio` or `/creative`.

**Recommended use-case page priority:**

| Use case | Priority | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Creative portfolios (artists, actors, musicians) | **Now** | High word-of-mouth, differentiated viewer UX, emotional story |
| Healthcare (patient records, imaging) | **Now** | High-trust purchase, "cannot read" is a compliance argument |
| Legal (document exchange, evidence) | Next quarter | High value, longer sales cycle, needs case study |
| Supply chain (certificates, compliance docs) | Next quarter | B2B requires separate pitch, different buyer |
| Personal (family photos, financial docs) | Homepage supporting copy | Not a dedicated page — background social proof |

**Musicians specifically:** The showreel use case maps directly to music — sending a demo to a label, a session pack to a producer. The read-receipt question ("has my producer listened to my demo?") is emotionally identical to the actor/agent question. Bundle into `/creative`, not a separate page.

## 8. Content to Remove — Brand Rationale

**`/agents/` page — move to `/transparency`, reframe entirely**

The `/agents/` page currently reads as internal team documentation made public. It belongs on the site, but as a trust and transparency page, not a team page. Rename to `/how-it-was-built` or `/transparency`. Lead with the audit-trail angle (see Section 4). Remove the "18 agents" headline stat from the homepage.

**`/architecture/` page — keep but de-promote**

The architecture page is valuable for technical evaluators and press. Do not remove it. But it should not be in the main navigation — move to footer under "Trust & Transparency." The average recipient does not need to see the system topology to trust the link.

**Stats bar (endpoints, tests, agents) — remove from homepage**

The stats bar ("73 endpoints, 393 tests, 18 agents") is internally impressive and externally meaningless to 95% of visitors. It signals "startup in progress" rather than "trusted tool." Replace with credibility signals that map to visitor concerns: the six-sentence privacy policy, the open-source audit trail, the number of successful transfers (when that number is meaningful).

**Any copy using "will not" when "cannot" is accurate — remove immediately**

Every instance of "we will not read your files" must be replaced with "we cannot read your files." The "cannot not will not" distinction is the entire architecture advantage. Any weakening of this to a policy promise erases the brand's primary differentiator.

## 10. Social Proof and Credibility Signals

Ranked by persuasive weight for the primary audience (recipients and evaluators):

**1. The six-sentence privacy policy (displayed inline)**
The single most powerful credibility signal on the site. No visitor has to read it. The fact that they *could* read it in 30 seconds is the signal. Display all six sentences on the homepage below the fold. No link required.

**2. Open-source audit trail**
"The full build history is public. Every decision documented. Read it on GitHub." This is the "AI-built" story converted into a trust asset. Links to the repo and the agent documentation.

**3. Transfer count (when meaningful)**
"X files transferred, zero data breaches" is a credibility signal — but only when X is large enough to matter. Do not display until the number is impressive. Placeholder: "Used by individuals in 30+ countries" if geographic spread data exists.

**4. Zero cookies — verifiable**
"Open your browser's developer tools. Check for yourself." This is an extraordinary claim that invites verification. Most privacy claims are un-verifiable. This one is not. That difference is enormous.

**5. Architecture in one sentence**
"Your file is encrypted in your browser before it leaves your device. The key never reaches our servers." This is not a tagline — it is a technical fact stated plainly. Place it in the "How it works" panel, not the hero.

## 12. Messaging Hierarchy

**Top-to-bottom order for the new homepage with rationale:**

| Position | Element | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — Hero | "We cannot read your files. Not 'will not.' Cannot." + Subhead explaining viewer UX | Lead with the architectural claim. Second sentence earns the viewer differentiator. |
| 2 — Recipient panel | "Someone sent you a link? Here's what happens when you open it." | The majority of visitors are recipients. Answer their question immediately. |
| 3 — Six-sentence privacy policy | Displayed inline, all six sentences | Social proof via radical brevity. Most visitors will not read it — the fact that they could is the signal. |
| 4 — How it works | Three steps: encrypt in browser → upload ciphertext → recipient views in browser | Technical credibility without jargon. One sentence per step. Diagram optional. |
| 5 — Competitive contrast | Side-by-side table: ZK encryption, viewer, no account, zero cookies, pay-per-use | At this point the visitor wants validation. Give it to them factually. |
| 6 — Use cases | Creative portfolios, healthcare, legal — with emotional anchors not feature lists | Helps visitors self-identify. "Is this for me?" answered with real scenarios. |
| 7 — Pricing | "£0.01 per file. No account. No subscription. Pay when you send." | Pricing is a trust signal here, not just a commercial message. Place after use cases, not before. |
| 8 — Transparency panel | "Every line of code documented. Built by AI agents under human architecture." + link to audit trail | Converts the AI-built story into a trust asset. Footer-adjacent, not hero-level. |
| 9 — Footer | Privacy policy (6 sentences), Architecture, GitHub, /tools, Contact | No marketing copy in footer. Pure utility. |

**What is explicitly absent from this hierarchy:**

- Investor language or traction metrics
- Agent/team page link in main nav
- Stats bar (endpoints, tests, agents count)
- "Coming soon" or roadmap teases
- Any copy using "will not" where "cannot" is accurate
