## Ambassador Assessment: Brand, Privacy, and Market Strategy

### Executive Summary

Three briefs directly concern the Ambassador role: SPKI brand strategy, the six-sentence privacy policy, and the Portugal strategy. Together they define a coherent narrative: SPKI is the platform brand, "cannot" is the differentiator, and Portugal is the first localised market. The gallery concept adds a visual product surface that is inherently shareable.

### SPKI Brand Strategy -- Assessment

**Verdict: Strong. Proceed as described.**

The brief correctly identifies the limits of "SG/Send" as a brand:
- The slash is a technical liability (URLs, search, disambiguation)
- "Send" describes one feature, not the platform
- "SG" requires two layers of explanation

SPKI solves all three:
- Four letters, no special characters, globally consistent
- Describes the technology category (Public Key Infrastructure), not a single action
- Language-neutral -- acronyms do not need translation

The organic transition approach (no forced migration, no deadlines) is the right call. Brand transitions that are forced create confusion. Brand transitions that are organic create momentum.

**Key messaging opportunity:** "SPKI -- infrastructure you can verify, not promises you have to trust." This ties the brand directly to the privacy policy's "cannot" framing.

**i18n advantage is significant.** The brief correctly notes that English-verb brands fragment across languages. For a product targeting Portugal, this matters immediately. "SPKI" works in Portuguese without translation or explanation.

### Six-Sentence Privacy Policy -- Assessment

**Verdict: This is the most powerful marketing asset the project has produced.**

The brief is right: the average EULA is 4,000-8,000 words. Six sentences is remarkable. But the real power is not brevity -- it is verifiability.

**The "cannot" framing is the entire brand message.** Every competitor says "will not." SPKI says "cannot." One word separates a promise from a guarantee. This should be the primary messaging across all channels.

**Placement recommendations from the brief are correct:**
- Homepage: visible as text, not a link
- Every transfer/vault page: single-line summary with link
- Footer: full six sentences (short enough to fit)
- Dedicated /privacy page with technical explainer

**The comparison table is devastating.** The industry-standard-vs-ours table sells itself. This should be:
- On the /privacy page
- In investor materials (the Alchemist should integrate this)
- In every sales conversation
- In LinkedIn content

**The "verify it yourself" prompt is brilliant.** "Open your browser's dev tools. Check the network tab. Check the cookies. You will find nothing." This is a marketing message that doubles as a technical proof. No other company can make this claim.

**Social media strategy:**
- The policy fits in a single post. "Our privacy policy is 6 sentences. Here it is:" -- that is viral content.
- The comparison table is screenshot-friendly.
- The QR code idea makes it physically shareable.

**Legal companion document needed.** The brief acknowledges GDPR/ePrivacy compliance requires an appendix. This should be written in the same direct style. The appendix exists for regulators; the six sentences exist for humans.

### Portugal Strategy -- Assessment

**Verdict: Good first-market strategy. Gallery is the hook.**

The Portugal angle works because:
- The human is physically present (authentic content)
- Portuguese is the first non-English locale already supported
- The secure communication gap in Portugal is real and researchable
- A public gallery of Algarve photos is both personal content and product demo

**Gallery as entry point:** The gallery concept (gallery.sgraph.ai) is the first product surface designed to be beautiful, not just functional. This is important -- file transfer is invisible, vaults are structural, but a photo gallery is visual and shareable. Screenshots of a gallery are marketing assets.

**Public key registry is ambitious but high-value.** Scraping Portuguese public keys creates:
- A free public service (goodwill)
- A "send secure message" button per entry (user acquisition)
- Content for Portuguese-language articles
- A viral discovery mechanism ("my key is in the SPKI registry")

**Risk:** Legal/GDPR implications of indexing public keys need review before launch.

**Content plan:**
1. Algarve photo gallery (immediate, visual, shareable)
2. Portuguese article on secure communication gaps (SEO, authority)
3. Public key registry announcement (viral potential)
4. LinkedIn content in Portuguese targeting PT professionals

### Gallery UX -- Communications View

The gallery is a marketing surface as much as a product feature:
- Screenshots look good (requirement: "easy screenshot")
- Public galleries are shareable links
- The slideshow feature is demonstrable
- Client-side encryption means the privacy story applies: "even your photos are encrypted"

**Recommendation:** Every public gallery should include a small "Powered by SPKI -- your photos, encrypted" watermark or footer. The gallery itself is the marketing.

### LLM Image Generation -- Communications Angle

The user test (non-technical user, image editing, multiple rounds via SG/Send) is a case study waiting to be written. The workflow validates:
- Real users find SG/Send intuitive through repeated use
- The friction is in switching between tools (SG/Send, WhatsApp, ChatGPT)
- The solution (all-in-one interface) is a natural product extension

**"API keys encrypted with your public key"** is a powerful one-liner for security-conscious developers. The encrypt-for-reader pattern applied to API key distribution is novel and communicable.
