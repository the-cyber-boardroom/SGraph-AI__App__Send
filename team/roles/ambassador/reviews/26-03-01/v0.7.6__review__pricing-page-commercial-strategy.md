# Ambassador Review: Pricing Page Commercial Strategy

**Version:** v0.7.6
**Date:** 1 March 2026
**Role:** Ambassador
**Scope:** Commercial/conversion review of pricing page, home page, cloud detail page, and early access page
**Objective:** Maximise conversions to EAP sign-up or Starter Pack purchase (GBP 5)

---

## Executive Summary

The pricing page faithfully implements the brief's Option A layout and is structurally sound. However, it has a critical conversion problem: **the page is optimised for comprehensiveness, not conversion**. A visitor arriving at the pricing page encounters four free options before ever seeing something they can buy. The "free" section dominates the viewport. The commercial section -- where revenue comes from -- requires scrolling past a full screen of free alternatives. The page answers the question "what are all the ways to use SG/Send?" when it should be answering "why should I start using SG/Send right now?"

The home page has a related but different problem: it positions SG/Send as an AI-agent showcase first and a product second. The hero CTA sends users directly to `send.sgraph.ai` (the app) without context about tokens, pricing, or EAP -- creating a dead-end experience for anyone who does not already have an access token.

**Bottom line:** The funnel has three leaks that, if fixed, would materially increase conversion:
1. The home page does not nudge toward pricing or EAP.
2. The pricing page leads with free options, burying the revenue path.
3. The "Buy Credits" CTA on the cloud page resolves to a `mailto:` link, not a Stripe checkout.

## 2. Value Proposition Clarity

### Home Page

The home page hero reads: **"AI agents that build, use, and secure the same platform."**

This is the company narrative, not the product value proposition. A first-time visitor does not know what SG/Send does from this headline alone. The subtext explains it, but the subtext is long and buries the user benefit ("zero-knowledge encrypted file sharing") inside a sentence about AI agents.

**Problem:** The home page talks about how SG/Send is built (by AI agents) before explaining what it does for the user (encrypts their files so nobody else can read them).

**Recommendation:** The hero should lead with the user's problem, not the company's story.

Current:
> "AI agents that build, use, and secure the same platform."

Suggested:
> "Send files nobody else can read."

The AI-agent angle is compelling and differentiating but it belongs in a supporting section, not the headline. The headline must answer: "What does this do for me?"

### Product Page

The product page does this much better. "Your files, your keys, your privacy" is a strong headline. The comparison table against traditional file sharing is excellent. But the product page has no pricing CTA -- it sends users to `send.sgraph.ai` at every touchpoint. Users who are convinced by the product page have no direct path to purchase.

### Pricing Page

The pricing hero reads: **"Pricing & Deployment Options"**

This is functional but uninspiring. It describes the page content rather than reinforcing the value proposition. A visitor who lands here from an external link (e.g. a search result, a referral) gets no reminder of WHY they should care.

**Recommendation:** Add a value-reinforcing subhead before the pricing grid:

> "Zero-knowledge encryption for every file you send. From GBP 0.01 per transfer."

## 3. Pricing Psychology

### The "Free" Section Is Too Dominant

The free section occupies four cards in a four-column grid. It is the first content a visitor sees. The visual weight is overwhelming:

- Four cards, all saying "Free"
- Large "Free" and "GBP 0" price labels
- A full "Why is it free?" link at the bottom

The psychological effect: "Why would I pay when there are four free options?" The free section makes the commercial section feel like a premium upsell rather than the natural path.

**Recommendation (P1):** Restructure the page into three sections:

1. **"Get Started"** -- Two cards side by side: EAP (free trial) and Starter Pack (GBP 5). These are the two actions we want most visitors to take. Make Starter Pack the featured/highlighted card.

2. **"Run It Yourself"** -- Three cards: Open Source, Your Cloud, Partner/White Label. These are for technical evaluators and enterprises. Position them as "for developers and enterprises who want full control."

3. **"Enterprise"** -- Two cards: Dedicated and Managed. These are for procurement conversations. Position them as "for regulated industries and large organisations."

Remove AWS Marketplace to a footnote or a "Coming Soon" mention within the Enterprise section. A "Coming Soon" card in the pricing grid dilutes the available options with something visitors cannot act on.

### GBP 5 for 500 Files Positioning

The price point is excellent: GBP 0.01 per file is psychologically trivial. But the current presentation buries this insight. The card says "GBP 5" in large text and "500 file transfers, GBP 0.01/file" in small text below.

**Recommendation (P2):** Lead with the unit price, not the total:

> **GBP 0.01** per encrypted transfer
> Starter Pack: GBP 5 for 500 transfers

This reframes the purchase from "do I want to spend GBP 5?" to "a penny per file -- that is practically free." The total (GBP 5) becomes the anchor for how cheap it is rather than a barrier.

### Credit Expiry Concern

"3-month credit validity" is listed as a feature but reads as a limitation. Three months is generous for 500 files, but presenting it alongside features like "Stripe payment" makes it feel like a restriction.

**Recommendation (P3):** Reframe as: "Use at your own pace -- credits valid for 3 months." This is already done on the cloud detail page but not on the main pricing card.

## 4. Competitive Positioning

The product page has an excellent comparison table against "Traditional Sharing." However, the pricing page -- where purchasing decisions happen -- has no competitive framing at all.

A visitor evaluating pricing wants to know: "How does this compare to what I'm already using?"

**Recommendation (P2):** Add a brief competitive framing section to the pricing page, between the encryption callout and the SG/Send Cloud section:

| | WeTransfer Pro | Dropbox Business | SG/Send Cloud |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monthly cost | GBP 10/month | GBP 13.50/user/month | GBP 0 when idle |
| Encryption | Server-side | Server-side | Client-side (zero-knowledge) |
| Provider can read files | Yes | Yes | No, by design |
| Minimum commitment | Monthly subscription | Annual contract | None (pay per file) |

This table makes the case that SG/Send is not just more secure but also more economical for episodic use. The "GBP 0 when idle" point is a powerful differentiator against subscription fatigue.

## 5. Enterprise Appeal

### Dedicated Section

The Dedicated card currently says:
- "Custom" pricing
- "Contact us for pricing"
- Five feature bullet points

This is adequate but misses enterprise buying signals. Enterprise buyers want to see:
- Compliance certifications or readiness (SOC 2, ISO 27001, GDPR)
- Data residency options
- SLA guarantees
- Procurement-friendly language ("custom contract," "annual invoicing")

**Recommendation (P2):** Add to the Dedicated card features:
- "Compliance-ready isolation" (already there -- good)
- "Data residency control"
- "Annual invoicing available"
- "Custom contract terms"

### Managed Section

The Managed card is currently thin. "Your Cloud, We Manage" is a strong concept but the description does not convey enough to start a procurement conversation.

**Recommendation (P3):** Add: "Full data sovereignty -- your region, your compliance framework. We handle deployment, monitoring, and updates."

### Missing Enterprise CTA

Neither the Dedicated nor Managed sections have urgency. Enterprise buyers respond to specificity.

**Recommendation (P3):** Change "Contact Us" to "Talk to Our Team" or "Book a Discussion" -- language that implies a conversation rather than a generic contact form.

## 6. Call to Action Effectiveness

### Home Page CTAs

Current:
- Primary: "Try SG/Send" (goes to send.sgraph.ai)
- Secondary: "Meet the Team" (goes to /agents/)

**Problem:** Neither CTA leads to conversion. "Try SG/Send" sends visitors to an app they cannot use without a token. "Meet the Team" is interesting but not commercially relevant.

**Recommendation (P1):** Replace with:
- Primary: "Get Started Free" (goes to /early-access/)
- Secondary: "See Pricing" (goes to /pricing/)
- Keep "Try SG/Send" as a tertiary link in the platform section below

### Pricing Page CTAs

Current structure:
- 4 free cards with secondary CTAs
- 1 featured commercial card with primary CTA ("Buy Credits")
- 3 non-featured commercial cards with secondary CTAs

**Problem:** The primary CTA ("Buy Credits") competes with 7 other CTAs on the same page. The EAP card, which is the lowest-friction conversion, uses a secondary (less visually prominent) CTA style.

**Recommendation (P1):** Make the EAP card and the Starter Pack card both use primary CTA styling. All other cards use secondary. This creates a clear visual hierarchy: "these two are for you right now."

### Cloud Detail Page CTA

The "Buy Credits -- GBP 5" button currently resolves to:
```
mailto:sherpa@sgraph.ai?subject=Buy%20Credits%20...
```

**This is the most critical conversion leak in the entire funnel.** A user who has navigated through the home page, to the pricing page, clicked "Buy Credits," read the cloud detail page, and decided to purchase -- is then asked to compose an email. This is a massive drop-off point.

**Recommendation (P1 -- Critical):** Replace the mailto link with either:
1. A Stripe checkout link (if the integration exists)
2. A prominent note: "Stripe checkout launching soon. Email sherpa@sgraph.ai to purchase now and we will set you up within 24 hours."

The current implementation gives no indication that clicking "Buy Credits" will open an email client. This breaks user expectations.

### Product Page CTAs

The product page has three CTAs, all pointing to send.sgraph.ai. None point to pricing or EAP.

**Recommendation (P2):** In the "Pricing Philosophy" section (Section 6 of product page), change the CTA from "Try SG/Send" to "See Pricing" or add a second CTA: "GBP 5 for 500 encrypted transfers."

## 8. Home Page Optimisation

### Current State

The home page has four sections:
1. Hero (CTA: Try SG/Send, Meet the Team)
2. The Platform (feature cards + CTA: Try SG/Send)
3. Meet the Team (agent cards)
4. Built in Public (stats + CTA: GitHub, Architecture)

There is **no mention of pricing, EAP, or GBP 5** anywhere on the home page. The page tells visitors what SG/Send is and how it is built, but never tells them how to get started as a paying (or evaluating) user.

### Recommendations

**P1 -- Add pricing touchpoint to hero:**

After the existing hero CTAs, add a subtle pricing anchor:

> "Free to try. GBP 0.01 per file when you are ready."

This sets the pricing expectation before the visitor even reaches the pricing page. It also signals that SG/Send is remarkably cheap, which builds curiosity.

**P1 -- Add EAP callout section:**

Between "The Platform" and "Meet the Team" sections, add a conversion-focused section:

> **Ready to try it?**
>
> Join the Early Access Programme for free evaluation credits. No credit card required.
>
> [Join Early Access (free)] [Buy 500 transfers -- GBP 5]

This gives visitors two clear conversion paths without leaving the home page.

**P2 -- Add pricing link to footer:**

The home page footer links to send.sgraph.ai, Architecture, and Team. It does not link to Pricing. This is an oversight -- the pricing page is in the nav but not in the footer.

*Note: On review, the footer does include a Pricing link. This is correct.*

**P3 -- Reconsider hero headline:**

The current headline ("AI agents that build, use, and secure the same platform") is the company story, not the user story. Consider testing:

> "Encrypted file sharing. Zero knowledge. Zero subscriptions."

This communicates the three key differentiators (encryption, privacy, pricing model) in one line.
