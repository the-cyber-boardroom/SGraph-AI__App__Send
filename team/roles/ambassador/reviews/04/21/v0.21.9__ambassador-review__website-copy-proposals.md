## Brand Voice Rules (Non-Negotiable)

Before every copy decision, apply these filters:

- **"Cannot" not "will not"** — the server cannot read your files. This is an architectural fact, not a policy promise. Every weakening of this to "will not" erases the primary differentiator.
- **"Client-encrypted" not "zero-knowledge"** — zero-knowledge is jargon. Client-encrypted answers the buyer's implicit question ("encrypted where?") in one word.
- **"Version-controlled" leads** — it differentiates immediately. Every encrypted storage product claims "encrypted" first. We don't.
- **Short is a feature** — brevity signals confidence. Hedging, padding, and qualifications signal anxiety. Trust the architecture to do the work.
- **The hero line is locked** — "Version-controlled, client-encrypted vaults. For humans and agents." Ship it unchanged.

Three candidates drawn from the positioning brief and expanded:

> Every commit, every share, every agent: encrypted in your browser. The server is blind.

*Best for:* Technical visitors, security professionals, developers.
*Tone:* Architecturally confident, minimal.

**Candidate B — The Git-Semantics Line**
> Git semantics, browser-grade encryption, multi-agent ready. Your vault is your account.

*Best for:* Developers and AI-adjacent builders who already know what "git semantics" means. Self-selects correctly for the early audience.
*Tone:* Dense, technically specific.

**Candidate C — The Three-Verbs Line**
> Encrypt locally. Version globally. Share safely. Across humans, CLIs, and AI agents.

*Best for:* A slightly wider technical audience; the three imperative verbs create a scan rhythm. More explicit about the platform.
*Tone:* Active, instructional.

**Candidate D (New) — The Claim Line**
> Your files are encrypted before they leave your browser. The version history is yours. The server holds nothing but ciphertext.

*Best for:* Evaluators doing due diligence who want the full claim in one breath.
*Tone:* Declarative, evidence-first.

**Recommended pair for initial A/B:** Candidate A and Candidate C. A is tighter; C gives the broader platform picture. Both are factual.

## Section 5 — Six-Sentence Privacy Policy

To be displayed inline on the homepage. Must be exactly six sentences. No link required — the fact that it is readable in 30 seconds is the signal.

> 1. SG/Send cannot read your files — your browser encrypts them before upload, and the decryption key never leaves your device.

> 5. We do not sell, share, or process your personal data for any purpose beyond delivering the service.
> 6. The complete source code and build history are public on GitHub — you can verify every claim we make.

**Voice note:** Sentence 1 uses "cannot" — architectural impossibility. Sentence 6 invites verification. This is Option F from the March Ambassador review, now drafted in full.

## Copy Decision Notes for Implementation

1. **Hyphenation is mandatory:** "version-controlled" and "client-encrypted" always hyphenated. No exceptions. Check every page before deploying.

2. **"Cannot" vs "will not" audit:** Before any page goes live, do a full-text search for "will not read" and replace with "cannot read". This is not optional — it is the brand's primary differentiator.

3. **Subhead A/B testing:** Ship Candidate A as the default. Add Candidate C as the variant. Use browser-based A/B (simple JS flag or URL parameter) for the conference. Test with real visitors, not committee preference.

4. **The receive CTA is a blocker:** The "Already have a token?" link must be visible above the fold on the homepage AND on send.sgraph.ai. The entire conference demo depends on sharing tokens that recipients can redeem. This is not a nice-to-have.

5. **"Proposed" labelling for Code Execution Sandbox:** Any copy referencing the code execution sandbox must include the note that it is in development and not yet available. Do not describe it as shipped.
