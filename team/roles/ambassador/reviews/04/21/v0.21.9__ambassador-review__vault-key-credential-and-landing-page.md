## 1. The Communication Challenge

The auth MVP redesign (doc 297) drops Cognito entirely. The vault key is the credential. This is architecturally elegant and zero-knowledge preserving — but it requires careful external framing. The risks:

1. **Users will ask "where is my password?"** — the answer is "you don't have one. The vault key is both your vault and your login."
2. **Browser password managers are not universally trusted** — some users will worry about storing a vault key there.
3. **Recovery anxiety** — "what if I lose it?" is the first question from every non-technical user.

The messaging framework below addresses all three directly.

---

## 2. Core Messaging Framework: Vault Key as Credential

### 2.1 Primary Claim

> "Your vault key is your account. No password, no email, no reset link — because the key that unlocks your vault IS the proof you own it."

### 2.2 Three-Level Explanation

**One line (headline):**
> "No account needed. Just a key."

**One paragraph (landing page / onboarding):**
> "SG/Send doesn't have accounts. When you create a vault, you get a vault key — a short, memorable phrase like `apple-river-1234`. Save it in your browser's password manager (it'll offer to save it automatically), and you're done. The key opens your vault on any device. If you lose the key, the vault cannot be recovered — and that's the point. No one holds a master key, not even us."

**Technical explainer (for developers, security teams, due diligence):**
> "Credentials are stored as `PasswordCredential` entries in the browser's native Credential Management API. The vault key is the username; the credential secret is a `sg1.`-prefixed form of the key. SG/Send servers never see the vault key. There is no server-side credential store, no session token, no JWT. Authentication is possession of the key — zero-knowledge by construction."

### 3.2 The "Architectural" Distinction

This is the single most important message to communicate. Draft:

> **Most file sharing services say "your files are secure."**
> **We say: we physically cannot read them.**
>
> WeTransfer, Google Drive, Dropbox — they all use server-side encryption. That means they encrypt your files, but they hold the key. They can read your files if compelled by a court, compromised by a breach, or simply decide to.
>
> SG/Send works differently. Your browser encrypts the file using a key that never leaves your device. Our servers store a blob of ciphertext we cannot decrypt. If anyone ever seized our servers, they'd get scrambled noise.
>
> This is AES-256-GCM via the Web Crypto API. No proprietary code. Auditable in your browser's developer tools right now.

**Tone note:** Avoid "zero-knowledge" as the primary term — it sounds technical and abstract. Lead with the concrete human claim ("we can't read it"), then explain why.

## 4. Vault Key Storage — User-Facing FAQ

These questions will come up. Draft answers:

**"Where is my vault key stored?"**
> In your browser's password manager — the same place Chrome, Safari, and Firefox offer to save your passwords. If you use a password manager like 1Password or Bitwarden, save it there. If you don't trust either, write it on paper and store it somewhere safe.

**"What if I forget my vault key?"**
> The vault cannot be recovered without the key. This is intentional — it means SG/Send also cannot recover it, and neither can anyone who compromises our servers. We recommend saving the key in your browser's password manager when prompted.

**"Can SG/Send reset my key?"**
> No. There is no server-side record of your vault key. If we could reset it, we could access your vault. We can't, so we can't.

**"Is the vault key the same as a password?"**
> Functionally yes — it's a secret that unlocks your vault. The difference is that standard passwords authenticate you to a server that then grants access. Your vault key decrypts your files directly, in your browser. The server never learns the key.
