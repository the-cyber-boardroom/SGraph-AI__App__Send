## Executive Summary

These four briefs represent a coherent package: the Challenge campaign is the outreach engine, the website improvements are the landing surface, the translation scaling strategy is the infrastructure for multilingual reach, and the translator community is both a contributor pool and a market segment. Taken together, they define SGraph Send's first coordinated go-to-market motion.

**Overall assessment: Strategically sound, tactically sequencing-dependent.** The briefs are individually strong but contain hidden dependencies. If executed out of order, the Challenge will launch into a broken funnel. If sequenced correctly, this is the most promising customer acquisition path the project has had.

**The single most important finding in this review:** The Challenge cannot launch until the post-purchase experience is resolved. Buying credits is now one click. Receiving a token and knowing what to do with it is not. The token delivery gap identified in the page redesign review (`v0.10.9__review__page-redesign-proposal.md`) is amplified by the Challenge's requirement for same-session completion. This is the Campaign's blocking dependency.

### Messaging Strategy Recommendations

**1. The "challenge" framing is the right move.**

The brief's core insight -- framing outreach as a game rather than a sales pitch -- is psychologically sound. It achieves three things simultaneously:

- **Lowers resistance.** "Can you complete this challenge?" is easier to say yes to than "Will you buy my product?"
- **Creates natural completion incentive.** Challenges trigger the Zeigarnik effect -- people feel compelled to finish what they start.
- **Generates diagnostic data.** Every point where a participant gets stuck is a free usability report.

**2. The two-message WhatsApp strategy is correct.**

The brief's instruction to "wait for their 'yes' before sending the detailed steps" is critical. Front-loading instructions kills response rates. The initial message should be 3-4 lines maximum: what it is, what you are asking, what they get. The detailed steps come only after they express interest.

**3. The vulnerability angle is the strongest hook.**

The LLM brief captures this well: "I built this thing and I need people to test the full flow." This honest, builder-asking-for-help framing outperforms every other approach for warm contacts. The Ambassador recommends leading with this vulnerability in all channels.

**4. The multilingual LinkedIn strategy is a differentiator.**

Posting in Spanish, Portuguese, German, and French on LinkedIn is unusual and attention-getting. Most English-speaking founders post only in English. A Spanish LinkedIn post with a screenshot of a Spanish website is a signal of genuine investment in that market, not a Google Translate afterthought. This approach should be a permanent part of the Ambassador playbook, not just a Challenge tactic.

### Channel-Specific Recommendations

**WhatsApp:**
- Lead channel for the Challenge. Highest open rate (98%), highest response rate for personal contacts, lowest formality barrier.
- Use the two-step approach: hook message first, detailed steps on confirmation.
- Attach the Challenge card image (when produced) to the follow-up message, not the initial hook.
- Do NOT send the Challenge card as the first message -- an unsolicited image from a contact looks like spam.
- WhatsApp does NOT support SVG. The brief notes this as a question to verify. The answer is: use PNG. WhatsApp compresses images aggressively; a clean, high-contrast PNG at 1080x1080 will render well.
- Send at 10:00-11:00 local time (morning, but after the inbox-clearing rush) or 19:00-20:00 (evening relaxation).

**LinkedIn DM:**
- Second channel. Use for professional contacts where WhatsApp would be too informal.
- LinkedIn DMs have lower open rates than WhatsApp but higher perceived professionalism.
- The multilingual angle is more impactful here than on WhatsApp. A LinkedIn DM in Portuguese to a Portuguese contact, with a screenshot of the Portuguese website, is a strong differentiator.
- LinkedIn supports PNG images in DMs. Attach the Challenge card.
- Avoid LinkedIn voice messages -- they have low completion rates and cannot be skimmed.

**LinkedIn public post:**
- Tertiary channel. Use for broad reach and social proof.
- The public post should NOT mention the Amazon voucher. The voucher is for DM-driven personal outreach.
- The public post should frame the Challenge as a conversation: "I'm building something and I want to see if it actually works for real people."
- Post in English first. Follow up with language-specific posts over subsequent days (one per day, to avoid flooding followers).
- Best posting time for LinkedIn UK: Tuesday-Thursday, 08:00-09:00 GMT.

**Email:**
- Quaternary channel. Use only for contacts where WhatsApp and LinkedIn are not appropriate.
- The email version should be longer, more explanatory, and include full context (what SG/Send is, why you are building it, the Challenge steps, the Amazon voucher).
- Subject line recommendation: "Can you do something for me? (10 minutes, free)" -- curiosity + low commitment.

### Messaging Strategy Implications

**The three-zone model directly affects outreach messaging.** When writing multilingual LinkedIn posts or WhatsApp messages, the Ambassador must only link to Zone 1 (tools) and Zone 2 (commercial website) content. Any link to Zone 3 (docs, guides, deep-dives) should include a language caveat: "Documentation is currently in English."

**Specific recommendation for multilingual outreach messages:**

- Every multilingual message should link to the language-specific URL: `sgraph.ai?lang=es-es`, `sgraph.ai?lang=pt-pt`, etc.
- The message should NOT link to `sgraph.ai` without a language parameter for non-English contacts. Auto-detect may work, but cannot be guaranteed (a Portuguese user on a UK-configured browser would see English).
- Verify that the `?lang=` parameter actually works and persists. If it does not, the multilingual outreach strategy has a broken link problem.

### Multilingual Strategy Assessment

The three-zone model is sound from a campaign perspective. It correctly identifies that:

1. **Tool UIs must be multilingual** -- a Spanish user sending an encrypted file needs the UI in Spanish.
2. **The commercial website must be multilingual** -- this is the landing surface for outreach and the first impression.
3. **Documentation can wait** -- no Challenge participant will read API docs.

**The Ambassador endorses the Zone 3 English-only decision.** For campaign purposes, it reduces scope without reducing reach. The Campaign targets people who will use the product, not people who will read the documentation.

**Cost management is a real constraint.** The brief reports that the human hit Claude Code usage limits during the initial translation session. If the Campaign generates demand for content updates (new news posts, feature announcements), those updates must be budgeted for translation cost. The Ambassador recommends that all Campaign-generated content be written in English first and translated only into the top 5 priority languages (EN-GB, ES-ES, PT-PT, DE-DE, FR-FR), with remaining variants translated asynchronously.

### Target Audience Analysis: Translators

The brief's insight that translators are both contributors and customers is strategically valuable. The Ambassador's analysis:

**As contributors (Phase 2):**
- Translators have the skills and motivation to fix AI-generated translation errors.
- The GitHub PR workflow is accessible to technically literate translators (a subset of the profession).
- The "first external PR" metric is the right success measure -- it validates the entire pipeline.
- ProZ.com and TranslatorsCafe are the right channels for contributor outreach.

**As customers (Phase 3):**
- Translators receive and send files constantly (manuscripts, contracts, NDA-protected documents).
- They work across borders and need GDPR-compliant file transfer.
- They deal with client credentials and project access tokens.
- SG/Send's zero-knowledge encryption is a genuine value proposition for this audience.
- The pitch: "You review our translations, we provide you with the encrypted file transfer tool you need for your clients."

**Sizing the opportunity:**
- ProZ.com has 300,000+ registered translators globally.
- The top 5 language markets for SG/Send (ES, PT, DE, FR, IT) represent a significant fraction of that base.
- Even 0.1% conversion (300 users) at GBP 5 each would be GBP 1,500 in revenue and, more importantly, 300 active users generating usage data.

### Messaging Strategy for Translator Outreach (Phase 2)

When the time comes, the Ambassador recommends a two-pronged message:

**Prong 1 (Contributor):**
> "We've AI-translated our encrypted file sharing product into 17 language variants. We know the AI got some things wrong. If you're a native [Spanish/Portuguese/German] speaker, we'd love your help making the translations feel natural. You can review the translations side-by-side and submit fixes via GitHub. Every contributor is credited on our site."

**Prong 2 (Customer):**
> "By the way -- SG/Send is exactly the kind of tool translators need. Zero-knowledge encrypted file transfer for sending manuscripts, contracts, and credentials to clients. No account needed for the recipient. GBP 0.01 per file. Built for people who handle sensitive documents every day."

The two prongs should appear in the same message. The contributor ask builds goodwill; the product pitch follows naturally from the relationship.

### Content Review -- Ambassador's Specific Concerns

The brief identifies "references to pricing that don't match reality" and "claims about file hosting costing GBP 5,000." From the Ambassador's lens, this is a **P0 blocker** for the Campaign:

1. **A Challenge participant will scrutinise the site more carefully than a casual visitor.** They are being asked to find the buy button and complete a purchase. They will read pages they would otherwise skim. Any inaccuracy they find will be reported back (which is useful) but also damages the perception of professionalism.

2. **The multilingual variants multiply the risk.** An inaccurate claim in English may also appear in 16 other language variants. The content review must check all 17 variants, not just English.

3. **Competitor pricing claims are legally risky.** Stating that a competitor charges GBP 5,000 for a service, without a verifiable source, is a comparative advertising claim. In the UK (where The Cyber Boardroom Ltd is registered), the Advertising Standards Authority requires comparative claims to be "objectively verifiable." Unverifiable competitor pricing claims should be removed entirely, not just corrected.

**Ambassador action:** The Sherpa leads the content review. The Ambassador should provide a checklist of claims that are marketing-sensitive (pricing, performance, security comparisons) and review the Sherpa's output before sign-off.
