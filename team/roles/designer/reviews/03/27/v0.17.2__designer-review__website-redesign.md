## 1. Current Design Audit

### What's working
- **Aurora colour palette** — dark navy `#1A1A2E` + teal `#4ECDC4` is distinctive and visually coherent
- **Typography** — DM Sans is readable and modern; JetBrains Mono for code elements is correct
- **CSS custom properties** — design tokens are in place; the system is ready to evolve
- **Card component** — the three-column feature cards have good structure
- **CTA buttons** — primary/secondary button hierarchy is clear
- **Footer grid** — clean four-column layout

### What's weak
- **No product screenshots anywhere** — the entire homepage is text. For a product with gallery views, folder browsers, and SgPrint, this is a critical missed opportunity. "Show the product" is the #1 design principle and the current site does not do it.
- **Hero section is text-only** — the hero visual is just a headline and two buttons on a dark background. No imagery, no screenshot, no animation.
- **Stats bar looks like a metrics dashboard, not a trust signal** — "18 AI Agents / 73 HTTP Endpoints / 393 Passing Tests / 1 Human" means nothing to a recipient or evaluator without context. The numbers are impressive but unexplained.
- **"Built Entirely by AI Agents" section undermines trust** — placed before any product proof, this section tells a security-sensitive visitor "robots built this" before they've been shown the product.
- **Feature cards are generic** — lock icon, no-entry icon, globe icon with text. No screenshots, no product-specific visuals. Could describe any SaaS product.
- **Pricing cards are buried** — the Get Started section (pricing) appears after the "Built by AI" section, which is wrong funnel order.
- **Mobile hero** — on a phone, the hero headline wraps awkwardly. No mobile-specific visual consideration.

## 2. What to Remove Visually

### `/agents/` page — REMOVE
Three AI profile pages have no visual value on the marketing site. They create a confusing experience for visitors expecting product content. Move to qa.send.sgraph.ai.

### `/architecture/` page — REPLACE with `/security/`
Keep the content, reframe the visual presentation from "internal architecture doc" to "security trust page". The current architecture page likely has technical diagrams that belong in a CISO-facing security page with better visual treatment.

### "Built Entirely by AI" stats section — REPOSITION
Don't remove — but move it below the conversion section. It should come AFTER the visitor has seen the product and is convinced. It then becomes a "how was this made?" curiosity satisfier, not a trust-undermining opener. Alternatively, reframe the stats as product quality signals: "393 passing tests | Open source | Auditable" rather than "18 AI agents".

### `/product/` page — DEPRECATE
Merge into homepage. The homepage redesign renders it redundant.

## 4. `<sg-public-viewer>` as Hero Visual

The interactive demo is the hero's right-column visual. Design requirements:

- **Two-column hero layout** — headline + CTAs on left (60%), live demo on right (40%)
- **Demo shows the gallery view** — most visually impressive, immediately communicates "this is not just a download link"
- **Loading state** — skeleton shimmer while content decrypts (~200ms). Show the decryption happening — it's a feature, not a bug.
- **Fallback** — static screenshot of gallery view with "Enable JavaScript for live demo" text. Must look good as a static image.
- **Mobile** — demo stacks below the headline on screens < 768px. The demo is secondary on mobile; the headline and CTA are primary.
- **Caption below demo** — "Live demo — actually encrypted. Key is intentionally public." — this explains what they're seeing and demonstrates the ZK model.

## 9. Mobile-First Considerations

The recipient journey on mobile is the most critical path:

1. **Hero** — headline first, demo/screenshot second (stacked). CTA must be thumb-reachable (bottom 60% of viewport). Button minimum 48px height.
2. **Feature screenshots** — full-width, swipeable on mobile. Do not show two-column on screens < 768px.
3. **Six-sentence privacy policy** — full-width card, large enough to read without zooming. This is a trust moment — don't squeeze it.
4. **Share modes** — stack to single column on mobile. Three cards side-by-side is too small.
5. **Nav** — hamburger menu is already implemented. Ensure `/security/` replaces `/architecture/` in the mobile menu.
6. **Footer** — single column on mobile. Logo and key links (send.sgraph.ai, GitHub) above the fold.
7. **Font sizes** — H1 on mobile should be 2rem minimum. Current display class may be too large and cause awkward wrapping.

## 10. Competitive Visual Positioning

### WeTransfer (white/minimal)
WeTransfer is clean, bright, consumer-friendly. It looks like a design tool. The aesthetic communicates "easy and beautiful" but does not communicate "private" or "trustworthy". SG/Send's dark theme is an immediate visual contrast — it signals seriousness and security without being cold.

### ProtonDrive (dark/corporate)
ProtonDrive is dark and privacy-focused but feels corporate and slightly cold. It lacks warmth. The typography is utilitarian. SG/Send has an opportunity to be dark + warm + alive — the teal accent brings energy that ProtonDrive's palette lacks.

### SG/Send visual differentiator
The competitive opportunity is: **dark + warm + shows the product inline**.

- WeTransfer shows no product on its homepage
- ProtonDrive shows minimal product UI
- SG/Send can show a live encrypted gallery in the hero

A visitor who sees an actual decrypted gallery loading in their browser during their first visit has experienced the product before signing up. No competitor does this. This is the single most powerful visual decision available.

**Visual positioning statement:** "Trustworthy without being cold. Powerful without being intimidating. Shows you the product, not a description of it."
