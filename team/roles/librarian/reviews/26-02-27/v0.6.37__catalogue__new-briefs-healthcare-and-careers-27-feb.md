## Brief Set 1: Healthcare Use Case (7 documents)

**Location:** `team/humans/dinis_cruz/briefs/02/26/health-care-use-case/`

### Document Summaries

#### 01 — Competitive Landscape Overview
**File:** `01-competitive-landscape-overview.md`
**Audience:** All

Frames the enterprise secure messaging market for regulated industries (healthcare, legal, finance). Identifies three structural failure modes of incumbents: security theatre (server holds all keys while marketing "AES-256"), UX punishment (mandatory account registration to read a single message), and feature stagnation (products frozen circa 2008). Positions SG/Send as the challenger that is both genuinely secure and dramatically better to use.

#### 02 — Security Architecture Analysis
**File:** `02-security-architecture-analysis.md`
**Audience:** Technical / Security

Reverse-engineers the incumbent's server-custodied encryption model from a received message. Documents that the vendor decrypts server-side on every read (the `ksURL="http://localhost:8080/ks/ks"` field routes to an internal key server by message UUID). Compares the incumbent's threat model (protects against wire interception but not vendor access) against SG/Send's zero-knowledge model. Notes the recipient portal password policy caps at 20 characters — a weakness suggesting non-modern password storage.

#### 03 — User Journey & UX Analysis
**File:** `03-user-journey-ux-analysis.md`
**Audience:** Product / Design

Maps the complete recipient experience step-by-step: wrapper email with no content preview, mandatory account registration (password + security question), broken reply flow (Reply button routes to an unconfigured alias), no structured actions (no "Add to Calendar" despite containing appointment data), desktop-first fixed layout. Measures 5-10 minutes to first message read for new recipients vs. SG/Send's target of under 60 seconds. Identifies a proportionality problem: generic FAQs and pathology reports receive identical heavy-handed encryption treatment.

#### 04 — Wire Format & Protocol Deep Dive
**File:** `04-wire-format-protocol-deep-dive.md`
**Audience:** Engineering

Deconstructs the incumbent's wire format: a proprietary XML-based secure document format (namespace dated `/2001/3/`, version 7). The encrypted payload is chunked into 45 hidden HTML form fields of 1,925 characters each, auto-POSTed to the vendor server on page load. Documents the full MIME structure (~170KB for a short appointment confirmation vs. 5-15KB unencrypted), the processing pipeline (gzip, AES-256-CBC encrypt, base64, chunk, embed in form), and the metadata envelope with trivially reversible letter-pair subject encoding. Contrasts with a modern SG/Send approach: JSON envelope, AES-256-GCM, Ed25519 signing, Web Crypto API.

#### 05 — Feature Gap Analysis & Opportunities
**File:** `05-feature-gap-analysis-opportunities.md`
**Audience:** Product / Business

Synthesises the competitive analysis into 10 structured opportunities: zero-knowledge encryption, no-registration recipient experience, structured actions in messages, proper reply workflow, calendar/ICS generation, mobile-first rendering, phishing-resistant delivery, proportional encryption (content-aware policies), transparent architecture, and usage-based pricing. Includes a prioritisation matrix. Defines the Minimum Viable Difference as: compliance parity + genuine security superiority + dramatically lower recipient friction.

#### 06 — Classification, Provenance, PKI as Differentiator
**File:** `06-healthcare-review__doc06__classification-provenance-pki.md`
**Audience:** Technical / Security / Compliance

Addresses three gaps not covered in Docs 01-05: data classification that travels with the document (SGMETA envelope carrying classification inside ciphertext), document provenance (cryptographic vs. server-assertion — sender's private key signature vs. platform certificate), and PKI as a healthcare differentiator (non-repudiation for consent forms, referral letters, clinical decisions). Proposes five classification tiers for healthcare (UNRESTRICTED through LEGAL). Frames the regulatory argument: GDPR Article 9 requires controls throughout a document's lifecycle, not just at creation.

#### 07 — Strategic Synthesis: How SG/Send Wins
**File:** `07-strategic-synthesis-how-sgSend-wins.md`
**Audience:** All — Business, Product, Engineering, Sales

Connects the observed incumbent failures (Docs 01-05) to SG/Send capabilities (Doc 06) across a three-layer gap model: experience gap (UX), architectural gap (key custody, wire format), and trust model gap (server-assertion vs. cryptographic proof). Maps six specific failure modes to SG/Send responses. Argues the incumbent's failures are structural — none can be fixed without replacing the architecture. Frames the "don't trust us, verify us" sales narrative for different buyer personas (IT/Security, Compliance/Legal, Clinical/Operations, Procurement).

## Brief Set 2: Wikipedia of Careers (3 documents)

**Location:** `team/humans/dinis_cruz/briefs/02/26/wikipedia-of-carrers/`

### Document Summaries

#### 1 — Concept
**File:** `1__wikipedia-of-careers__concept.md`
**Audience:** All

Introduces the "Wikipedia for Careers" concept: an open, Creative Commons, community-built body of knowledge mapping the actual paths people take to career success — including the uncertain parts, the pivots, and the hidden accelerators that don't appear on LinkedIn profiles. Frames the social mobility problem: career paths are opaque, and that opacity is not evenly distributed. Introduces "trespasser syndrome" (people who feel a career is not meant for people like them) and the "last-mile credit problem" (the people who do the real work are invisible). Proposes cybersecurity as the pilot domain.

#### 2 — Agentic Implementation Plan
**File:** `2__wikipedia-of-careers__agentic-implementation.md`
**Audience:** SG/Send team, technical collaborators

Details a six-phase agentic pipeline for scaling the project: recruitment/consent, interview brief creation, multi-mode interviews (human, agent voice, agent text, visual, self-directed), transcription and initial processing, pattern analysis across the corpus, and cross-career mapping. Argues that agent-conducted interviews are a first-class pathway (not a fallback) due to scale, multilingual reach, timezone flexibility, and evidence that subjects are sometimes more candid with agents. Describes provenance requirements: every transcript signed by the interviewer, every agent analysis signed by the run, every review signed by the human reviewer.

#### 3 — SGraph Team Brief
**File:** `3__wikipedia-of-careers__sgraph-team-brief.md`
**Audience:** SG/Send team

Maps the Wikipedia for Careers' needs against SG/Send's existing and planned capabilities across five problems: (1) encrypted storage for sensitive interview data (vault/data rooms), (2) cryptographically verifiable provenance and attribution (PKI signing), (3) multilingual processing with signed translation metadata, (4) open-source knowledge base hosting (static site infrastructure), (5) contribution tracking that inverts the credit pyramid (contribution graph). Identifies specific gaps in the current platform and estimates effort to close them. Frames the project as a compelling reference use case that demonstrates SG/Send's value beyond file transfer.

## Key Themes Across Both Brief Sets

### 1. Cryptographic Proof vs. Server Assertion
Both brief sets centre on the same architectural distinction: the difference between "the server says so" (incumbent healthcare model, traditional research provenance) and "the mathematics says so" (SG/Send's zero-knowledge model, signed document chains). This is the foundational narrative that runs through all 10 documents.

### 2. Provenance That Travels With the Document
Healthcare Docs 06-07 and Careers Doc 2 both require provenance metadata that is embedded inside the document, not stored in a separate server database. The SGMETA envelope is the proposed carrier in both cases — for classification metadata in healthcare, and for interview/analysis attribution in careers.

### 3. PKI as the Trust Foundation (Not Just an Encryption Feature)
Both brief sets treat PKI not as a feature but as an enabling layer. In healthcare: non-repudiation for clinical decisions, consent, and referrals. In careers: signed attribution chains for research integrity. The current PKI infrastructure (key publish/lookup) is a starting point; the full signing-at-point-of-creation workflow is the gap.

### 4. Agent-Assisted Workflows on Top of Encrypted Infrastructure
Both brief sets describe agentic workflows that operate on encrypted data: healthcare structured actions (calendar, confirm, reschedule) and careers interview processing pipelines. Both depend on MCP access to vaults/rooms, which is partially built but not fully agentic-accessible.

### 5. Open, Verifiable, Zero-Lock-In
The healthcare briefs emphasise transparent architecture (publish the protocol, open-source the client) and the careers briefs are explicitly Creative Commons. Both align with SG/Send's existing zero-lock-in principle.

### 6. The "Don't Trust Us, Verify Us" Narrative
Doc 07 articulates this explicitly for healthcare buyers. Doc 3 articulates it for research credibility. This is the single most important positioning statement across both brief sets and applies directly to the Town Planner/Alchemist investor narrative.
