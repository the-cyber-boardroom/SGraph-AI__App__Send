## Executive Summary

SGraph Send is crossing a threshold: it is transitioning from a technical proof-of-concept into a commercial product operated by **The Cyber Boardroom Ltd**, a UK legal entity, and marketed internationally in 17+ locales. This transition triggers legal obligations that were deferred during the Explorer phase — they can no longer be deferred.

The v0.7.6 brief batch introduces three GRC-critical developments:

1. **Revenue collection begins.** Stripe Checkout is being wired. Personal data (email, payment) will be processed. GDPR Art. 6 lawful basis must be established before the first transaction.
2. **Public research into competitor tracking.** The whatismyipaddress.com analysis is both a marketing asset and a legal liability if its factual claims are unsound.
3. **Multi-cloud, multi-region deployment.** EU data sovereignty obligations vary by cloud provider and jurisdiction. Architecture decisions being made now will determine regulatory exposure for years.

This document proposes **12 GRC workstreams**, classified by business, delivery, and product impact. Each workstream includes effort estimates, priority rankings, and explicit dependencies.

**Continuity note:** Prior GRC work at v0.6.17 (28-brief governance assessment) and v0.6.20 (fastapi-mcp dependency governance) remains open. Where prior GRC recommendations are relevant they are cross-referenced rather than repeated.

### B-GRC-01: Legal Entity Disclosure and Terms of Service

**ID:** B-GRC-01
**Title:** Legal Entity Disclosure, Terms of Service, and Privacy Policy for The Cyber Boardroom Ltd
**Classification:** B* (Business — Legal)
**Priority:** CRITICAL (P0)

#### Description

SGraph Send is currently operating under the brand "sgraph.ai" with no publicly disclosed legal entity, no Terms of Service, and no Privacy Policy. The Librarian confirmed in the batch-2 review that an "About" page stating the legal entity does not exist (`sgraph_ai__website/` has no `/about/index.html`). The pricing page exists and links to a "Buy Credits" button that is being connected to Stripe Checkout. Once a customer completes a Stripe transaction, The Cyber Boardroom Ltd has entered into a contract with that person — without any documented terms governing that contract.

This is not a theoretical risk. Under UK Consumer Rights Act 2015 and the Consumer Contracts (Information, Cancellation and Additional Charges) Regulations 2013, distance contracts must provide mandatory pre-contract information including: (a) identity of the trader, (b) description of the goods/services, (c) total price including taxes, (d) information on payment arrangements, and (e) cancellation rights. Failure to comply means the contract may be unenforceable and the customer retains rights to cancel without penalty.

This workstream covers four documents:

1. **Terms of Service** — Governs the relationship between The Cyber Boardroom Ltd and users of send.sgraph.ai. Must address: acceptable use, account termination, credit expiry (3-month policy as stated in the pricing brief), limitation of liability, dispute resolution, and governing law (England and Wales).
2. **Privacy Policy** — Discloses what personal data is collected, why, on what lawful basis (GDPR Art. 6), how long it is retained, and how data subjects can exercise their rights (Arts. 15–22). Must cover at minimum: IP hashing (pseudonymisation), access token email, Stripe payment processing (via DPA).
3. **About page** — Legal entity disclosure: The Cyber Boardroom Ltd, company registration number (to be inserted), registered address, contact email. Required for consumer law compliance and for the Stripe dashboard (Stripe requires legal entity information to be publicly accessible).
4. **Cookie/Consent Notice** — Even a minimal notice is needed. If sgraph.ai sets no cookies (which is not confirmed — the Stripe checkout redirect sets cookies), the notice can be a simple "We do not use tracking cookies" statement. If Stripe sets cookies on the payment flow, a proper consent mechanism may be required.

#### Rationale

Every day Stripe Checkout is live without Terms of Service is a day the company is entering into undocumented contracts. The ICO (UK data protection regulator) requires a Privacy Policy to be accessible before personal data is collected. Stripe's own merchant requirements mandate that the business's legal name, address, and a link to its Terms and Privacy Policy are accessible from the payment page.

#### Dependencies

- Legal counsel review of drafted terms (external, not an agent deliverable)
- Company registration number and registered address from the human stakeholder
- Stripe dashboard access to confirm what cookies/data Stripe sets on redirect

### B-GRC-02: GDPR Compliance for the Billing and Payment Flow

**ID:** B-GRC-02
**Title:** GDPR Lawful Basis and Data Subject Rights for Stripe Payment Processing
**Classification:** B* (Business — Privacy/Legal)
**Priority:** CRITICAL (P0)

#### Description

The pricing brief (v0.7.6) establishes that customers pay GBP 5 via Stripe Checkout and receive an access token by email. This flow involves processing the following personal data:

| Data Item | Controller | Processor | Lawful Basis Needed |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Email address (for token delivery) | The Cyber Boardroom Ltd | SG WorkMail / AWS WorkMail | Art. 6(1)(b) — contract performance |
| Payment card data (card number, expiry, CVV) | Stripe (as controller) | N/A | Stripe's own lawful basis |
| Billing name and address | Stripe (as controller) | N/A | Stripe's own lawful basis |
| Transaction ID and amount | The Cyber Boardroom Ltd | Stripe (as processor) | Art. 6(1)(b) — contract performance |
| Access token issued | The Cyber Boardroom Ltd | In-system | Art. 6(1)(b) — contract performance |

**Key GRC findings:**

1. **Stripe as data processor:** When Stripe processes payment data on behalf of The Cyber Boardroom Ltd (not independently), a **Data Processing Agreement (DPA)** is required under GDPR Art. 28. Stripe provides a standard DPA; it must be executed by the merchant before going live with payment processing.
2. **Stripe as independent controller:** For Stripe's own fraud detection, account management, and compliance purposes, Stripe acts as an independent data controller. Their Privacy Policy covers this. The merchant's Privacy Policy must disclose the Stripe relationship.
3. **Email address retention:** The email address used for token delivery must have a documented retention period. Suggested: retain until the credit pack expires (3 months from purchase) plus any statutory requirement for transaction records (typically 6 years under UK company law).
4. **Token-to-identity linkage:** The current architecture issues access tokens that are not linked to identity. If email is collected only for delivery and then discarded, this is good privacy practice — but the process must be documented.
5. **Right to erasure (Art. 17):** A customer can request deletion of their personal data. The process for handling such requests must be defined. For access tokens with remaining credits, erasure creates a tension with fraud prevention (the refund/cancellation policy must address this).

This workstream produces: (a) an assessment of GDPR lawful bases for each data processing activity, (b) a recommendation on DPA execution with Stripe, (c) data retention schedule for billing data, and (d) a data subject rights procedure document.

#### Rationale

The ICO can impose fines of up to GBP 17.5 million or 4% of global annual turnover for serious GDPR violations. Processing payment-related personal data without a documented lawful basis and without a DPA with Stripe is a straightforward violation. This is not speculative risk — it is a concrete gap that exists the moment the first Stripe transaction completes.

#### Dependencies

- Stripe DPA execution requires human stakeholder action (not an agent deliverable)
- Privacy Policy from B-GRC-01 must exist before billing goes live
- Email retention decision requires human stakeholder input on 3-month credit expiry policy

#### Effort

| Task | Effort |
|------|--------|
| GDPR lawful basis mapping document | 2 hours (agent) |
| Data retention schedule | 1 hour (agent) |
| Data subject rights procedure | 2 hours (agent) |
| Stripe DPA — review and flag for execution | 1 hour (agent review) + human action |

**Total (agent):** ~6 hours
**External dependency:** Stripe DPA execution by the human stakeholder

### B-GRC-03: Data Processing Agreements (DPA) Register

**ID:** B-GRC-03
**Title:** Third-Party DPA Register: Stripe, AWS, CloudFront
**Classification:** B* (Business — Supply Chain / Legal)
**Priority:** HIGH (P1)

#### Description

GDPR Art. 28 requires that where a controller uses a processor to process personal data on its behalf, the arrangement must be governed by a written contract (the DPA). The following third-party processors are used by SGraph Send and require assessment:

| Processor | Data Processed | DPA Required | DPA Available |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Stripe** | Payment data, billing email, transaction records | Yes — Art. 28 | Yes — Stripe provides standard DPA |
| **AWS** (Lambda, S3) | Encrypted ciphertext (non-personal by design), IP hashes, access logs | Conditional — if IP hash + other data = personal data | Yes — AWS Data Processing Addendum |
| **AWS CloudFront** | Request logs (timestamps, endpoints, user agents, country codes, edge locations) | Conditional — logs contain behavioural metadata | Covered by AWS DPA |
| **AWS WorkMail** | Email addresses, email content (for token delivery) | Yes — email is personal data | Covered by AWS DPA |
| **GitHub** (Actions, repos) | Source code, CI/CD pipeline data, potentially secrets in env vars | Conditional — depends on secrets exposure | Yes — GitHub DPA |

**Key assessment points:**

1. **AWS DPA:** The AWS Customer Agreement includes a Data Processing Addendum that covers all AWS services under GDPR. The Cyber Boardroom Ltd must confirm it has accepted the AWS DPA (done by accepting the Customer Agreement with DPA terms active). This needs to be verified and documented.

2. **CloudFront logs — personal data determination:** The reality document confirms that client IP addresses are deliberately excluded from CloudFront logging. The external security review (28 Feb 2026) confirmed no PII in logs. However, the combination of user agent strings, timestamps, and endpoint paths could theoretically allow fingerprinting. GRC should formally document the personal data determination: "CloudFront logs do not contain personal data as defined by GDPR Art. 4(1) because [reasons]." This protects against regulatory challenge.

3. **GitHub — secrets in CI/CD:** The GitHub Actions workflows use repository secrets (`WEBSITE_S3_BUCKET`, `WEBSITE_CF_DIST`). These are infrastructure identifiers, not personal data. However, if any workflow accidentally logs personal data, GitHub becomes a processor. The DPA register should document this risk.

4. **Cloudflare, GCP, Azure (PROPOSED):** The multi-cloud brief proposes deployments to Cloudflare R2, GCP Cloud Storage, and Azure Blob Storage. Each would require DPA assessment before deployment.

This workstream produces a **DPA Register** documenting: processor identity, data processed, DPA reference (document, version, date executed), and any gaps requiring action.

#### Rationale

A DPA register is a fundamental GDPR compliance artefact. Regulators expect controllers to be able to produce a list of all processors and the DPA governing each relationship. Without this register, The Cyber Boardroom Ltd cannot demonstrate GDPR compliance even if the underlying DPAs exist.

#### Dependencies

- B-GRC-01 (Privacy Policy must reference processors)
- Human stakeholder confirmation of AWS account acceptance of DPA terms
- Multi-cloud deployment decisions (which providers to add to the register)

#### Effort

| Task | Effort |
|------|--------|
| DPA register template and initial entries | 2 hours (agent) |
| CloudFront log personal data determination | 1 hour (agent) |
| GitHub Actions secrets assessment | 1 hour (agent) |
| Multi-cloud DPA gap analysis (PROPOSED providers) | 2 hours (agent, after deployment decisions) |

**Total (agent):** ~6 hours

### B-GRC-04: Credit Expiry — Consumer Rights Assessment

**ID:** B-GRC-04
**Title:** 3-Month Credit Expiry: Consumer Rights and Unfair Contract Terms Analysis
**Classification:** B* (Business — Consumer Law)
**Priority:** HIGH (P1)

#### Description

The pricing brief proposes a 3-month expiry on purchased credits (access tokens). This is a commercially common practice, but it has legal implications under UK consumer law that must be assessed before the expiry mechanism is implemented.

**Relevant legal framework:**

1. **Consumer Rights Act 2015 (UK):** Digital content must be of satisfactory quality, fit for purpose, and as described. If a customer purchases 500 file transfer credits and 200 expire unused, they may argue the product was not "as described" if the expiry was not clearly disclosed at the point of sale.

2. **Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977 / CRA 2015 Schedule 2:** A term that allows a supplier to retain payment for a service not delivered could be considered unfair if it creates a significant imbalance to the consumer's detriment. Courts assess: was the term transparent? Was the consumer given adequate notice? Was the expiry period reasonable?

3. **Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013:** The 14-day cooling-off right for distance contracts. If a customer exercises this right within 14 days of purchase (before using any credits), they are entitled to a full refund. The current proposed model (no refunds described) must address this.

4. **EU Consumer Rights Directive (if selling to EU customers):** Similar 14-day withdrawal right. SG/Send has 17 locales including EU countries. EU customers purchasing via Stripe would fall under EU consumer law.

**GRC assessment required:**

- Is 3 months a reasonable expiry period for a GBP 5 purchase? (Comparative analysis: industry practice for prepaid credits)
- Is the expiry term "transparent" and "prominent" as required under CRA 2015?
- What happens to unused credits if the service is discontinued? (No policy exists.)
- Does the 14-day cooling-off right apply, and how is it exercised?
- Is the auto-expiry mechanism sufficient notice, or must an expiry reminder email be sent?

This workstream produces: (a) a legal risk assessment of the credit expiry model, (b) recommended wording for the Terms of Service expiry clause, and (c) a flag to the Conductor if the expiry model requires modification.

#### Rationale

UK trading standards can take action against unfair contract terms. Stripe's dispute resolution process (chargebacks) also penalises merchants with unfair practices. Getting this right before the first customer pays avoids chargebacks and regulatory exposure.

#### Dependencies

- B-GRC-01 (Terms of Service must contain the expiry clause)
- Human stakeholder decision on whether the expiry period can be negotiated (e.g., extended from 3 to 6 months for early adopters)

#### Effort

| Task | Effort |
|------|--------|
| Consumer rights assessment document | 3 hours (agent research + analysis) |
| Recommended ToS expiry clause wording | 1 hour (agent) |
| Cooling-off right procedure | 1 hour (agent) |

**Total (agent):** ~5 hours

### B-GRC-05: Open Source Licensing and Self-Hosted Deployment

**ID:** B-GRC-05
**Title:** Open Source Licence Selection, Self-Hosted Legal Framework, and White-Label Programme Terms
**Classification:** B* (Business — IP/Legal)
**Priority:** MEDIUM (P2)

#### Description

The pricing matrix brief (v0.7.6 batch 2) proposes four free deployment options including "Open Source — Self-Hosted" (clone repo, deploy anywhere, free forever) and "White Label / Partner Deployment" (brand it as their own). Neither a confirmed open source licence nor a white-label legal framework exists. The reality document confirms both are "DOES NOT EXIST."

**Open Source Licence Selection:**

The project currently has no licence file visible in the reality document (the GRC dependency review at v0.6.20 referenced "Apache 2.0" as the project's licence, but this was inferred not confirmed). Before announcing "open source" to customers, the licence must be:

1. **Selected** — Apache 2.0 is a reasonable choice given the prior GRC reference, but alternatives (MIT, AGPL-3.0) have different implications:
   - **MIT:** Maximum permissiveness. Allows proprietary forks. Anyone can build a competing commercial product without attribution beyond the copyright notice.
   - **Apache 2.0:** Similar permissiveness but includes patent grant. Protects against patent-based attacks by contributors. Compatible with enterprise use.
   - **AGPL-3.0:** Copyleft with a network use clause. Anyone who deploys a modified version to users must release their modifications. Strong protection against commercial free-riding. May deter enterprise adoption.

2. **Applied** — A `LICENSE` file must exist at the root of the repository. Copyright notice must identify The Cyber Boardroom Ltd.

3. **Documented** — The self-hosted detail page (`/pricing/self-hosted`) must state the licence clearly.

**White-Label / Partner Programme:**

A white-label arrangement means a third party deploys SG/Send branded as their own product. This requires a separate legal framework beyond the open source licence:
- A **Reseller/OEM Agreement** specifying: what the partner can brand, what they must disclose (e.g., "Powered by SGraph technology"), what liability the partner assumes for their deployment, and what support The Cyber Boardroom Ltd provides.
- A **Partner Code of Conduct** (acceptable use for partner deployments).
- Whether the partner's customers are in a direct relationship with The Cyber Boardroom Ltd or only with the partner.

**Prior GRC context:** The v0.6.17 review assessed CC BY-S signed licence variants for a different context (creative commons for document signing). The open source licence for SG/Send codebase is a separate question. However, the IP strategy work from v0.6.17 (eIDAS analysis, CC Foundation engagement) is relevant if The Cyber Boardroom Ltd wishes to claim IP protection over the "-S signed" encryption approach.

#### Rationale

Publishing a repo without a licence means it is "all rights reserved" by default under copyright law — users technically have no right to clone, fork, or deploy it. If the pricing page says "open source, free to deploy" but no licence exists, the company is making a claim it cannot legally back.

#### Dependencies

- Human stakeholder decision on licence type (MIT vs Apache 2.0 vs AGPL-3.0)
- `/pricing/self-hosted` page (PROPOSED — does not exist yet) depends on licence decision
- White-label programme depends on Partner Agreement drafting (legal counsel required)

| LICENSE file creation (once decided) | 15 minutes (agent) |
| White-label programme legal framework scoping | 2 hours (agent) + legal counsel |
| Self-hosted page licence disclosure | Included in Developer's page work |

### P-GRC-01: Cookie Policy and Consent Management for sgraph.ai

**ID:** P-GRC-01
**Title:** Cookie Audit, Consent Mechanism, and ePrivacy Compliance for sgraph.ai
**Classification:** P* (Product — Privacy/UX)
**Priority:** HIGH (P1)

#### Description

The UK Privacy and Electronic Communications Regulations 2003 (PECR), implementing the EU ePrivacy Directive, require informed consent before setting non-essential cookies. The GDPR layer adds requirements for that consent to be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous.

**Current state (code-verified):**

The sgraph.ai website (`sgraph_ai__website/`) has:
- No cookie consent banner or mechanism
- No cookie policy page
- Contact form using `mailto:` (no cookies from this)
- Stripe payment redirect (Stripe sets cookies on payment pages)
- i18n locale routing (no cookies — URL-based routing per the reality document)
- Pricing page with a "Buy Credits" link to Stripe Checkout

**What Stripe does with cookies:**

When a user clicks "Buy Credits" and lands on Stripe's hosted checkout page, Stripe sets cookies for: fraud prevention (Stripe's legitimate interest), session management (strictly necessary), and potentially analytics on Stripe's own domain. Because the checkout is hosted on Stripe's domain (`checkout.stripe.com`), those cookies are set by Stripe under Stripe's cookie policy — not The Cyber Boardroom Ltd's. However, the sgraph.ai Privacy Policy must disclose this redirect and Stripe's data collection.

**What the send.sgraph.ai User UI does with cookies:**

The User Lambda sets `room_session` cookies (24h expiry, revocable per the reality document). Room session tokens are set server-side. These are **strictly necessary cookies** (they are required for the room session functionality to work). Under PECR, strictly necessary cookies do not require consent, but they must be disclosed in the cookie policy.

**Required actions:**

1. **Cookie audit** — Enumerate all cookies set by sgraph.ai, send.sgraph.ai, and any embedded third parties (Stripe). Classify each as: strictly necessary, functional, analytics, or marketing.
2. **Cookie policy page** — Document all cookies with: name, purpose, type (1st/3rd party), expiry, whether consent is required.
3. **Consent mechanism (if needed)** — If any non-strictly-necessary cookies are set by sgraph.ai or send.sgraph.ai directly, a consent banner is required. If only strictly necessary cookies are set by the company's own domains, a notice (not a banner with accept/reject) is sufficient.

#### Rationale

The ICO has enforcement powers under PECR and regularly issues fines for cookie violations. For a company explicitly positioning itself as privacy-first (and publishing educational content about whatismyipaddress.com's tracking violations), cookie non-compliance would be particularly damaging reputationally.

#### Dependencies

- B-GRC-01 (Privacy Policy must link to Cookie Policy)
- DevOps confirmation of all cookies set server-side (room_session, any others)
- Stripe integration implementation (to determine what Stripe sets on the redirect flow)

#### Effort

| Task | Effort |
|------|--------|
| Cookie audit (both domains) | 2 hours (agent + DevOps input) |
| Cookie policy page (HTML) | 1 hour (agent drafting + Dev implementation) |
| Consent mechanism assessment | 1 hour (agent) |

**Total (agent):** ~4 hours

### P-GRC-02: GDPR / UK GDPR Lawful Basis for whatismyipaddress.com Research and Educational Content

**ID:** P-GRC-02
**Title:** Legal Risk Assessment — Publishing Research on Competitor Tracking Practices
**Classification:** P* (Product — Legal/Reputational)
**Priority:** HIGH (P1)

#### Description

The privacy-first IP tool brief (v0.7.6 batch 2) contains a detailed forensic analysis of whatismyipaddress.com's post-rejection tracking behaviour, including: specific third-party company names, specific cookie names and values, specific storage keys, and characterises the behaviour as occurring "after clicking Disagree" on the consent banner.

The Librarian's batch-2 review assigned GRC the following research tasks:
- Research whatismyipaddress.com ownership and GDPR compliance
- Map legality of tracking-after-reject under GDPR Art. 6, Art. 7, and the ePrivacy Directive

This workstream addresses both the **research output** (GDPR mapping) and the **publication risk** (legal exposure from publishing the analysis).

**Part 1: GDPR Art. 6 / Art. 7 Analysis — Tracking After Consent Rejection**

The brief's factual claims, if correct, describe a pattern that likely violates:

- **GDPR Art. 7(3):** Withdrawal of consent must be as easy as giving it. If clicking "Disagree" on a consent banner still results in tracking cookies being set, this directly violates the ability to withdraw consent.
- **ePrivacy Directive Art. 5(3) (implemented as PECR in UK):** Requires consent before storing information on a user's device. Setting 25+ localStorage keys and 20+ cookies after the user has clicked "Disagree" appears to violate this directly.
- **GDPR Art. 6(1):** No consent = no lawful basis for tracking (unless legitimate interest applies, but behavioural advertising cannot rely on legitimate interest under EDPB guidance).

The analysis would form the factual basis for any regulatory complaint and would also be credible as educational content if the analysis is peer-reviewed and methodologically sound (AppSec has been asked to independently verify).

**Part 2: Publication Risk Assessment**

Publishing named analysis of a specific company's alleged GDPR violations carries legal risks for The Cyber Boardroom Ltd:

1. **Defamation risk (UK Defamation Act 2013):** If the factual claims in the analysis are incorrect, the company that operates whatismyipaddress.com could bring a defamation claim. Defence: truth (justification). The analysis must be factually accurate and reproducible.
2. **Mitigation:** The brief describes browser-observable behaviour (localStorage, cookies, network requests). This is not speculation — it is observable by any user with developer tools open. Peer review by AppSec is the primary mitigation.
3. **Jurisdiction:** whatismyipaddress.com's operator is US-based (to be confirmed). UK defamation law has a "serious harm" threshold for companies. The publication would be in the UK (sgraph.ai is UK-operated). Cross-border defamation is complex.
4. **Safe harbour:** Publishing factual, methodologically reproducible findings is a well-established practice in the security/privacy research community. The analysis is observational (not hacking). However, naming the company in a "Compare" section on the sgraph.ai website takes this beyond research into marketing copy territory, which has a higher defamation risk.

**GRC Recommendation (preliminary):**

- The GDPR analysis (Art. 6, Art. 7 mapping) is appropriate and valuable.
- The "Compare" section that directly names whatismyipaddress.com on the sgraph.ai website should be reviewed by legal counsel before publication.
- The educational framing ("here is the legal framework for tracking-after-reject") is lower risk than the competitive framing ("whatismyipaddress.com breaks the law").
- AppSec peer review is a prerequisite — unverified claims are the primary defamation risk vector.

#### Rationale

GRC was explicitly assigned this research in the Librarian's batch-2 brief processing. Additionally, the publication of named competitor research without legal review is a material business risk for a company in its launch phase.

#### Dependencies

- AppSec peer review of the whatismyipaddress.com analysis (prerequisite for publication)
- Human stakeholder decision on publication framing (educational vs competitive)
- Legal counsel review of "Compare" section before live deployment

#### Effort

| Task | Effort |
|------|--------|
| GDPR Art. 6/7 mapping document | 3 hours (agent) |
| whatismyipaddress.com ownership research | 1 hour (agent) |
| Publication risk assessment | 2 hours (agent) |

### P-GRC-03: EU Data Sovereignty — Multi-Cloud Deployment Legal Assessment

**ID:** P-GRC-03
**Title:** EU Data Sovereignty Requirements for Multi-Cloud and Multi-Region Deployment
**Classification:** P* (Product — Regulatory/Infrastructure)
**Priority:** MEDIUM (P2)

#### Description

The multi-cloud brief (v0.7.6 batch 1) proposes deployment to: AWS eu-west-1 + us-east-1, GCP Cloud Storage + CDN, Azure Blob + Azure CDN, Cloudflare R2/Pages, and an "EU-sovereign provider" (`eu.sgraph.ai`). None of these exist yet. However, architecture decisions being made in the Explorer phase will determine regulatory obligations when they are deployed.

**Key legal frameworks:**

1. **GDPR Chapter V (International Transfers):** Transferring personal data to countries outside the UK/EEA requires a legal mechanism: Adequacy Decision, Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs), or Binding Corporate Rules. The US has the UK Extension to the EU-US Data Privacy Framework (as of 2025), meaning US-based providers with certification can receive UK personal data. AWS and Google Cloud are certified.

2. **Zero-knowledge architecture mitigates but does not eliminate transfer risk:** The server stores only encrypted ciphertext. The key claim is that the ciphertext is not "personal data" under GDPR Art. 4(1) because it cannot identify a natural person without the decryption key (which the server never holds). If this claim holds, GDPR Chapter V transfers do not apply to the ciphertext. However, **access logs, IP hashes, token usage records, and audit trails** may constitute personal data and would be subject to Chapter V if stored in the US.

3. **EU data sovereignty providers:** The brief mentions Hetzner (Germany), OVHcloud (France), and Scaleway (France) as potential EU-sovereign providers. These would be EEA-based with no Chapter V transfer requirement. However, they would require a DPA under Art. 28.

4. **DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act):** DORA entered into force for financial entities in the EU on 17 January 2025. SGraph Send is not a financial entity in the primary sense. However, if The Cyber Boardroom Ltd provides ICT services to financial entities (e.g., a bank using SG/Send for secure document transfer), DORA may classify The Cyber Boardroom Ltd as a **Critical ICT Third-Party Service Provider**. This is a watch item, not an immediate obligation, but should be assessed before pursuing financial sector customers.

5. **UK GDPR vs EU GDPR:** Post-Brexit, the UK has its own data protection regime (UK GDPR + Data Protection Act 2018). The UK has adequacy decisions for the EEA. SGraph Send, as a UK company, operates under UK GDPR. If it serves EU customers, it may also need to appoint an **EU representative under GDPR Art. 27** if it processes EU residents' personal data systematically.

**GRC assessment required:**

- Does the zero-knowledge architecture claim successfully exclude ciphertext from GDPR personal data classification? (Legal opinion recommended)
- Which operational data (logs, hashes, tokens) is personal data and where is it stored?
- For each proposed cloud provider, is a UK/EU adequacy mechanism in place?
- Is an EU representative under GDPR Art. 27 required?
- DORA relevance assessment (watch item for now)

#### Rationale

Deploying to a new cloud region or provider without completing this assessment could result in unlawful international transfers. The EU representative requirement (Art. 27) is frequently overlooked by UK companies with EU customers and is directly enforceable by EU supervisory authorities.

#### Dependencies

- Multi-cloud deployment decisions (which providers, which regions) — currently PROPOSED
- Operational data storage location decisions (where are logs, audit trails, token records stored?)
- Human stakeholder decision on whether to pursue EU-sovereign deployment

#### Effort

| Task | Effort |
|------|--------|
| GDPR transfer mechanism assessment per provider | 3 hours (agent) |
| Zero-knowledge ciphertext personal data analysis | 2 hours (agent) + legal opinion |

### D-GRC-01: CloudFront Log Data Classification and Retention Policy

**ID:** D-GRC-01
**Title:** CloudFront Log Data Classification, Retention Schedule, and Access Controls
**Classification:** D* (Delivery — Data Governance)
**Priority:** HIGH (P1)

#### Description

The external security review (28 Feb 2026, ChatGPT-assisted) concluded that CloudFront logs from send.sgraph.ai are "low-risk operational metadata." The reality document confirmed this finding and noted that client IP addresses are deliberately excluded from CloudFront logging. However, a formal GRC-endorsed data classification document does not exist.

This workstream formalises what the security review validated:

**Data present in CloudFront logs (from reality document Section 12):**

| Data Element | Classification | Personal Data? | Rationale |
|--------------|---------------|----------------|-----------|
| Timestamps | Public operational | No | Not linked to individual |
| HTTP methods (GET, POST) | Public operational | No | Not linked to individual |
| HTTP status codes | Public operational | No | Not linked to individual |
| Hostnames | Public operational | No | Public domain names |
| Endpoint paths (including transfer IDs) | Operational — sensitive | No | Transfer IDs are opaque without decryption key |
| User agent strings | Operational — potentially identifiable | Borderline | UA string alone is rarely uniquely identifying; combined with other factors could contribute to fingerprinting |
| TLS version and cipher | Operational | No | Technical metadata |
| Response sizes | Operational | No | Ciphertext length does not reveal content |
| CloudFront edge locations | Operational | No | Coarse geographic indicator |
| Country codes | Operational | Borderline | Country code + timestamp + UA could narrow to individual in small jurisdictions |

**Conclusion (preliminary):** CloudFront logs, as currently configured, do not contain personal data as defined by GDPR Art. 4(1) because the deliberate exclusion of client IP addresses removes the primary re-identification vector. The combination of user agent + country code + timestamp is theoretically a fingerprinting risk but is assessed as low given the coarseness of country-level data and the absence of persistent identifiers.

**Retention policy gap:** No documented retention period exists for CloudFront logs stored in S3 (`s3://{account}--sgraph-send-cf-logs--eu-west-2/cloudfront-realtime/`). GDPR's storage limitation principle (Art. 5(1)(e)) requires data be kept no longer than necessary. For operational logs that are not personal data, a retention period is still best practice. Recommended: 90 days for real-time operational purposes, 1 year for security incident investigation purposes.

**Access controls gap:** Who has access to the CloudFront log S3 bucket? The reality document does not document this. GRC should request an access control review from DevOps.

#### Rationale

The security review validates the current configuration is safe. This workstream translates that validation into a governance document that: (a) provides a defensible basis if a regulator asks about log data, (b) establishes a formal retention period to comply with Art. 5(1)(e), and (c) documents the personal data determination so it does not need to be re-derived each time the question arises.

#### Dependencies

- DevOps confirmation of actual CloudFront logging configuration (to verify client IP exclusion)
- DevOps access control review for CloudFront log S3 bucket
- AppSec independent validation of the external security review findings

#### Effort

| Task | Effort |
|------|--------|
| Data classification document | 2 hours (agent) |
| Retention policy document | 1 hour (agent) |
| Access control requirements for DevOps | 1 hour (agent) |

**Total (agent):** ~4 hours

### D-GRC-02: UK GDPR / Data Protection Act 2018 Compliance Baseline

**ID:** D-GRC-02
**Title:** UK GDPR and DPA 2018 Compliance Baseline Assessment
**Classification:** D* (Delivery — Regulatory)
**Priority:** HIGH (P1)

#### Description

The Cyber Boardroom Ltd is a UK company. Its primary regulatory obligation is the UK GDPR (as retained in UK law by the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018 and amended by the Data Protection, Privacy and Electronic Communications (Amendments etc.) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019) and the Data Protection Act 2018 (DPA 2018).

No compliance baseline document exists for SGraph Send. This workstream produces a **compliance checklist** covering the key UK GDPR obligations relevant to the current and near-term state of the product:

| Obligation | UK GDPR Reference | Current Status | Gap |
|------------|------------------|----------------|-----|
| Lawful basis established for all processing | Art. 6 | Not documented | Gap — B-GRC-02 addresses billing; full processing inventory needed |
| Privacy notice provided to data subjects | Arts. 13, 14 | Does not exist | Gap — B-GRC-01 addresses this |
| DPAs in place with all processors | Art. 28 | Not documented | Gap — B-GRC-03 addresses this |
| Data retention periods defined | Art. 5(1)(e) | Not documented | Gap — D-GRC-01 addresses logs; broader policy needed |
| Data subject rights procedure | Arts. 15–22 | Does not exist | Gap — B-GRC-02 partially addresses |
| Records of processing activities (ROPA) | Art. 30 | Does not exist | Gap — this workstream |
| DPIA for high-risk processing | Art. 35 | Not done | Assess whether any current processing is "high-risk" |
| Data breach notification procedure | Arts. 33, 34 | Does not exist | Gap — CISO/AppSec deliverable, GRC oversight |
| ICO registration (if required) | DPA 2018 s.62 | Unknown status | Confirm with human stakeholder |
| PECR compliance (cookies) | PECR 2003 | Gap identified | P-GRC-01 addresses this |

**Key items in this workstream:**

1. **Records of Processing Activities (ROPA):** Art. 30 requires controllers with more than 250 employees, or who process data that could result in a risk to individuals, to maintain written records of all processing activities. The Cyber Boardroom Ltd, as a small company, may fall under the 250-employee exemption. However, if processing of personal data is not occasional, the exemption does not apply. Payment data processing is not occasional. A ROPA should be created.

2. **DPIA assessment:** Art. 35 requires a DPIA before processing that is "likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons." The criteria include: systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas (the IP tool proposal could qualify), processing of sensitive categories of data (not applicable), and innovative use of technology. The zero-knowledge encryption approach is innovative. A DPIA screening assessment is warranted.

3. **ICO registration:** Under DPA 2018, most organisations that process personal data must pay the data protection fee to the ICO (the "ICO registration" colloquially). The Cyber Boardroom Ltd should confirm it is registered with the ICO. This is a direct legal obligation with a GBP 400–2,900 annual fee depending on organisation size, and a criminal offence if not complied with.

#### Rationale

Operating a commercial product that processes personal data (email, payment data, access logs) without a compliance baseline is a regulatory risk. This workstream is the foundation for all other GRC workstreams — it scopes the full compliance obligation and identifies gaps.

#### Dependencies

- Human stakeholder confirmation of ICO registration status
- All other B-GRC and P-GRC workstreams feed into this baseline
- CISO/AppSec for data breach notification procedure

#### Effort

| Task | Effort |
|------|--------|
| UK GDPR compliance checklist | 2 hours (agent) |
| Records of Processing Activities (ROPA) — initial | 3 hours (agent) |
| DPIA screening assessment | 2 hours (agent) |
| ICO registration check and briefing | 30 minutes (agent) |

**Total (agent):** ~8 hours

### D-GRC-03: DORA Relevance Assessment

**ID:** D-GRC-03
**Title:** Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA) — Relevance Assessment for SGraph Send
**Classification:** D* (Delivery — Regulatory)
**Priority:** LOW (P3 — Watch Item)

#### Description

DORA (Regulation (EU) 2022/2554) has applied to EU financial entities and their ICT third-party service providers since 17 January 2025. It imposes requirements for: ICT risk management, incident reporting, operational resilience testing, and oversight of critical third-party providers.

SGraph Send is not a financial entity. However, the following scenarios could bring DORA within scope:

1. **SGraph Send as ICT service provider to financial entities:** If a bank, insurance company, payment institution, or other EU financial entity uses SG/Send for secure document transfer, SGraph Send becomes an ICT third-party service provider to that entity. The financial entity would need to include SGraph Send in its ICT third-party risk management programme.

2. **Critical ICT Third-Party Service Provider (CTPP) designation:** The European Supervisory Authorities (EBA, EIOPA, ESMA) can designate ICT providers as critical. At the current scale, SGraph Send is extremely unlikely to be designated. But if the self-hosted and white-label programmes grow and SG/Send is widely deployed in the financial sector, this becomes a long-range consideration.

3. **UK position:** DORA is EU legislation. The UK has not adopted DORA (post-Brexit divergence). The Bank of England's Operational Resilience Policy (2022) covers UK financial entities, but its third-party requirements differ from DORA. If SGraph Send operates in the UK financial sector, UK rules apply; if it serves EU financial entities, DORA applies to those entities' requirements for their third-party providers.

**GRC recommendation:** DORA is currently a **watch item**, not an immediate compliance obligation. However:
- The pricing matrix brief explicitly includes a "Dedicated Single-Tenant" option (described as suitable for compliance-sensitive environments). Financial sector customers are an obvious target for this tier.
- Before actively marketing to financial sector customers, a DORA readiness assessment should be completed.
- The BCP test plan mentioned in the multi-cloud brief (PROPOSED) would be relevant to DORA's operational resilience testing requirements.

This workstream produces a one-page DORA relevance assessment and a trigger list: "When any of these conditions are met, DORA compliance work must begin."

#### Rationale

Regulatory readiness is faster to achieve if the baseline is documented before the need arises. A one-page assessment now prevents a rushed compliance programme when the first financial sector customer asks "are you DORA compliant?"

#### Dependencies

- Marketing/Go-to-market decision on whether financial sector is a target vertical
- No immediate engineering dependencies

#### Effort

| Task | Effort |
|------|--------|
| DORA relevance assessment document | 2 hours (agent) |
| DORA trigger list | 30 minutes (agent) |

**Total (agent):** ~2.5 hours
