## Brief #24: Signed Creative Commons — Legal Enforcement

**Priority:** STRATEGIC | **GRC role:** Lead

### Legal Framework Analysis

The CC-Signed proposal (CC BY-S and variants) extends existing Creative Commons licences with cryptographic signature preservation requirements. This is the single most consequential GRC brief in the batch.

**Strengths:**
- Builds on decades of CC case law (enforceable globally)
- Digital signatures have legal standing in EU (eIDAS), US (ESIGN Act, UETA), and UK
- Maps cleanly to existing licence violation enforcement mechanisms

**Risks identified:**
- No precedent for cryptographic signature preservation as a licence condition
- CC Foundation engagement required before any public claim of "CC-Signed" branding
- Cross-jurisdictional enforcement varies: eIDAS defines "advanced electronic signatures" with specific requirements that PKI signatures may or may not satisfy
- LLM training data obligations under CC BY-S are legally untested territory

### Research Assignments Accepted

| # | Task | Status |
|---|------|--------|
| 1 | Can cryptographic signatures qualify as attribution under existing CC? | Accepted — requires legal opinion |
| 3 | Enforceability of "preserve signature" as licence condition (EU, US, UK) | Accepted — jurisdictional analysis |
| 4 | Draft CC BY-S licence text | Accepted — depends on #1 and #3 |
| 5 | Draft MIT-S / Apache-S signed variants | Accepted — after CC BY-S draft |
| 6 | eIDAS analysis: does PKI signature meet "advanced electronic signature"? | Accepted — joint with Historian |
| 9 | LLM training data obligations under CC BY-S | Accepted — joint with Architect |

### Regulatory Risk Assessment

- **eIDAS compliance:** The EU requires specific technical properties for advanced electronic signatures (unique to signatory, capable of identifying signatory, under sole control, linked to data). Our PKI model must be validated against each requirement.
- **DMCA / Copyright Act implications:** Removing a cryptographic signature could constitute circumvention under DMCA section 1202 (integrity of copyright management information). This is a potential enforcement avenue beyond licence terms.

## Brief #2: Trust Web — Privacy Implications

**Priority:** CRITICAL | **GRC role:** Supporting

### Privacy Trade-Off Documentation

The Trust Web requires the server to know communication patterns (which public keys communicate with which public keys). This is metadata exposure that must be documented.

**Assessment:**
- The trade-off is analogous to email server metadata (who emails whom, not what they say)
- Under GDPR, IP hash + public key hash + transaction timestamps constitutes personal data if the individual is identifiable
- The daily-salt SHA-256 IP hashing (existing design) provides pseudonymisation, not anonymisation
- Public key hashes associated with trust scores are linkable across transactions

**GRC requirements:**
1. GDPR Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) required before Trust Web deployment
2. Privacy notice must disclose metadata collection for trust scoring
3. Right to erasure (Art. 17) conflicts with trust score persistence — legal basis analysis needed
4. Lawful basis: legitimate interest (Art. 6(1)(f)) for fraud prevention is the strongest candidate

### Sybil Resistance and Discrimination Risk

Trust levels that gate platform capabilities (5 files/day for anonymous vs generous limits for high trust) must be assessed for:
- Discrimination against new users in regulated contexts
- Accessibility obligations if the platform is used for legally required document exchange
- Proportionality of restrictions under competition law

## Brief #8: Network Threat Detection — Policy Implications

**Priority:** HIGH | **GRC role:** Supporting

### Compliance Requirements

The "cascade destruction" and "silent detection" approaches raise governance questions:

**Silent surveillance (intelligence option):**
- Monitoring accounts without notification has GDPR implications (data subject rights)
- The "observe without tipping off" model must be balanced against transparency obligations
- Lawful basis: fraud prevention legitimate interest applies, but proportionality must be demonstrated
- Retention periods for surveillance data must be defined and limited

**Cascade destruction:**
- Destroying associated accounts based on network proximity is a collective sanction
- Appeals process required — affected users must have a mechanism to contest trust reduction
- Documentation requirements: each cascade decision must be logged with justification

**Law enforcement cooperation:**
- Pattern extraction and network mapping may constitute evidence relevant to criminal investigations
- Data retention and disclosure obligations vary by jurisdiction
- A law enforcement request protocol must be defined before this capability goes live

## Brief #13: Fractal Document Signing — Contract and EULA Implications

**Priority:** STRATEGIC | **GRC role:** Supporting

### Regulatory Landscape

**Electronic signatures on contracts:**
- Per-clause signing has legal standing under eIDAS (qualified electronic signatures on individual clauses are valid)
- US: ESIGN Act and UETA do not require whole-document signatures — per-section is acceptable
- The .sgdoc format must meet the requirements for "reliable" electronic signatures in each jurisdiction

**EULA third-party review model:**
- Privacy organisations signing EULA clauses creates a new liability model: if a reviewer approves a clause that later proves harmful, what liability attaches?
- Consumer protection law implications: does a "reviewed by Privacy Foundation" badge create reliance that could be actionable?
- The micropayment model for clause review must comply with financial services regulation in applicable jurisdictions

**GRC recommendation:** Engage legal counsel on liability framework for third-party EULA reviewers before the feature ships.

## Brief #22: Agent Isolation — Security Claims Validation

**Priority:** CRITICAL | **GRC role:** Supporting

### Compliance Assessment

The architecture correction (encryption alone is not isolation) has direct governance implications:

**Security claims in marketing and investor materials:**
- Any previous claims about "cryptographic isolation" in shared-process agents must be corrected
- Marketing materials must accurately represent the isolation model (container/VM, not encryption-only)
- SOC 2 or ISO 27001 audit scope would include container isolation controls

**Data processing agreements:**
- If agents process personal data, the isolation model determines whether separate agents are separate data processors
- Container isolation with ephemeral environments supports data minimisation (GDPR Art. 5(1)(c))
- Private key injection via secret management must be documented in the processing record

**GRC validation accepted:** Will review the container isolation architecture against SOC 2 CC6.1 (logical access) and CC6.6 (system boundary) trust services criteria.
